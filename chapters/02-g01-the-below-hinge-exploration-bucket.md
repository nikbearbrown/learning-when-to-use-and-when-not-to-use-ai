# Chapter 2 — G01: The Below-Hinge Exploration Bucket

## The Decision on the Table

Priya Raman, assistant superintendent for teaching and learning, is building next year's strategic plan, and a board member has done some reading. The email arrives with a spreadsheet attached — Hattie's 252 influences, sorted descending — and a proposal highlighted in yellow: *"Everything below 0.40 comes out of the plan. Music, drama, the bilingual expansion, the classroom-management strand in new-teacher mentoring — all below the line. Redirect the funds to AI tutoring."*

The same week, from the opposite direction, the district's AI committee forwards a vendor menu for her review: an AI presentation generator ("saves teachers four hours a week"), a gamified AI math app, an AI homework helper ("instant support, every student, every night"), and a phone-based microlearning platform. Three of the four pitches cite Hattie. All four of the influences they map to sit below 0.40 — not that any pitch mentions it.

One board member wants to use the below-hinge zone as a demolition list. Four vendors are hoping nobody checks whether their products live in it. Both errors come from the same source: treating 130 influences with d < 0.40 as a single undifferentiated category called "doesn't work." This chapter is about what is actually down there — and it is the immediate stress test of Chapter 1's central rule. If an effect size is a prioritization signal and never a verdict, the below-hinge bucket is where that discipline either holds or collapses.

## What This Group Is

G01 collects every influence on the 2018 list with an effect size below 0.40: **130 of the 252**, ranks 123 through 252, ranging from d = 0.39 down to d = −0.90, weighted average 0.15. It is the largest group in the book by a factor of six, and the only one defined purely by band rather than by classification signature — which is precisely why it must not be read as one thing.

The first act of honesty is to refuse the flat list. The bucket contains three bands, and they are different in kind, not just degree:

**Below hinge (81 influences, d = 0.15 to 0.39).** Real influences with real but modest average effects: social skills programs (0.39), drama and arts (0.38), music (0.37), worked examples (0.37), mobile phones (0.37), bilingual programs (0.36), classroom management (0.35), homework (0.29), class size reduction (0.21). Almost everything interesting in this chapter lives here, because almost everything here has a story the average conceals.

**Negligible (32 influences, d = 0.01 to 0.14).** Effects statistically distinguishable from nothing but practically close to it as measured: school choice programs (0.12), mentoring as aggregated (0.12), teacher subject-matter knowledge as measured (0.11), background music (0.10), charter schools (0.09), single-sex schools (0.08), teacher performance pay (0.05), humor (0.04). Some of these are measurement casualties; several are genuinely deflating findings about expensive structural reforms.

**Harmful (17 influences, d = −0.01 to −0.90).** And here, stop — because this band is where the flat-list misread does its worst damage. Boredom (−0.49), depression (−0.36), retention in grade (−0.32), moving between schools (−0.34), students feeling disliked (−0.19), suspension and expulsion (−0.20), television (−0.18), deafness (−0.61), ADHD (−0.90). **These are not interventions that hurt. With few exceptions, they are not interventions at all.** ADHD at −0.90 does not mean some program called ADHD damages children; it means the condition is associated with lower measured achievement — it describes a population that needs support, not a treatment to avoid. The same is true of deafness, depression, and family welfare status. The actionable exceptions are the genuine policy choices hiding among the conditions: retention and suspension are decisions institutions make, and the negative signs on those rows are worth taking seriously as warnings. One paragraph of band hygiene — *condition or choice?* — prevents the single most embarrassing misread the Hattie list makes possible.

So: a drawer of specialized tools, a shelf of weak-as-measured averages, and a list of headwinds mislabeled as failures. Not a graveyard. The job is sorting, and sorting requires knowing *why* a number landed below the line.

## Four Reasons a Number Lands Below 0.40

Every d below the hinge got there by one of four routes, and the four demand opposite responses. This taxonomy is the chapter's working tool — and, reused on every vendor pitch you ever receive, the book's first transferable skill.

### Reason 1: Wrong ruler

Recall Kraft's benchmarks from Chapter 1: in rigorous field trials on standardized outcomes, d = 0.20 is *large*. The hinge was computed over a synthesis thick with small, brief, proximal-measure studies; honest field results look weak standing next to inflated lab results. Class size reduction (d = 0.21) is the classic case — its evidence base includes some of the largest true randomized experiments in education history, which is exactly why its number is modest. A 0.21 from that kind of evidence may be worth more than a 0.50 from ten one-week lab studies.

The wrong-ruler reason is also where cost belongs, because the hinge is silent about it. Run the arithmetic on two below-hinge options: class size reduction at roughly $3,000 per student per year for d ≈ 0.21, versus an AI tutoring deployment at roughly $50 per student for d ≈ 0.17 (illustrative figures; the point is the structure, not the decimals). Per 0.10 standard deviations of effect, the two differ in cost by more than an order of magnitude. The hinge sees two sub-0.40 failures; a budget sees two utterly different propositions. Effect size answers "how much, on average, ignoring price." Adoption decisions are made of everything the hinge ignores: cost, scalability, equity, and who specifically benefits.

### Reason 2: Averaged-away moderators

Some influences work powerfully for specific learners under specific conditions, reverse outside them — and the average smears both into mediocrity. The bucket's resident example is its most instructive row: **worked examples, d = 0.37.**

Cognitive load theory delivers one of the most replicated findings in instructional psychology: novices learn problem-solving better from *studying worked solutions* than from unsupported problem-solving (Sweller and Cooper, 1985; Atkinson, Derry, Renkl and Wortham, 2000). Sweller has called the worked-example effect the best known and most widely studied of all cognitive load effects. Then the **expertise reversal effect** (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler and Sweller, 2003) flips the sign: for advanced learners, worked examples become redundant or actively harmful, and problem-solving wins. "What is the effect of worked examples?" has no single answer — *the moderator is the finding*. Works strongly for novices; reverses for experts; the practice that honors both is fading: worked example → completion problem → full problem, retired as expertise grows.

The public number even has a biography that dramatizes the averaging. Visible Learning 2009 reported worked examples at d = 0.57 — comfortably above the hinge — based on one meta-analysis. The 2018 list shows 0.37, after a later meta-analysis was averaged in: Wittwer and Renkl's (2010) synthesis of *instructional explanations within example-based learning* — a related but different construct — which came in around 0.16 [verify: the reconciliation comes from a critique site's reading of the editions; direction and components are corroborated, but confirm the arithmetic against the MetaX database before quoting the mechanics]. The underlying cognitive-load literature did not change at all. The number moved twenty points because the *averaging pool* changed. Any reader who internalizes this one biography is permanently inoculated against treating a Hattie decimal as a stable property of the world.

### Reason 3: Not an intervention at all

Dozens of bucket rows are correlations, conditions, and structural variables wearing the same costume as interventions. "Relations of high school achievement to career performance" (d = 0.38) is a correlation between two outcomes — there is nothing to implement. Relative age, family structure, parental employment, gender: context, not levers. The entire Harmful band minus its two policy exceptions, as above. The dataset marks row after row "not a manipulable intervention." For these, the question "should we adopt it?" is grammatically broken — and any pitch or plan built on such a row has failed before evidence even enters the room.

### Reason 4: Genuinely weak as deployed

And some entries earn their number honestly. Web-based learning: 0.18. One-to-one laptop programs: 0.16. Distance education: 0.13. Use of PowerPoint: 0.26. These are aggregates of technology as actually implemented at scale, across decades — and the dataset's recurring annotation for them is the chapter's refrain: *the effect size below the hinge is the honest number vendors should be required to cite.* Not zero — modest, real, and a fair warning about what technology-as-delivery-mechanism historically returns. These rows are the book's anti-hype receipts, and they will be spent in earnest in the ICT and writing-technology chapters.

Four reasons, four responses: re-judge on the right ruler; chase the moderator; reclassify as context; believe the number. The unforgivable move is applying any response before asking which reason holds.

## Where Vendor Pitches Go to Hide

Now the AI angle, and it is sharper for this group than for any other: **the below-hinge bucket is where vendor pitches go to hide.** The camouflage works because the influences *sound* good — homework, gamification, presentations, mobile learning are all familiar, plausible, classroom-shaped words — and because almost nobody checks the rank of the influence behind the product. A pitch that says "our tool supports homework completion" is wrapping itself in an influence at d = 0.29. The word sounds educational; the number is sub-hinge; the pitch is counting on you hearing the word and never seeing the number.

The mirror error matters just as much: **AI novelty does not lift the host influence's ceiling.** An AI-generated slide deck inherits the evidence base of slide decks (0.26). An AI gamified app inherits gamification's (0.35). A phone-delivered tutor inherits the mobile-phone context (0.37 — and see below for why even that number flatters). The mechanism did not change; the ceiling did not either. The only honest exception: if the tool genuinely changes *who does the cognitive work or what feedback loop exists*, then it is a different mechanism — and it should be classified and evidenced as one, on its own results, not on the host influence's borrowed clothes.

Two bucket rows deserve named warnings because the products aimed at them are already in your inbox:

**Homework (d = 0.29) — the danger zone.** The bucket's only high-vendor-activity row, classified AI-RESISTANT with conflict present. Homework's modest average conceals its mechanism: independent practice and consolidation — the unassisted engine-run. An AI "homework helper" that supplies answers does not augment that mechanism; it deletes it, replacing consolidation with transcription. This is the Bastani pattern from Chapter 1 sold as a subscription: better-looking homework, less learning inside it. A below-hinge influence with high vendor activity and a conflict flag is the exact coordinates of maximum skepticism. The dataset's hypothetical column even posits a negative AI effect here (d ≈ −0.10 [HYPOTHESIS] — the author's conjecture, never a finding).

**Mobile phones (d = 0.37) — the row that teaches you to read the notes.** A naive reading: phones nearly clear the hinge; a phone-based learning platform is nearly above-average. The row's notes say the opposite: physical phone presence carries distraction and multitasking costs, and the hypothetical column posits d ≈ −0.30 [HYPOTHESIS] for AI learning apps on personal phones. The external evidence is genuinely mixed and modest, but its direction is consistent: Beland and Murphy's UK study of school phone bans found post-ban exam gains of roughly 0.07 standard deviations overall, concentrated in low achievers at around 0.14, with high achievers essentially unaffected [verify exact coefficients against the published Labour Economics version before quoting decimals]; a 2024 rapid review puts ban effects near d = 0.16, stronger for wellbeing than achievement; recent scoping reviews counsel against one-size-fits-all conclusions [contested: magnitudes and the right policy grain are actively debated]. A mid-pack d sitting on top of a sign-flipping, population-dependent literature: the number alone would have told you precisely nothing you needed.

## Case Study: The Strategic Plan Survives Both Errors

Priya Raman's response handled the demolition proposal and the vendor menu with the same instrument.

**The board member's cut list first.** She returned the spreadsheet with one column added: *which of the four reasons puts this row below 0.40?* Music and drama: the achievement effect size was never the right measure — Sala and Gobet's (2017) review finds far transfer from music instruction to academic outcomes small to null in the best-controlled designs [contested: the near/far-transfer partition has named critics, including Bigand and Tillmann], so the honest defense of the arts is not test-score alchemy but the outcomes arts actually produce: musicianship, ensemble experience, performance before a human audience, belonging. Both rows are classified AI-ADJACENT for a reason — embodied practice and human audience *are* the mechanism. Defending arts with achievement effect sizes loses on the evidence; cutting them with achievement effect sizes was never a valid move. Wrong measure, both directions — and note what just happened: the below-hinge analysis protected programs from a bad defense as much as from a bad attack.

The classroom-management strand was the cut she refused outright, and it is the bucket's failure case in miniature. The 2018 number is 0.35; Visible Learning 2009 had reported 0.52, and Marzano's synthesis found similar achievement effects alongside much larger effects on disruption [verify: the 0.52 → 0.35 shift mechanics, like worked examples', come from edition-comparison readings — confirm before quoting]. More fundamentally, classroom management is an *enabling condition*: it purchases the time-on-task and relational order inside which every above-hinge influence operates. Cutting it to fund AI tutoring would demolish the floor to buy furniture. The row is classified AI-RESISTANT, conflict present — behavioral authority, physical presence, and relational credibility are the mechanism, and the dataset's note is blunt: AI has no meaningful role. A below-hinge influence can be a load-bearing wall. Rank is not importance.

Bilingual programs (0.36) she flagged for separate, local analysis — a population-specific intervention whose aggregate number says little about its value for the district's actual multilingual learners.

**Then the vendor menu, sorted with the same column.** The presentation generator: host influence d = 0.26, reason four, honest number — *niche*; legitimate as a teacher time-saver, and the time savings count only if the recovered hours return to learning; no learning claims allowed in the contract. The gamified math app: gaming/simulations at 0.35 with a partial-conflict flag — *explore*; a small pilot with transfer conditions stated in advance. The homework helper: *decline* — mechanism conflict plus high vendor activity; the committee's one-line rationale read "this product automates the part that was the learning." The phone microlearning platform: *decline*, with the mobile-phones row and its notes attached in full as the explanation.

The final artifact was one page: each proposal tagged **explore / niche / outside the plan**, each tag carrying a one-sentence mechanism rationale and, where relevant, which of the four reasons applied. The board member who proposed the demolition got the same page. Nothing below the hinge entered the strategic center; nothing was executed for its rank.

The sort, note well, is not in the data. The same rows sort differently in a district whose pressing problem is early literacy than in one bleeding new teachers. The bucket is an exploration bucket precisely because the local problem statement — not the spreadsheet — supplies the sorting key.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** serve as the bucket's research assistant and honest broker: pull the evidence behind any below-hinge influence, surface the moderators an average conceals, generate the four-reasons analysis for a committee to interrogate, draft cost-per-effect comparisons, and — for the bucket's legitimately AI-native rows like worked examples — generate instructional material *for novices, faded on schedule*.

**The humans must** supply the local problem statement before any sorting begins; make the explore/niche/de-prioritize calls and own them; refuse both the demolition read (below hinge = cut) and the novelty promotion (it's AI now = re-rank); and keep every conflict-flagged mechanism — independent practice, classroom presence, embodied performance — entirely on the human side of the line.

**Evidence that the sorting worked is Z:** twelve months on, the strategic plan's center contains only influences chosen for mechanism fit to a named local problem; every explore-tier pilot carries a pre-registered, delayed, unassisted outcome measure; and no purchase in the plan rests on an influence whose row the committee cannot explain in one sentence, including which of the four reasons applies.

## The Evidence Check

For any proposal that maps to a below-hinge influence, demand:

1. **The reason, named.** Wrong ruler, averaged moderators, not-an-intervention, or honestly weak? A proposer who cannot answer has not read their own evidence base.
2. **The moderator statement.** For whom, under what conditions, at what phase of expertise? "Works for novices, reverses for experts" is a usable claim; a bare average is not.
3. **The cost arithmetic.** Effect per dollar per student, beside at least one alternative. The hinge ignores price; budgets cannot.
4. **The band check.** If anyone in the room is citing a Harmful-band number, verify it describes a choice and not a condition before any conclusion is drawn from it.
5. **The novelty audit.** If the pitch is an AI version of a bucket row: did the mechanism change, or just the interface? If the mechanism changed, where is the *new* evidence?

What does not count: the influence's familiar-sounding name, the excitement of the demo, teacher hours saved (until the destination of those hours is shown), and any effect size quoted without its band, its ruler, and its reason.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The three-way sort.** Take twelve rows from this chapter's bucket — mix interventions, conditions, correlations, and two Harmful-band entries — and tag each *explore / niche / de-prioritize* for your own institution, with a one-sentence mechanism rationale. Notice which tags you cannot assign without writing your local problem statement first. That noticing is the lesson.
2. **The moderator hunt.** For worked examples, complete this sentence from the cognitive-load literature: "Works for ___ under ___; reverses for ___ because ___." Then write the AI policy that sentence implies — including the fading schedule — in three lines.
3. **The reader audit (the book's standing exercise).** Choose one tool or routine in your setting that resembles this group — something familiar, modest, and possibly up for an AI upgrade. Write one sentence each: the mechanism, the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution, and the proof that learning happened.

## What Would Change My Mind

The claim beneath this chapter is that band membership carries almost no decision content until disaggregated by reason, moderator, and mechanism. Evidence against it would look like this: AI versions of below-hinge influences repeatedly and durably outperforming their host influence's ceiling on delayed, unassisted measures — gamified AI math apps producing transfer that gamification never did, AI homework helpers improving consolidation rather than destroying it. If that pattern emerged across honest trials, then AI would be changing mechanisms, not just interfaces, at scale — and the bucket would need re-sorting row by row, with several "declines" in this chapter reversed in public. The Bastani result and the bucket's own honest-number tech rows make this unlikely. They do not make it impossible.

## Still Puzzling

- **Interleaved practice at 0.21.** One of cognitive psychology's most celebrated lab effects sits in this bucket, twenty points under worked examples' worst press. Implementation gap, measurement timing (interleaving wins on delayed tests and loses on immediate ones), or another averaging casualty? The reconciliation would teach the field something important, and it has not been written.
- **Where is the fading line for AI worked examples?** "Novices only, faded over time" is the correct policy and an unmapped one. No one knows how to detect, at scale, the moment a learner crosses from benefiting to coasting — the expertise-reversal boundary is real in the literature and invisible in the tooling.
- **Do honest numbers ever update?** Web-based learning (0.18) and distance education (0.13) were measured on pre-LLM deployments. The skeptical prior is that interface novelty never moved these aggregates before. But "never before" is a prior, not a law — and the field is currently running the experiment, without a control group, in every district at once.

## Further Reading

- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The right ruler for the bucket's field-trial citizens; the corrective for every "below 0.40 means failure" reading this chapter dismantles.
- **Kalyuga, S., Ayres, P., Chandler, P., & Sweller, J. (2003). The expertise reversal effect. *Educational Psychologist*, 38(1), 23–31.** The moderator that explains the bucket's richest row — why worked examples have no single effect size and why AI tutors need a fading schedule.
- **Atkinson, R. K., Derry, S. J., Renkl, A., & Wortham, D. (2000). Learning from examples: Instructional principles from the worked examples research. *Review of Educational Research*, 70(2), 181–214.** The worked-examples evidence base in full — what the d = 0.37 is an average *of*.
- **Sala, G., & Gobet, F. (2017). Does far transfer exist? Negative evidence from chess, music, and working memory training. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 26(6), 515–520.** Why the arts should never be defended — or attacked — with achievement effect sizes. Contested at the edges; read with its critics.
- **Beland, L.-P., & Murphy, R. (2016). Ill communication: Technology, distraction and student performance. *Labour Economics*, 41, 61–76.** The phone-ban evidence behind the bucket's most misreadable row: modest average gains, concentrated among the students averages hide.
- **Slavin, R. (2018). John Hattie is wrong. *Robert Slavin's Blog*, June 21, 2018.** The inflation argument that explains why honest field numbers look weak beside the hinge — essential context for every "wrong ruler" call this chapter makes.
