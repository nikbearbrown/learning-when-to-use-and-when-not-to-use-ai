# Chapter 12 — G11: Cooperation Is the Intervention

## The Decision on the Table

Aisha Roundtree built the peer-tutoring center at Westbrook High from a storage room and six volunteers into the school's busiest academic space: forty trained student tutors, two hundred regulars, a waiting list on exam weeks. Which is why the line item in next year's draft budget landed on her desk like an accusation. The district has licensed an AI tutoring chatbot — strong math and writing coverage, available around the clock — and a budget analyst has drawn the obvious conclusion: the chatbot can absorb the tutoring center's evening hours, then perhaps its afternoons. The note attached is not hostile; it is arithmetic. "AI tutor: $11 per student per year. Peer program: stipends, coordinator time, training days. Recommend consolidation."

Aisha knows two things the spreadsheet does not. First, the research on intelligent tutoring is genuinely strong — she has read enough to know the chatbot will probably help the students who use it well. Second, half the learning in her center never touches the tutee. It happens to the *tutors* — the B-student who finally understood slope the third time she had to explain it to someone else, the senior whose attendance recovered because Tuesday afternoons, two sophomores were counting on him. The spreadsheet prices the explanation-delivery service. It cannot see the rest of the machine.

Her job for Thursday's budget meeting: explain, in evidence language, what exactly would be consolidated away.

## What This Group Is

Four influences: above the hinge, **AI-RESISTANT**, high human irreducibility, moderate substitution risk, **cognitive-work conflict present**. Effect sizes 0.40 to 0.53 on the 2018 252-influence list, weighted average 0.48.

This is the book's first full chapter on the AI-resistant side of the map, and the classification needs careful reading. AI-resistant does not mean AI-useless; it means the active ingredient is a human social mechanism — peer exchange, interdependence, group accountability — that AI cannot perform, only support from outside or imitate from inside. The conflict flag marks the imitation problem: a chatbot that plays the role of a peer is not a worse peer; it is a different intervention entirely, one that happens to share a transcript format with the real thing.

The moderate substitution risk is the spreadsheet's risk: not students sneaking answers, but institutions consolidating line items — replacing a social structure with a service because the two produce similar-looking sessions at very different prices. The substitution is rational, visible, and approved in meetings, which makes it more dangerous than the silent kind, not less.

## The Influences

**Peer tutoring (d = 0.53)** — rank 71 of 252. Structured programs in which students teach students. The finding the budget spreadsheet misses is the oldest one in this literature: tutoring benefits flow to *both* roles, and the tutor's gains — from organizing, explaining, fielding confusion, and being responsible for another person's understanding — are frequently comparable to the tutee's. The dataset's hypothesis is blunt: with the peer exchange replaced, almost nothing of the influence remains — d ≈ 0.10 [HYPOTHESIS], logistics-level support only.

**Cooperative vs. competitive learning (d = 0.53)** — rank 72 — and **cooperative vs. individualistic learning** (its sibling from Chapter 7's group, d = 0.55). Comparative influences: the d measures cooperation's edge *over competition and solo work*, grounded in social interdependence theory — Johnson and Johnson's long program of research on what happens when my success requires yours. Positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction: the named ingredients are all relational. Hypothesis d ≈ 0.10 [HYPOTHESIS].

**Small group learning (d = 0.47)** — rank 92. Effects depend on the group being a genuine unit — shared goals, mutual accountability — rather than four students seated together working alone. The dataset notes relational safety and peer accountability as the mechanism, and flags AI facilitation of the group's interaction as degradation, not support. Hypothesis d ≈ 0.10 [HYPOTHESIS].

**Cooperative learning (d = 0.40)** — rank 120. The generic row, sitting exactly on the hinge — a useful reminder that cooperation poorly implemented (no interdependence, no individual accountability) earns mediocre effects. The structure does the work, which cuts both ways: it is exactly the structure that an AI cannot inhabit and a school can fail to build.

One honesty note for the group: these are aggregations across enormously varied implementations, and the comparative rows inherit every critique of meta-meta-analysis this book has already flagged. Read them as a consistent directional signal — structured cooperation reliably beats unstructured individualism — not as a promise of 0.53 from any particular Tuesday.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is **consequential interdependence**: one person's cognitive act mattering to another person who is actually there.

Unpack the tutor's side first, because it is the side the spreadsheet cannot see. To teach slope to a confused sophomore, the tutor must retrieve the concept, reorganize it around someone else's misunderstanding, generate new examples on the spot, and monitor in real time whether the explanation is landing — retrieval, elaboration, and metacognitive monitoring, the highest-value operations in the learning-science inventory, performed under the most motivating condition psychology knows: another person depending on you. The explanation is a by-product. The tutor's reconstruction of their own knowledge is the event.

The tutee's side has a social engine too. Explanations from a near-peer arrive in reachable language, from someone who recently held the same confusion — and asking a peer is cheaper than asking the teacher, but not free. The mild social cost is load-bearing: it keeps help-seeking deliberate, and being helped by someone who chose to show up creates an obligation to try that no software license generates. Cooperative structures generalize the same force — in a well-built group, my preparation matters because my teammates will be examined on what only I was assigned to learn.

Now the necessary complication, because this book does not get to wave it away: on the *tutee's* side alone, machine tutoring is genuinely competitive. VanLehn's review put human tutoring at 0.79 and step-based intelligent tutoring systems at a statistically comparable 0.76 — the folklore gap between human and machine tutoring has not survived measurement. If peer tutoring were only an explanation-delivery service, the budget analyst would be right, and this chapter would be Chapter 10. The resistance classification rests on what else the structure produces: the tutor's learning, the obligation that gets a sophomore to show up Tuesday, the belonging that a waiting list signals. The chatbot replicates the transcript. It replicates nothing else in the machine.

## What AI Can Safely Do

- **Train the tutors.** Role-play a confused, frustrated, or overconfident tutee for practice; coach hint-giving instead of answer-giving; drill the opening moves of a session. Rehearsal against a simulator before the real human is the legitimate version of AI-as-peer.
- **Coach the tutors live and after.** The strongest evidence in this lane is Wang and colleagues' Tutor CoPilot trial: AI-generated, expert-modeled suggestions delivered *to human tutors* mid-session moved student mastery by 4 percentage points overall and 9 for students of the lowest-rated tutors. AI amplifying the human exchange, not replacing it — the design this whole chapter points at.
- **Run the logistics.** Matching, scheduling, reminders, room assignments, waitlist triage — the coordination overhead that kills volunteer programs.
- **Prepare the material.** Practice problems, worked examples, and session guides matched to what the tutee's teacher is actually teaching, so sessions start with substance instead of archaeology.
- **Mine the records.** Summarize session logs for the coordinator: which topics recur, which pairings work, which tutors need support, which tutees stop coming — pattern detection across a hundred sessions a week that no human coordinator can hold.
- **Backstop the off-hours.** A guardrailed practice tool at 11 p.m. when no humans are available — labeled as practice support, never counted as the program.

## The Substitution Trap

**The consolidation.** The headline trap is Aisha's budget line: replacing the social structure with the service because the sessions look alike. What is deleted is invisible on every metric the service reports — the forty tutors' own learning (the largest single casualty, and it appears on no dashboard because the tutors aren't the chatbot's users), the obligation network that produced attendance, the help-seeking habits formed when asking has a face. The chatbot's usage statistics will look excellent, because usage is what it does. The question the statistics cannot answer is what two hundred and forty students are no longer doing.

**The simulated group.** The subtler trap: AI *inside* the cooperative structure. A chatbot as "fourth group member," an AI that mediates the group's discussion, summarizes its disagreements, or smooths its conflicts. Each feature removes the friction — and the friction is the curriculum. A group whose disagreements are resolved by a machine is not practicing the resolution of disagreement; jigsaw-style designs where one "expert" is an AI dissolve the interdependence, because nobody's effort is actually required anymore. The conflict flag on this group means exactly this: the tool's helpfulness and the mechanism's operation are fighting over the same square of social space.

**Cooperation-flavored individualism.** The oldest failure mode, now AI-accelerated: each student consults the chatbot separately, then the group staples the outputs together. The room looks collaborative; the cognition was solitary and machine-assisted; the interdependence never fired. Bastani's caution applies with a social twist — the group's assisted output looks better than what they could produce together unaided, and the gap is the learning that didn't happen *between* them.

## Case Study

Aisha Roundtree walked into Thursday's budget meeting with one slide and a counter-proposal.

**The tool.** The district's AI tutoring chatbot — which she proposed to keep. **The influence.** Peer tutoring, d = 0.53, presented with the two-sided evidence read aloud: the effect belongs to a structure in which both roles learn, and the center's own records showed it — her tutors' GPA trajectories bent upward in the semesters they tutored, an effect the chatbot's $11 license would purchase for nobody.

**The useful moment.** Her counter-proposal put the chatbot to work in the three places the classification permits. Tutor training first: new tutors now rehearse against the model playing a frustrated freshman — wrong in realistic ways, impatient, occasionally hostile — before they ever face a real one, and the training days that used to consume her stipend budget run themselves. Coordination second: matching, scheduling, and session-log summaries that used to eat her Sundays. And a CoPilot-style experiment third: tutors who hit a wall mid-session can flag the coordinator's tablet, where the model drafts three possible next moves — *ask her to draw it; go back to the table of values; try the ramp example* — for the tutor to choose from. Her veteran tutors mostly ignore it. Her newest tutors call it the difference between drowning and swimming, which is roughly what the Tutor CoPilot trial measured: the assist concentrates exactly where the human capacity is thinnest.

**The dangerous moment.** The pilot's near-miss came from success. Off-hours chatbot usage grew so fast that by November the data showed a dozen of the center's regulars had stopped booking sessions entirely — the machine was easier, no scheduling, no social cost, no waiting list. Two of them were students Aisha knew: the kind for whom the center's Tuesday obligation had been the structural thread holding a week together. Easier had quietly become *alone*. The chatbot had not malfunctioned. It had substituted, one convenient evening at a time, and the only metric that caught it was a human coordinator noticing absence.

**The decision.** The budget meeting kept both lines, re-scoped. (1) The chatbot is infrastructure: training simulator, coordination engine, off-hours practice support — never described, funded, or measured as tutoring-program replacement. (2) The peer program remains the program; chatbot-heavy users who drop their human sessions get a personal nudge from a tutor, not an automated one. (3) The CoPilot-style assist stays tutor-facing: suggestions go to the tutor, never directly to the tutee. (4) Evidence is two-sided by design: tutee outcomes on unassisted classroom assessments, *and* tutor outcomes — grades, attendance, retention in the program — reported to the budget office annually as the return the license can't generate.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** train and rehearse tutors, coach them with mid-session suggestions, run matching and scheduling, prepare session materials, summarize logs for the coordinator, and provide clearly-labeled off-hours practice support.

**The students must** do the tutoring, the explaining, the asking, and the group work with each other — because the peer exchange, the interdependence, and the mutual accountability *are* the intervention, on both sides of the table. The coordinator must keep watching for the drift from supported-by to replaced-by, which no log flags automatically.

**Evidence that learning improved is** tutee performance on unassisted classroom and district measures, tutor academic trajectories and persistence, and the health of the structure itself — sessions kept, pairs sustained, help-seeking rates — none of which is a chatbot usage statistic.

## The Evidence Check

For any tool or proposal touching this group:

1. **Both sides of the exchange.** Tutee outcomes on work the tool never assisted, and tutor outcomes — the half of the effect that consolidation proposals systematically omit. If nobody is measuring tutor learning, the program's largest asset is invisible at exactly the moment a spreadsheet prices it.
2. **Structure metrics.** Session attendance, pair persistence, voluntary return rates, waiting-list behavior. A social intervention's vital signs are social; declining human-session bookings during rising chatbot usage is the substitution in progress.
3. **Interdependence audit.** For cooperative structures: can each member's necessary contribution be named? If the task would complete with one member and a chatbot, the design — not the students — failed first.
4. **Transfer to the unassisted room.** Performance in whole-class discussion, on individual assessments, in the next course — the downstream traces of explanations given and received by humans.

What does not count: chatbot session counts, conversation lengths, satisfaction ratings, or the polish of artifacts a group produced by stapling individual AI outputs together.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Pick one cooperative structure in your setting — a tutoring program, lab groups, literature circles. One sentence each: the mechanism (whose action matters to whom), the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution, and the proof that learning happened.
2. **Price the invisible half.** Take a real or imagined consolidation proposal for a peer program you know. List everything the program produces that the replacement service does not — tutor learning, obligation, belonging, help-seeking habits — and identify which of them your institution currently measures. The unmeasured list is your exposure.
3. **Red-team a group task.** Take one collaborative assignment you use and ask: could a student complete their share with an AI and no teammates? If yes, redesign for genuine interdependence — distributed information, individual accountability for the group's learning, a synthesis no single member can produce — and note that the redesign, not a chatbot policy, is what protects the mechanism.

## What Would Change My Mind

Two findings would force revision. If well-designed studies showed students working *with* AI partners gaining as much as students in well-structured human cooperative groups — on delayed individual measures *and* on collaborative-skill transfer to new human groups — then the interdependence story is wrong in part, and the conflict flag is overdrawn. Nothing current shows this; the VanLehn result covers tutee-side content gains, not the social half. And if longitudinal data showed peer-tutoring programs' tutor-side benefits failing to replicate at scale — the protégé effect as artifact of selection (the kind of student who tutors was already rising) — then the spreadsheet's arithmetic improves considerably, and this chapter's strongest card weakens. I would still bet on the structure. I would bet less.

## Still Puzzling

- The off-hours boundary will not stay drawn. Tonight's convenient chatbot session genuinely helps tonight; a hundred such nights quietly disassemble the program. Where the supplement becomes the substitute is a dose question nobody has data on, and "label it clearly" is a hope, not a mechanism.
- Tutor CoPilot's biggest gains went to the weakest tutors — which is wonderful, and raises an unresolved question: does the assist build those tutors' independent skill over time, or create skilled-seeming tutors who can't operate unassisted? The study measured students; nobody has measured the tutors' fading curve.
- For genuinely isolated learners — rural schools, homebound students, midnight workers — the human structure this chapter defends is sometimes not on offer at any price. What the second-best arrangement looks like, and how to keep it from becoming the default for the merely under-budgeted, is a question this book's framework can pose but local conscience has to answer.

## Further Reading

- **VanLehn, K. (2011). The relative effectiveness of human tutoring, intelligent tutoring systems, and other tutoring systems. *Educational Psychologist*, 46(4), 197–221.** The 0.79/0.76 result — read here as the honest complication: machine tutoring rivals human tutoring on the tutee side, which is precisely why the rest of the mechanism has to be argued and measured.
- **Wang, R., et al. (2024). Tutor CoPilot: A human-AI approach for scaling real-time expertise.** The +4/+9 percentage-point trial: AI inside the human tutoring loop, with gains concentrated where tutor skill is thinnest — this chapter's design template.
- **Johnson, D. W., & Johnson, R. T. (2009). An educational psychology success story: Social interdependence theory and cooperative learning. *Educational Researcher*, 38(5), 365–379.** The theory under the comparative rows: positive interdependence, individual accountability, promotive interaction — the ingredients no software inhabits.
- **Bastani, H., et al. (2025). Generative AI can harm learning. *PNAS*.** The assisted/unassisted divergence, applied here to groups: collective assisted output as the mask over absent collective cognition.
- **Aronson, E., & Patnoe, S. (2011). *Cooperation in the Classroom: The Jigsaw Method* (3rd ed.).** The fullest account of engineering interdependence on purpose — and the bridge to Chapter 15, where jigsaw gets its own decision.
