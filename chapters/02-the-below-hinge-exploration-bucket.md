# Chapter 2 — The Below-Hinge Exploration Bucket
*Why the largest group on the list is the one nobody reads correctly.*

This chapter covers the largest single collection in the book: the 130 influences on Hattie's list with effect sizes below 0.40 — his practical hinge point for instructional priority. Hattie's synthesis treats these as weaker, more context-dependent, or less central, and so does this book: they are gathered here for exploration rather than given a chapter each. The set runs from genuinely promising near-misses like worked examples and social skills programs down to influences with measured negative effects. For AI, this is mostly low-stakes territory — but low impact is exactly where novelty-driven adoption concentrates, which is why the group needs reading rather than skipping.

Here is a thing that happens in school districts. A board member reads John Hattie. They download a spreadsheet. They sort it descending by effect size, draw a horizontal line at 0.40, and send an email: everything below the line should be cut. Music. Drama. The bilingual expansion. The classroom-management strand in new-teacher mentoring. Redirect the funds to AI tutoring.

The same week, four AI vendors send pitches. All four cite Hattie. Three of the four map their products to influences below 0.40 — not that any pitch mentions it.

One person wants to use the below-hinge zone as a demolition list. Four vendors are hoping nobody checks whether their products live in it. Both errors come from the same mistake: treating 130 influences with d < 0.40 as a single thing called "doesn't work." This chapter is about what is actually in that drawer — and it is the first real stress test of Chapter 1's central principle. If an effect size is a prioritization signal and never a verdict, the below-hinge bucket is where that discipline either holds or collapses.

## What's Actually in the Drawer

This group collects every influence on the 2018 list with an effect size below 0.40: 130 of the 252, ranks 123 through 252, ranging from d = 0.39 down to d = −0.90, weighted average 0.15. It is the largest group in the book by a factor of six, and the only one defined by band rather than by any signature of its own. That is precisely why it must not be read as one thing.

The first honest act is to refuse the flat list. Three bands live in that drawer, and they are different in kind.

<!-- → [TABLE: Three-band breakdown of this group — columns: band name, d range, count of influences, representative examples, key characteristic; rows: Below-hinge, Negligible, Harmful — student should see that "below 0.40" contains three structurally different populations] -->

**Below hinge (81 influences, d = 0.15 to 0.39).** Real influences with real but modest average effects: social skills programs (0.39), drama and arts (0.38), music (0.37), worked examples (0.37), mobile phones (0.37), bilingual programs (0.36), classroom management (0.35), homework (0.29), class size reduction (0.21). Almost everything interesting in this chapter lives here, because almost everything here has a story the average conceals.

**Negligible (32 influences, d = 0.01 to 0.14).** Effects statistically distinguishable from nothing but practically close to it: school choice programs (0.12), mentoring as aggregated (0.12), teacher subject-matter knowledge as measured (0.11), background music (0.10), charter schools (0.09), single-sex schools (0.08), teacher performance pay (0.05), humor (0.04). Some are measurement casualties. Several are genuinely deflating findings about expensive structural reforms that many people built careers defending.

**Harmful (17 influences, d = −0.01 to −0.90).** And here is where the flat-list misread does its worst damage. Boredom (−0.49), depression (−0.36), retention in grade (−0.32), moving between schools (−0.34), suspension and expulsion (−0.20), television (−0.18), deafness (−0.61), ADHD (−0.90).

Stop. Read that band again. ADHD at −0.90 does not mean some program called ADHD damages children. It means the condition is associated with lower measured achievement — it describes a population that needs support, not a treatment to avoid. The same is true of deafness, depression, and family welfare status. They are not interventions. They are headwinds. The actionable exceptions are the genuine policy choices hiding among the conditions: retention in grade and suspension are decisions institutions make, and the negative signs on those rows are worth taking seriously. But the first question for any Harmful-band row is not "should we stop doing this?" — it is "is this a choice or a condition?" One paragraph of band hygiene prevents the most embarrassing misread the Hattie list makes possible.

So what the drawer actually contains: a set of specialized tools, a shelf of weak-as-measured averages, and a list of headwinds mislabeled as failures. Not a graveyard. The job is sorting, and sorting requires knowing *why* a number landed below the line.

## Four Ways a Number Gets to d < 0.40

Every influence below the hinge got there by one of four routes. The four demand opposite responses — which is why the taxonomy is the chapter's working tool, not just a framework to appreciate and forget.

**Wrong ruler.** Recall from Chapter 1 what Kraft (2020) established about field trials: in rigorous experiments on standardized outcomes, d = 0.20 is *large*. The hinge was computed over a synthesis thick with small, brief, proximal-measure studies. Honest field results look modest standing next to inflated lab results. Class size reduction at d = 0.21 is the classic case — its evidence base includes some of the largest true randomized experiments in education history, which is exactly why its number is modest. A 0.21 from that kind of evidence may be worth more than a 0.50 from ten one-week studies.

The wrong-ruler problem also means the hinge is silent about cost, and cost is not a detail. Run the arithmetic on two below-hinge options: class size reduction at roughly $3,000 per student per year for d ≈ 0.21, versus an AI tutoring deployment at roughly $50 per student for d ≈ 0.17 (illustrative figures — the point is the structure, not the decimals). Per 0.10 standard deviations of effect, the two differ in cost by more than an order of magnitude. The hinge sees two sub-0.40 failures. A budget sees two utterly different propositions. Effect size answers one question: "how much, on average, ignoring everything else?" Adoption decisions are made of everything the hinge ignores: cost, scalability, equity, and who specifically benefits.

**Averaged-away moderators.** Some influences work powerfully for specific learners under specific conditions and reverse outside them — and the average smears both into mediocrity. The bucket's resident example is its most instructive row: worked examples, d = 0.37.

Cognitive load theory delivers one of the most replicated findings in instructional psychology: novices learn problem-solving better from studying worked solutions than from unsupported problem-solving (Sweller and Cooper, 1985; Atkinson, Derry, Renkl and Wortham, 2000). Sweller has called this the worked-example effect the best known and most widely studied of all cognitive load effects. The reason is structural: a novice confronting an unguided problem has no schema to work with, so most of their working memory goes to managing the search rather than learning the structure. A worked solution shows the structure. Working memory can focus on understanding it.

Then the expertise reversal effect (Kalyuga, Ayres, Chandler and Sweller, 2003) flips the sign. For advanced learners, worked examples become redundant — and the redundancy is not harmless. The learner who has already internalized the schema finds the worked solution's guidance friction rather than scaffold. Problem-solving, stripped of that guidance, now wins. "What is the effect of worked examples?" has no single answer. The moderator — *where is the learner on the novice-to-expert continuum?* — is the finding. The practice that honors both is fading: worked example, then completion problem, then full problem, with the scaffolding retired as expertise grows.

The public number has a biography that makes the averaging visible. Visible Learning 2009 reported worked examples at d = 0.57 — above the hinge — based on one meta-analysis. The 2018 list shows 0.37, after a later meta-analysis was averaged in: Wittwer and Renkl's (2010) synthesis of instructional explanations within example-based learning, a related but different construct, which came in considerably lower [verify: the reconciliation mechanics come from edition-comparison readings; direction is corroborated, but confirm the arithmetic against the MetaX database before quoting specifics]. The underlying cognitive-load literature did not change at all. The number moved because the *averaging pool* changed. Any reader who internalizes that one biography is permanently inoculated against treating a Hattie decimal as a stable property of the world rather than an artifact of a particular synthesis at a particular moment.

**Not an intervention at all.** Dozens of bucket rows are correlations, conditions, and structural variables wearing the same costume as interventions. "Relations of high school achievement to career performance" is a correlation between two outcomes — there is nothing to implement. Relative age, family structure, parental employment, gender: context, not levers. The entire Harmful band, minus its two policy exceptions. For these rows, the question "should we adopt it?" is grammatically broken. Any pitch or plan built on such a row has failed before evidence enters the room.

**Genuinely weak as deployed.** And some entries earn their number honestly. Web-based learning at 0.18. One-to-one laptop programs at 0.16. Distance education at 0.13. Use of PowerPoint at 0.26. These are aggregates of technology as actually implemented at scale, across decades — and the dataset's recurring annotation for them is the chapter's refrain: *this is the honest number vendors should be required to cite.* Not zero — modest, real, and a fair warning about what technology-as-delivery-mechanism historically returns.

Four reasons. Four responses: re-judge on the right ruler; chase the moderator; reclassify as context; believe the number. The unforgivable move is applying any response before asking which reason holds for the row in front of you.

## Where Vendor Pitches Go to Hide

Here is the AI angle, and it is sharper for this group than for any other. The below-hinge bucket is where vendor pitches go to hide. The camouflage works because the influences sound good — homework, gamification, presentations, mobile learning are all familiar, plausible, classroom-shaped words — and because almost nobody checks the rank of the influence behind the product. A pitch that says "our tool supports homework completion" is wrapping itself in an influence at d = 0.29. The word sounds educational. The number is sub-hinge. The pitch is counting on the listener to hear the word and never see the number.

The mirror error matters just as much: AI novelty does not lift the host influence's ceiling. An AI-generated slide deck inherits the evidence base of slide decks (0.26). An AI gamified app inherits gamification's (0.35). A phone-delivered tutor inherits the mobile-phone context (0.37 — and see below for why even that number flatters). The mechanism did not change. The ceiling did not either. The only honest exception: if the tool genuinely changes *who does the cognitive work or what feedback loop exists*, then it is a different mechanism — and it should be classified and evidenced as one, on its own results, not on the host influence's borrowed clothes.

Two bucket rows deserve named warnings because the products aimed at them are already in circulation.

**Homework (d = 0.29).** The bucket's only high-vendor-activity row, and the most dangerous one. Homework's modest average conceals its mechanism: independent practice and consolidation — the unassisted engine-run. An AI "homework helper" that supplies answers does not augment that mechanism. It deletes it, replacing consolidation with transcription. This is the Bastani pattern from Chapter 1 sold as a subscription: better-looking homework, less learning inside it. A below-hinge influence with high vendor activity and a mechanism conflict is the exact coordinates of maximum skepticism.

**Mobile phones (d = 0.37).** A naive reading: phones nearly clear the hinge; a phone-based learning platform is nearly above-average. The row's notes say the opposite. Physical phone presence carries distraction and multitasking costs. The external evidence is genuinely mixed, but its direction is consistent: Beland and Murphy's UK study of school phone bans found post-ban exam gains of roughly 0.07 standard deviations overall, concentrated in low achievers at around 0.14, with high achievers essentially unaffected [verify exact coefficients against the published *Labour Economics* version before quoting decimals]; a 2024 rapid review puts ban effects near d = 0.16, stronger for wellbeing than achievement; recent scoping reviews counsel against one-size-fits-all conclusions [contested: magnitudes and the right policy grain are actively debated]. A mid-pack d sitting on top of a sign-flipping, population-dependent literature: the number alone would have told you precisely nothing you needed.

## The Case in Full

Return now to the assistant superintendent's problem: a demolition list from a board member and a vendor menu from an AI committee, arriving the same week. Both need the same instrument.

The board member's cut list first. She returns the spreadsheet with one column added: *which of the four reasons puts this row below 0.40?*

Music and drama. The achievement effect size was never the right measure — Sala and Gobet's (2017) review finds far transfer from music instruction to academic outcomes small to null in the best-controlled designs [contested: the near/far-transfer partition has named critics, including Bigand and Tillmann]. The honest defense of the arts is not test-score alchemy but the outcomes arts actually produce: musicianship, ensemble experience, performance before a human audience, belonging. Defending arts with achievement effect sizes loses on the evidence. Cutting them with achievement effect sizes was never a valid move either. Wrong measure, both directions — and notice what just happened. The below-hinge analysis protected programs from a bad defense as much as from a bad attack.

The classroom-management strand is the cut she refuses outright, and it is the bucket's failure case in miniature. The 2018 number is 0.35; Visible Learning 2009 had reported 0.52 [verify: the shift mechanics come from edition-comparison readings — confirm before quoting]. More fundamentally, classroom management is an enabling condition: it purchases the time-on-task and relational order inside which every above-hinge influence operates. Cutting it to fund AI tutoring would demolish the floor to buy furniture. A below-hinge influence can be a load-bearing wall. Rank is not importance.

Bilingual programs (0.36) she flags for separate, local analysis — a population-specific intervention whose aggregate number says little about its value for this district's actual multilingual learners.

Then the vendor menu, sorted with the same column. The presentation generator: host influence d = 0.26, reason four, honest number — legitimate as a teacher time-saver, and the time savings count only if the recovered hours return to learning; no learning claims allowed in the contract. The gamified math app: gaming and simulations at 0.35 with a partial-conflict flag — a small pilot with transfer conditions stated in advance, and an outcome measure that requires unassisted performance, not in-app scores. The homework helper: decline, mechanism conflict plus high vendor activity; the committee's one-line rationale reads "this product automates the part that was the learning." The phone microlearning platform: decline, with the mobile-phones row and its notes attached in full as the explanation.

The final artifact is one page: each proposal tagged *explore / niche / outside the plan*, each tag carrying a one-sentence mechanism rationale and, where relevant, which of the four reasons applies.

The sort, note well, is not in the data. The same rows sort differently in a district whose pressing problem is early literacy than in one bleeding new teachers. The bucket is an exploration bucket precisely because the local problem statement — not the spreadsheet — supplies the sorting key.

## Where AI Has Standing, and Where It Does Not

AI may serve as the bucket's research assistant and honest broker: pull the evidence behind any below-hinge influence, surface the moderators an average conceals, generate the four-reasons analysis for a committee to interrogate, draft cost-per-effect comparisons.

For the bucket's legitimately AI-native rows — worked examples among them — AI can generate instructional material, but only under the constraint the expertise reversal effect imposes: for novices, faded on a schedule, with independent performance as the measure of when fading should begin. An AI tutor that never fades is not implementing worked examples. It is implementing dependence.

The bucket's mechanism-conflict rows — homework as independent consolidation, classroom management as behavioral presence, arts as embodied practice before a human audience — stay on the human side of the line. Not because AI is categorically insufficient, but because the mechanism *is* the learning, and a substitution that changes the mechanism is not an upgrade. It is a replacement of a different product, without evidence that the replacement works.

The proof that the sorting held is not in the plan document. It is in the outcome measures, twelve months later: delayed, unassisted, on transfer tasks that the AI tool never touched.

## What Would Change My Mind

The claim beneath this chapter is that band membership carries almost no decision content until disaggregated by reason, moderator, and mechanism. Evidence against it would look like this: AI versions of below-hinge influences repeatedly and durably outperforming their host influence's ceiling on delayed, unassisted measures — AI homework helpers improving consolidation rather than destroying it, gamified AI math apps producing transfer that gamification never did. If that pattern emerged across honest trials, then AI would be changing mechanisms, not just interfaces, at scale — and several declines in this chapter would need to be reversed in public. The Bastani result and the bucket's own honest-number tech rows make this unlikely. They do not make it impossible.

## Still Puzzling

Interleaved practice sits at d = 0.21. One of cognitive psychology's most celebrated lab effects is in this bucket, twenty points under worked examples' worst press. Implementation gap? Measurement timing — interleaving wins on delayed tests and loses on immediate ones? Another averaging casualty? The reconciliation would teach the field something important, and it has not been written.

Where is the fading line for AI worked examples? "Novices only, faded over time" is the correct policy and an unmapped one. No one knows how to detect, at scale, the moment a learner crosses from benefiting to coasting — the expertise-reversal boundary is real in the literature and invisible in the tooling.

Do honest numbers ever update? Web-based learning (0.18) and distance education (0.13) were measured on pre-LLM deployments. The skeptical prior is that interface novelty never moved these aggregates before. But "never before" is a prior, not a law — and the field is currently running the experiment, without a control group, in every district at once.

## Further Reading

**Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The right ruler for the bucket's field-trial citizens; the corrective for every "below 0.40 means failure" reading this chapter dismantles.

**Kalyuga, S., Ayres, P., Chandler, P., & Sweller, J. (2003). The expertise reversal effect. *Educational Psychologist*, 38(1), 23–31.** The moderator that explains the bucket's richest row — why worked examples have no single effect size and why AI tutors need a fading schedule.

**Atkinson, R. K., Derry, S. J., Renkl, A., & Wortham, D. (2000). Learning from examples: Instructional principles from the worked examples research. *Review of Educational Research*, 70(2), 181–214.** The worked-examples evidence base in full.

**Sala, G., & Gobet, F. (2017). Does far transfer exist? Negative evidence from chess, music, and working memory training. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, 26(6), 515–520.** Why the arts should never be defended — or attacked — with achievement effect sizes.

**Beland, L.-P., & Murphy, R. (2016). Ill communication: Technology, distraction and student performance. *Labour Economics*, 41, 61–76.** The phone-ban evidence behind the bucket's most misreadable row.

**Slavin, R. (2018). John Hattie is wrong. *Robert Slavin's Blog*, June 21, 2018.** The inflation argument that explains why honest field numbers look weak beside the hinge.
