# Chapter 38 — G37: ICT as Infrastructure, Not Pedagogy

## The Decision on the Table

Owen Marsh has been a district technology director long enough to have personally decommissioned three revolutions. The interactive whiteboards are in a warehouse. The netbook carts are gone. The tablet initiative survives as a line item nobody defends. Now the district's four-year device lease is up for renewal, and the renewal is no longer a device lease: the vendor has bundled the laptops with an "AI Learning Suite" — embedded tutor, writing assistant, auto-grading, analytics — and priced the bundle so that declining the suite saves almost nothing. The proposal is $4.6 million over four years, and the deck's research slide says: *Information and communications technology: d = 0.47 (Hattie). Above the hinge. Technology works.*

Owen knows the number is doing something illegitimate, but the meeting needs more than a feeling. The CFO wants to know whether the bundle is worth it. The curriculum director wants to know whether the suite improves learning. The board wants one recommendation, not a seminar. And the strange truth Owen has half-articulated over three decommissioned revolutions is that these are *different questions with different standards of proof* — that a district which evaluates connectivity the way it evaluates pedagogy will starve its infrastructure, and a district that evaluates pedagogy the way it evaluates infrastructure will buy anything that runs.

This chapter is the seminar the meeting doesn't have time for, compressed to a distinction that fits on the one slide Owen gets: **infrastructure is judged by access and reliability; pedagogy is judged by mechanism and learning. ICT's d = 0.47 belongs to neither product on the table — and the AI suite, in particular, may not borrow it.**

## What This Group Is

A single influence, classified: Above hinge, **AI-ADJACENT**, low human irreducibility, **high substitution risk**, no cognitive-work conflict.

Read alongside Chapter 37's row and the kinship is plain — another AI-ADJACENT aggregate, another context variable rather than a practice. But where school effects carried low substitution risk, this row carries **high**, and the asymmetry is the chapter. Nothing in "ICT" as studied conflicts with any learner's cognitive work — the influence is mostly a measure of whether technology was present while something pedagogical happened. The substitution risk is at the *category* level: ICT is the bucket into which institutions pour spending that substitutes for pedagogical decisions. Buying the technology feels like doing the teaching reform. The risk is not that the tool does the student's thinking; it is that the purchase does the district's.

And this row carries the dataset's most explicit consumer warning, worth quoting in full because it will be needed in the case study: this aggregate pre-dates LLMs; *vendors citing this number for generative AI products are making an unsupported claim.* The d = 0.47 is a pre-LLM bucket — drill software, word processors, multimedia, early one-to-one programs — and it transfers to a 2026 AI suite exactly as well as the netbook literature transferred to the netbooks in Owen's warehouse.

## The Influences

**Information communications technology (ICT) (d = 0.47)** — rank 93 on the 2018 252-influence list. The honest reading of this number requires the longest historical lens in the book, because educational technology is the field's most replicated finding *about itself*.

Start with the refrain Hattie himself has repeated for years: across the decades of meta-analyses — computer-assisted instruction in the seventies, multimedia in the nineties, web-based learning in the two-thousands, one-to-one programs in the twenty-tens — the technology effects keep landing in the same modest band, typically somewhere in the 0.3-to-0.5 range, edition after edition, technology after technology [verify — the flat-trend claim is one of Hattie's most-cited observations; the exact band varies by aggregation]. Fifty years of revolutionary devices, one stubbornly unrevolutionary average. Larry Cuban documented the pattern's pre-digital prehistory — film, radio, and television each entered the classroom on the same rhetoric of transformation and exited into the same closets — and gave the post-mortem its standard title: *oversold and underused*.

The system-level evidence is blunter still. The OECD's 2015 *Students, Computers and Learning* analysis found that countries investing most heavily in classroom ICT showed **no appreciable improvement** in PISA reading, math, or science; students using computers moderately at school did somewhat better than rare users, while the heaviest users did *worse*, even after controlling for background. Zheng and colleagues' (2016) meta-analysis of one-to-one laptop programs — the closest research cousin of Owen's lease — found modest positive effects, strongest in writing, on the order of a tenth to a sixth of a standard deviation [verify — magnitudes by subject vary across the included studies]. Respectable, real, and a long way from a revolution; right at home against Kraft's benchmarks, and a useful calibration for any deck implying that devices move districts.

Why does the bucket average above the hinge at all? Because the bucket's contents are not devices — they are *pedagogical implementations that happened to involve devices*. Unbundle any era's positive studies and the active ingredients are familiar to readers of this book: more practice with feedback (Chapter 10's territory), better writing-process support (Chapter 39's, one chapter ahead), increased instructional time, clearer worked examples. The technology carried the influence; it was not the influence. **ICT's d = 0.47 is the average temperature of everything ever cooked in an electric oven.** It tells you ovens conduct heat. It does not tell you tonight's dinner will be good.

## Why It Works

So what is the mechanism of an influence that is mostly a container? It has one, and naming it precisely is what generates the chapter's decision rule. Technology contributes to learning through exactly three channels, all indirect:

**Access.** Devices and connectivity put learning materials, practice opportunities, and (post-2022) AI capabilities physically in front of students who would otherwise not have them. This channel is real, equity-loaded, and *binary in its logic*: its value is counted in who can now do what they couldn't, and it is the entire legitimate meaning of the word "infrastructure." Note what this implies: the access channel justifies devices and bandwidth on a *utility* standard — reliability, coverage, cost — the same standard as heating. No effect size required, and none honestly available.

**Amplification.** Technology makes existing pedagogical mechanisms cheaper, faster, or more frequent: more retrieval practice per week, feedback in minutes instead of days, texts at the right difficulty on demand. This channel is where every genuine positive result in the ICT literature lives — and its accounting rule is the one this book has enforced for thirty-seven chapters: the effect belongs to the *mechanism amplified*, audited by that mechanism's own chapter, under that mechanism's own phase gate. The oven takes no credit for the recipe.

**Displacement.** The channel the deck never mentions: every device-hour displaces something, every dollar displaces something, every unit of teacher attention spent managing platforms displaces a unit spent reading student work. The OECD's heavy-user finding is best read here — past a threshold, technology use displaces higher-value instructional interaction. Displacement is why "more technology" has no default sign, and why the heaviest-investing systems showed nothing.

The decision rule falls out of the three channels: **fund access on a utility standard; evaluate amplification mechanism by mechanism; account for displacement explicitly.** A bundle — by construction — prevents all three from being done separately. That is what a bundle is for.

## What AI Can Safely Do

For this row, "What can AI safely do" has an unusual referent: the question is less about learners than about what an AI-era district can safely do with the category. The safe roles:

- **Run the infrastructure itself.** Inventory, provisioning, network management, accessibility compliance, filtering, device health — the unglamorous layer where AI-assisted operations genuinely cut cost and downtime, and where no learning mechanism is anywhere near the loop.
- **Audit access.** Map who actually has working devices, bandwidth at home, and functioning assistive tech — the equity census most districts have never accurately taken. This is Chapter 37's instrument-panel logic applied to the technology layer.
- **Measure displacement.** Usage analytics aimed honestly: what is screen time during class *replacing*? Which platform hours correlate with which instructional configurations? Districts have never been able to see their own displacement channel; now they can, if anyone asks the dashboard that question.
- **Unbundle vendor claims.** Each AI suite component maps to an influence with a chapter in this book: the embedded tutor to Chapter 21's testing-and-tutoring gates, the writing assistant to Chapter 39's authorship gate, auto-grading to Chapter 31's feedback conditions, analytics to Chapters 6 and 37. The safe institutional move is to route each component to its own chapter's phase gate — and to refuse, on classification grounds, any evaluation of "the suite" as a unit.

The dataset's hypothesis column for this row is deliberately empty: N/A — *broad pre-LLM technology bucket; do not use as evidence for modern AI products.* No [HYPOTHESIS] effect is offered because the classification's whole claim is that this row must not be the unit of adoption analysis. The bucket gets no number; the contents get chapters.

## The Substitution Trap

The trap is institutional, and it has a fifty-year track record: **the purchase substitutes for the pedagogy.**

It works through three reliable conversions. *Spending converts to virtue:* a district that has bought devices feels it has modernized instruction, the way a person who has bought running shoes feels fitter. *Bundling converts to inevitability:* once the suite ships inside the lease, every classroom has the tutor and the writing assistant by default — Chapter 36's lesson at its largest grain: the default state of the learning environment is a pedagogical decision made, here, by a procurement schedule. Tools no teacher chose, governed by no phase gate, arrive as ambient conditions in nine schools at once. *The aggregate converts to evidence:* d = 0.47 stands in for the evaluation nobody ran, the pre-LLM bucket laundering the post-LLM product — the precise unsupported claim the dataset's warning names.

What makes the AI-era version of this trap categorically worse than the whiteboard era is the payload. A whiteboard oversold was money wasted; the displacement was budgetary. An AI suite oversold ships *active substitution engines* — unguardrailed tutoring (the Bastani −17 configuration), one-click writing generation, instant summarization — into every classroom simultaneously, as defaults. The infrastructure decision quietly makes a hundred pedagogical decisions, each one belonging to a different chapter of this book, none of them actually made by anyone. The bundle is not a discount structure. It is a gate bypass.

## Case Study

Owen Marsh's recommendation memo ran two pages, and its first line did the work: *We are being asked one question that is actually two — a utility renewal and a pedagogy adoption — and I recommend we answer them separately, because they have different standards of proof.*

**The tool.** The device lease plus AI Learning Suite bundle. **The influence.** ICT, d = 0.47 — presented to the board with the dataset's warning quoted verbatim, followed by Owen's translation: "This number was assembled before the products being sold existed. It can justify the laptops. It cannot justify the suite."

**The useful moment.** Unbundling turned out to be a negotiating position the vendor had priced to discourage, not prevent. The district renewed the device and connectivity layer on a pure utility evaluation — four years of ticket data, a coverage audit, total cost per functioning student-device against two competitors — the way it would renew a bus contract. The access audit Owen's team ran became, unexpectedly, the renewal's best artifact: it found four hundred students whose "district device" was functionally dead and a home-bandwidth gap concentrated in two attendance zones, and the savings from declining the suite's first year funded the fix. Infrastructure, judged as infrastructure, improved as infrastructure.

**The dangerous moment.** The suite nearly came in anyway, through the side door bundles are built for: the vendor offered the AI components "free for year one" rather than lose the device contract, and the curriculum office — under genuine pressure from teachers already improvising with consumer chatbots — wanted to say yes to everything at once. The meeting turned on one exchange. The vendor's representative, pressed for evidence on the embedded tutor, reached for the slide: *technology in schools, d = 0.47.* The curriculum director asked one question: "Is that number from studies of this product, this product category, or any product using a language model?" The answer, honestly given, was no, no, and no. The suite went to pilot instead of district-wide default: the tutor under Chapter 21's guardrail conditions in two schools, the writing assistant held entirely pending the writing program's authorship-gate work (one chapter from here), auto-grading configured under Chapter 31's teacher-review gate.

**The decision.** (1) Devices and connectivity renewed on the utility standard, with the access audit institutionalized as an annual census; (2) the AI suite unbundled contractually — no component activates by default; each enters classrooms only through its own influence's phase gate, piloted with pre-registered evidence plans; (3) the d = 0.47 slide formally retired from district procurement by a one-line policy Owen drafted for the adoption framework: *aggregate technology effect sizes are not admissible evidence for specific products*; (4) displacement reporting added to the analytics contract: the district now asks its own dashboard, quarterly, what platform time is replacing.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** operate and optimize the infrastructure layer, run the access and reliability census, measure usage and displacement honestly, and support the unbundling analysis that routes each embedded pedagogical tool to its own influence's gate.

**The humans must** make the pedagogy decisions the bundle would otherwise make by default — component by component, chapter by chapter, with named owners — and hold the line that no aggregate technology number ever stands in for a product evaluation. **The institution must** keep two ledgers: infrastructure judged on access, reliability, and cost; pedagogy judged on mechanism and learning evidence — and refuse any contract that forces the ledgers to merge.

**Evidence that the spending worked is,** for the infrastructure ledger: access gaps closed, uptime, cost per functioning device, accessibility compliance — utility metrics, honestly sufficient. For the pedagogy ledger: each activated component's own Z, from its own chapter — delayed, unassisted, transfer-bearing performance, defined before activation. Never device counts, deployment speed, platform engagement, or an aggregate effect size born before the product category existed.

## The Evidence Check

For any device program, platform renewal, or bundled AI suite, demand:

1. **The two-ledger split.** Which line items are infrastructure (utility standard) and which are pedagogy (learning standard)? A proposal that cannot be split is a proposal to bypass every gate in this book at once; split it or decline it.
2. **Citation provenance.** For every research number in the deck: was it measured on this product, this category, or this century's technology? The pre-LLM bucket test — and the question that ended this chapter's dangerous moment in one sentence.
3. **The access census.** Who gains what capability they lacked? Infrastructure's honest value is here, and a vendor with a strong access story should be made to tell it as an access story, not an achievement story.
4. **The displacement account.** What does the proposal's usage model replace — instructional minutes, teacher attention, budget lines — and who signed off on the trade? The OECD heavy-user curve is what unexamined displacement looks like at national scale.
5. **Default-state inventory.** List every capability that activates by default across every classroom on day one. Each item on the list is a pedagogical decision; each needs an owner and a gate, or it needs to ship disabled.

What does not count: devices deployed, rollout speed, login rates, teacher enthusiasm in week two, vendor case studies from unnamed districts, or d = 0.47.

Write one sentence each: the mechanism (which channel — access, amplification, displacement), the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution (the purchase standing in for the pedagogy), and the proof that anything improved.
2. **Split a real bundle.** Take an actual proposal or contract your institution holds and sort every line item into the two ledgers. Mark each pedagogy item with the chapter of this book that owns its gate. Items with no chapter and no owner are your institution's ungoverned defaults — count them.
3. **Run the provenance test.** Collect the research citations from the last vendor deck your institution received. For each number, answer the curriculum director's three questions: this product? this category? any LLM-era product at all? Score the deck. Decks scoring zero for three are the norm, which is the point.

## What Would Change My Mind

The infrastructure/pedagogy distinction would need rebuilding if the *integration itself* turned out to carry learning value — if randomized comparisons showed bundled, default-on AI suites outperforming the same components adopted selectively through individual gates, because coherence, ubiquity, and zero-friction access beat governance. Owen's three decommissioned revolutions argue otherwise, but the LLM era's tools differ in kind from whiteboards, and "the gates cost more learning than they protect" is an empirical claim someone will eventually test. Likewise, if a new generation of technology-bucket meta-analyses — built entirely on LLM-era products, with Kraft-credible designs — converged well above the historical 0.3-to-0.5 band, the fifty-year flat trend would be broken, and this chapter's deepest prior with it. The honest current state: the early generative-AI outcome literature is young, heterogeneous, and vendor-adjacent, and the flat trend has survived every previous challenger.

## Still Puzzling

- The access channel is genuinely different in the AI era: a device now carries not content but *capability* — a tutor, a writing engine, an answer machine. Whether universal access to capability is an equity floor or a universally distributed substitution risk is the unresolved tension between this chapter and every gate in the book; it is both, and the ratio is unmeasured.
- Teachers improvising with consumer chatbots while the district deliberates is the pressure that nearly activated the suite — and it names a real governance hole: the alternative to a gated bundle is not no AI; it is ungoverned AI on personal accounts. Whether slow unbundled adoption beats fast bundled adoption *in practice* depends on that counterfactual, and nobody has good data on it.
- Displacement remains the channel nobody instruments. The tooling now exists to measure what platform time replaces; the institutional appetite to know is another matter. The first district to publish an honest displacement account will teach the field more than the next dozen efficacy studies.

## Further Reading

- **OECD (2015). *Students, Computers and Learning: Making the Connection*. PISA, OECD Publishing.** The system-level null: heavy national ICT investment, no PISA improvement, and the heavy-user curve that gives the displacement channel its data.
- **Cuban, L. (2001). *Oversold and Underused: Computers in the Classroom*. Harvard University Press.** The pattern's historian — and *Teachers and Machines* (1986) for the film-radio-television prehistory that makes the fifty-year flat trend a century-long one.
- **Zheng, B., Warschauer, M., Lin, C.-H., & Chang, C. (2016). Learning in one-to-one laptop environments: A meta-analysis and research synthesis. *Review of Educational Research*, 86(4), 1052–1084.** The fairest research cousin of a device lease: modest, real, writing-tilted effects — the honest ceiling for what hardware alone has ever delivered.
- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The ruler that prices every claim in this chapter's procurement scenes — and the reason "modest, real" is not faint praise.
- **U.S. Department of Education (2023). *Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning*.** The policy frame for the unbundling move: human-in-the-loop adoption, component-level evaluation, and the refusal to treat "AI" as a single thing to be for or against.
