# Chapter 38 — G37: ICT as Infrastructure, Not Pedagogy
*The d = 0.47 Belongs to Neither Product on the Table.*

Owen Marsh has personally decommissioned three revolutions.

The interactive whiteboards are in a warehouse. The netbook carts are gone. The tablet initiative survives as a line item nobody defends. Now the district's four-year device lease is up for renewal, and the renewal is no longer a device lease. The vendor has bundled the laptops with an "AI Learning Suite" — embedded tutor, writing assistant, auto-grading, analytics — and priced the bundle so that declining the suite saves almost nothing. The proposal is $4.6 million over four years. The deck's research slide says: *Information and communications technology: d = 0.47 (Hattie). Above the hinge. Technology works.*

Owen knows the number is doing something illegitimate, but the meeting needs more than a feeling. The CFO wants to know whether the bundle is worth it. The curriculum director wants to know whether the suite improves learning. The board wants one recommendation. And the strange truth Owen has half-articulated over three decommissioned revolutions is that these are different questions with different standards of proof — that a district which evaluates connectivity the way it evaluates pedagogy will starve its infrastructure, and a district that evaluates pedagogy the way it evaluates infrastructure will buy anything that runs.

This chapter is the seminar the meeting doesn't have time for, compressed to the distinction that fits on one slide: **infrastructure is judged by access and reliability; pedagogy is judged by mechanism and learning. The d = 0.47 belongs to neither product on the table — and the AI suite, in particular, may not borrow it.**

## The Classification and Its Warning

A single influence, classified: above the hinge, AI-ADJACENT, low human irreducibility, high substitution risk, no cognitive-work conflict.

Nothing in "ICT" as studied conflicts with any learner's cognitive work — the influence is mostly a measure of whether technology was present while something pedagogical happened. The substitution risk is at the category level: ICT is the bucket into which institutions pour spending that substitutes for pedagogical decisions. Buying the technology feels like doing the teaching reform.

And this row carries the dataset's most explicit consumer warning, worth stating in full because it will be needed in the meeting: this aggregate predates LLMs. Vendors citing this number for generative AI products are making an unsupported claim. The d = 0.47 is a pre-LLM bucket — drill software, word processors, multimedia, early one-to-one programs — and it transfers to a 2026 AI suite exactly as well as the netbook literature transferred to the netbooks in Owen's warehouse.

## What the 0.47 Actually Represents

Information communications technology (d = 0.47) — rank 93 on the 2018 252-influence list.

The honest reading requires the longest historical lens in the book, because educational technology is the field's most replicated finding about itself. Across the decades of meta-analyses — computer-assisted instruction in the seventies, multimedia in the nineties, web-based learning in the two-thousands, one-to-one programs in the twenty-tens — technology effects keep landing in the same modest band, edition after edition, technology after technology. [verify — the flat-trend claim is among Hattie's most-cited observations; exact band varies by aggregation] Fifty years of revolutionary devices, one stubbornly unrevolutionary average. Larry Cuban documented the pattern's pre-digital prehistory — film, radio, and television each entered the classroom on the same rhetoric of transformation and exited into the same closets — and gave the post-mortem its standard title: *oversold and underused*.

The system-level evidence is blunter. The OECD's 2015 analysis of sixty-four countries found that those investing most heavily in classroom ICT showed no appreciable improvement in PISA reading, math, or science. Students using computers moderately at school did somewhat better than rare users; the heaviest users did worse, even after controlling for background. Zheng and colleagues' (2016) meta-analysis of one-to-one laptop programs — the research closest to Owen's lease — found modest positive effects, strongest in writing, on the order of a tenth to a sixth of a standard deviation. [verify — magnitudes by subject vary across included studies] Respectable, real, and a long way from a revolution.

Why does the bucket average above the hinge at all? Because the bucket's contents are not devices. They are pedagogical implementations that happened to involve devices. Unbundle any era's positive studies and the active ingredients are familiar: more practice with feedback, better writing-process support, increased instructional time, clearer worked examples. The technology carried those influences; it was not them.

ICT's d = 0.47 is the average temperature of everything ever cooked in an electric oven. It tells you ovens conduct heat. It does not tell you tonight's dinner will be good.

## Three Channels, and Why Distinguishing Them Is Everything

Technology contributes to learning through exactly three channels, all indirect. Naming them precisely generates the decision rule.

**Access.** Devices and connectivity put learning materials, practice opportunities, and AI capabilities physically in front of students who would otherwise lack them. This channel is real, equity-loaded, and binary in its logic: its value is counted in who can now do what they previously couldn't. This is the entire legitimate meaning of the word infrastructure. Access justifies devices and bandwidth on a utility standard — reliability, coverage, cost — the same standard as heating. No effect size required, and none honestly available.

**Amplification.** Technology makes existing pedagogical mechanisms cheaper, faster, or more frequent: more retrieval practice per week, feedback in hours instead of days, texts at the right difficulty on demand. This channel is where every genuine positive result in the ICT literature lives — and its accounting rule is the one this book has enforced for thirty-seven chapters: the effect belongs to the mechanism amplified, audited by that mechanism's own chapter, under that mechanism's own phase gate. The oven takes no credit for the recipe.

**Displacement.** Every device-hour displaces something. Every dollar displaces something. Every unit of teacher attention spent managing platforms displaces a unit spent reading student work. The OECD's heavy-user finding is best read here — past a threshold, technology displaces higher-value instructional interaction. Displacement is why "more technology" has no default sign, and why the heaviest-investing systems showed nothing.

The decision rule falls out of the three channels: fund access on a utility standard; evaluate amplification mechanism by mechanism; account for displacement explicitly. A bundle prevents all three from being done separately. That is what a bundle is for.

## What AI Can Safely Do

For this row, "what can AI safely do" has an unusual referent. The question is less about learners than about what an AI-era district can safely do with the category.

Run the infrastructure itself. Inventory, provisioning, network management, accessibility compliance, filtering, device health — the layer where AI-assisted operations genuinely cut cost and downtime, and where no learning mechanism is anywhere near the loop.

Audit access. Map who actually has working devices, bandwidth at home, and functioning assistive tech — the equity census most districts have never accurately taken.

Measure displacement. Usage analytics aimed honestly: what is screen time during class replacing? Which platform hours correlate with which instructional configurations? Districts have never been able to see their own displacement channel at this resolution. The capability exists; the institutional appetite to look is another matter.

Unbundle vendor claims. Each AI suite component maps to an influence with a chapter in this book: the embedded tutor to Chapter 21's testing-and-tutoring gates, the writing assistant to Chapter 39's authorship gate, auto-grading to Chapter 31's feedback conditions, analytics to Chapters 6 and 37. The safe institutional move is to route each component to its own chapter's phase gate — and to refuse, on classification grounds, any evaluation of "the suite" as a unit.

The dataset's hypothesis column for this row is deliberately empty: *N/A — broad pre-LLM technology bucket; do not use as evidence for modern AI products.* No hypothesized effect is offered because the classification's whole claim is that this row must not be the unit of adoption analysis.

## The Substitution Trap

The trap is institutional, and it has a fifty-year track record: the purchase substitutes for the pedagogy.

It works through three reliable conversions. Spending converts to virtue: a district that has bought devices feels it has modernized instruction, the way a person who has bought running shoes feels fitter. Bundling converts to inevitability: once the suite ships inside the lease, every classroom has the tutor and the writing assistant by default — the lesson from Chapter 36 at its largest grain. Tools no teacher chose, governed by no phase gate, arrive as ambient conditions in nine schools at once. And the aggregate converts to evidence: d = 0.47 stands in for the evaluation nobody ran, the pre-LLM bucket laundering the post-LLM product — the precise unsupported claim the dataset's warning names.

What makes the AI-era version categorically worse than the whiteboard era is the payload. A whiteboard oversold was money wasted; the displacement was budgetary. An AI suite oversold ships active substitution engines — unguardrailed tutoring, one-click writing generation, instant summarization — into every classroom simultaneously, as defaults. The infrastructure decision quietly makes a hundred pedagogical decisions, each belonging to a different chapter of this book, none of them actually made by anyone.

The bundle is not a discount structure. It is a gate bypass.

## What Happened in Owen's District

Owen Marsh's recommendation memo ran two pages, and its first line did the work: *We are being asked one question that is actually two — a utility renewal and a pedagogy adoption — and I recommend we answer them separately, because they have different standards of proof.*

The district renewed the device and connectivity layer on a pure utility evaluation — four years of ticket data, a coverage audit, total cost per functioning student-device against two competitors — the way it renews a bus contract. The access audit Owen's team ran became unexpectedly the renewal's best artifact: it found four hundred students whose district device was functionally dead, and a home-bandwidth gap concentrated in two attendance zones. The savings from declining the suite's first year funded the fix. Infrastructure, judged as infrastructure, improved as infrastructure.

The suite nearly came in anyway through the side door bundles are built for: the vendor offered the AI components "free for year one" rather than lose the device contract, and the curriculum office — under genuine pressure from teachers already improvising with consumer chatbots — wanted to say yes to everything at once. The meeting turned on one exchange. The vendor's representative, pressed for evidence on the embedded tutor, reached for the slide: *technology in schools, d = 0.47.* The curriculum director asked one question:

"Is that number from studies of this product, this product category, or any product using a language model?"

The answer, honestly given, was no, no, and no.

The suite went to pilot instead of district-wide default. The tutor ran under Chapter 21's guardrail conditions in two schools. The writing assistant was held entirely pending the writing program's authorship-gate work. Auto-grading configured under Chapter 31's teacher-review gate.

Adoption conditions written into the framework: devices and connectivity renewed on the utility standard, with the access audit institutionalized as an annual census. The AI suite unbundled contractually — no component activates by default; each enters classrooms only through its own influence's phase gate, piloted with pre-registered evidence plans. The d = 0.47 slide formally retired from district procurement by a one-line policy: *aggregate technology effect sizes are not admissible evidence for specific products.* Displacement reporting added to the analytics contract: the district now asks its own dashboard, quarterly, what platform time is replacing.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** operate and optimize the infrastructure layer, run the access and reliability census, measure usage and displacement honestly, and support the unbundling analysis that routes each embedded pedagogical tool to its own influence's gate.

**The humans must** make the pedagogy decisions the bundle would otherwise make by default — component by component, chapter by chapter, with named owners — and hold the line that no aggregate technology number ever stands in for a product evaluation.

**The institution must** keep two ledgers: infrastructure judged on access, reliability, and cost; pedagogy judged on mechanism and learning evidence — and refuse any contract that forces the ledgers to merge.

**Evidence that the spending worked is,** for the infrastructure ledger: access gaps closed, uptime, cost per functioning device, accessibility compliance — utility metrics, honestly sufficient. For the pedagogy ledger: each activated component's own evidence, from its own chapter — delayed, unassisted, transfer-bearing performance, defined before activation. Never device counts, deployment speed, platform engagement, or an aggregate effect size assembled before the product category existed.

## What Evidence to Demand

The two-ledger split. Which line items are infrastructure (utility standard) and which are pedagogy (learning standard)? A proposal that cannot be split is a proposal to bypass every gate in this book at once.

Citation provenance. For every research number in the deck: was it measured on this product, this category, or this century's technology? The three-question test — this product, this category, any LLM-era product — applied to every number before the meeting ends.

The access census. Who gains what capability they lacked? Infrastructure's honest value is here, and a vendor with a strong access story should be made to tell it as an access story, not an achievement story.

The displacement account. What does the proposal's usage model replace — instructional minutes, teacher attention, budget lines — and who signed off on the trade? The OECD heavy-user curve is what unexamined displacement looks like at national scale.

Default-state inventory. List every capability that activates by default across every classroom on day one. Each item is a pedagogical decision; each needs an owner and a gate, or it needs to ship disabled.

What does not count: devices deployed, rollout speed, login rates, teacher enthusiasm in week two, vendor case studies from unnamed districts, or d = 0.47.

## What Would Change My Mind

The infrastructure/pedagogy distinction would need rebuilding if the integration itself turned out to carry learning value — if randomized comparisons showed bundled, default-on AI suites outperforming the same components adopted selectively through individual gates, because coherence, ubiquity, and zero-friction access beat governance. Owen's three decommissioned revolutions argue otherwise, but the LLM era's tools differ in kind from whiteboards, and "the gates cost more learning than they protect" is an empirical claim someone will eventually test. Likewise, if a new generation of technology meta-analyses — built entirely on LLM-era products, with Kraft-credible designs — converged well above the historical 0.3-to-0.5 band, the fifty-year flat trend would be broken and this chapter's deepest prior with it. The honest current state: the early generative-AI outcome literature is young, heterogeneous, and vendor-adjacent, and the flat trend has survived every previous challenger.

## Still Puzzling

The access channel is genuinely different in the AI era: a device now carries not content but capability — a tutor, a writing engine, an answer machine. Whether universal access to capability is an equity floor or a universally distributed substitution risk is the unresolved tension between this chapter and every gate in this book. It is both, and the ratio is unmeasured.

Teachers improvising with consumer chatbots while the district deliberates is the pressure that nearly activated the suite, and it names a real governance hole: the alternative to a gated bundle is not no AI. It is ungoverned AI on personal accounts. Whether slow unbundled adoption beats fast bundled adoption in practice depends on that counterfactual, and no one has good data on it.

Displacement remains the channel nobody instruments. The tooling now exists to measure what platform time replaces; the institutional appetite to know is another matter. The first district to publish an honest displacement account will teach the field more than the next dozen efficacy studies.

## Further Reading

**OECD (2015). *Students, Computers and Learning: Making the Connection*. PISA, OECD Publishing.** The system-level null: heavy national ICT investment, no PISA improvement, and the heavy-user curve that gives the displacement channel its data.

**Cuban, L. (2001). *Oversold and Underused: Computers in the Classroom*. Harvard University Press.** The pattern's historian — and *Teachers and Machines* (1986) for the film-radio-television prehistory that makes the fifty-year flat trend a century-long one.

**Zheng, B., Warschauer, M., Lin, C.-H., & Chang, C. (2016). Learning in one-to-one laptop environments: A meta-analysis and research synthesis. *Review of Educational Research*, 86(4), 1052–1084.** The fairest research cousin of a device lease: modest, real, writing-tilted effects — the honest ceiling for what hardware alone has ever delivered.

**Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The ruler that prices every claim in this chapter's procurement scenes.

**U.S. Department of Education (2023). *Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning*.** The policy frame for the unbundling move: human-in-the-loop adoption, component-level evaluation, and the refusal to treat "AI" as a single thing to be for or against.
