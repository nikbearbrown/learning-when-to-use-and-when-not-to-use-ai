# Chapter 9 — G08: AI as Expert Task Analyst

## The Decision on the Table

Marcus Webb directs the career and technical education programs at a regional technical high school — automotive, welding, HVAC, certified nursing assistant — and his best instructor is also his hardest problem. Ray Delgado, the welding instructor, holds thirty years of trade mastery and cannot say where most of it lives. Watch him set up a vertical weld and he makes forty decisions; ask him to list them and he names six, because the other thirty-four stopped being decisions decades ago and became hands. His students learn whatever survives the translation: the strong ones reconstruct the missing steps by trial and scrap metal; the weak ones fail in ways Ray finds mysterious, because the step they missed is one he no longer knows he takes.

Ray retires in eighteen months. Marcus's decision wears two faces. A curriculum consultant quotes four months and a five-figure sum to run formal cognitive task analysis interviews with Ray and write the welding program's skill progressions. And Marcus's instructional designer has just shown him something unsettling: an afternoon with a frontier LLM produced a draft decomposition of vertical-position arc welding — prerequisite chains, decision points, common novice errors, practice sequence — that looks, to Marcus's half-trained eye, remarkably like what the consultant sells. The designer's question is the chapter's: is this the consultant's deliverable at one percent of the price, or a fluent imitation of expertise with hallucinated specs where the knowledge should be — and how would we tell?

## What This Group Is

Four influences: **High band**, AI-AUGMENTED, medium human irreducibility, **low substitution risk, no cognitive-work conflict**. Effect sizes 0.72 to 1.29 on the 2018 252-influence list, weighted average 1.07 — the highest-band group in the book that carries *neither* a conflict flag nor elevated substitution risk.

That combination is nearly unique in the dataset. Chapter 5's high-band group came wrapped in partial conflict; the high-band groups ahead (feedback, discussion, collective efficacy) come wrapped in worse. G08 is the rare shelf where enormous effect sizes sit next to a genuinely permissive classification, and the reason is structural. These influences run on *analysis of the learning task itself*: decomposing expertise, matching tasks to developmental readiness, mapping and confronting misconceptions, lowering the cost of asking for help. The cognitive work being supported here is mostly the **teacher's design work and the system's diagnostic work** — and unlike Chapter 8's live-delivery half, this design work benefits from exactly what LLMs are: vast, fast, pattern-rich decomposition engines with no ego about being corrected. If the book has a chapter where the answer leans *yes, use it, substantially*, this is it. The discipline is that the yes comes with one non-negotiable clause, and the clause is the chapter.

## The Influences

**Cognitive task analysis (d = 1.29)** — rank 4 of 252. CTA is a family of interview and observation methods for extracting what experts actually do — including, crucially, what they no longer know they do. Its premise is a well-replicated embarrassment in expertise research: experts' self-reports omit large fractions of the decision steps novices need — figures around seventy percent of critical decision points appear in the CTA literature [verify — the canonical claim traces to Clark and colleagues' reviews; confirm provenance before quoting]. Instruction built from CTA-extracted models routinely beats instruction built from expert free recall. Two honesty notes travel with the 1.29: the meta-analytic base is small and leans on military, medical, and technical training rather than K-12 classrooms [verify], and what the number rewards is *better instructional input*, not a classroom technique — which is exactly why it lands in a CTE chapter.

**Piagetian programs (d = 1.28).** The group's strange passenger. The headline number derives substantially from work correlating children's Piagetian developmental-stage measures with achievement — closer to a measurement finding (stage assessments predict performance) than to a program effect [contested — treat this row as part context-variable, Chapter 6's grammar, rather than a purchasable intervention]. The durable practical content: tasks matched to a learner's demonstrated readiness outperform tasks matched to their grade label, and the dataset notes AI can support stage-matched task design while the developmental progression itself is biologically paced.

**Conceptual change programs (d = 0.99).** Instruction that surfaces a learner's existing misconception, confronts it with discrepant evidence, and supports rebuilding — because misconceptions are not absences of knowledge but rival theories that fight back. The dataset's split: AI misconception detection in student work is legitimate; deep schema restructuring benefits from teacher mediation.

**Help seeking (d = 0.72).** The learner-side row: students who seek help adaptively learn more. The mechanism has a known enemy — social cost. Asking reveals ignorance to people whose regard you want. The dataset's note: AI can lower the threshold (anonymous questions, friction-free access); the psychological safety that makes help-seeking habitual lives in human classroom climate.

## Why It Works

One mechanism unifies the four rows: **learning accelerates when instruction is built from an accurate model of the task and of the learner's current head.** Most instruction is built from neither — it is built from the expert's compressed memory of a path she no longer walks, delivered to a learner whose actual prior knowledge is guessed at. Each row repairs part of that double blindness. CTA repairs the task model: it reconstructs the forty decisions, so the curriculum teaches what experts do rather than what they remember doing. Piagetian matching repairs the readiness model: tasks meet the learner where the learner demonstrably is. Conceptual change repairs the prior-knowledge model: it treats the learner's existing theory as the object of instruction rather than an empty space. Help seeking is the same repair run by the learner personally — the question is a self-issued diagnostic, the fastest possible alignment of instruction to need.

Why this group tolerates AI so well now follows from the mechanism. The work being accelerated is **model-building about tasks and minds** — decomposition, pattern-matching across thousands of described performances, hypothesis generation about where novices break. That is teacher-side, design-time cognition, and the learner's effortful work (practicing the decomposed skill, fighting through the discrepant event, formulating the question) remains fully intact downstream. No learner act is displaced; no live human event is simulated. The structure/effort separability of Chapter 3 holds here at the high band.

And the same mechanism dictates the non-negotiable clause. A task model is only as good as its correspondence to the real task — and an LLM's decomposition is a synthesis of how tasks like this are *described in text*, not an observation of Ray's hands. Where the textual record is rich and consistent, the draft will be strong; where the craft knowledge is tacit, local, or safety-critical, the model will fill gaps with plausible interpolation, indistinguishable in fluency from the parts it got right. The failure mode of an expert task analyst is visible incompetence; the failure mode of an artificial one is *convincing* incompetence. Hence the dataset's phrasing, the strongest it uses anywhere in an endorsement: expert validation is **non-negotiable**.

## What AI Can Safely Do

- **Draft the decomposition.** Generate first-pass task hierarchies, prerequisite chains, decision-point inventories, and "where novices fail" predictions for any teachable skill — the consultant's scaffolding at conversational speed. The draft's correct role: an interview instrument, not a deliverable.
- **Interview the expert better.** Use the draft to drive elicitation: walk Ray through the AI's forty-step model and ask what is wrong, missing, or dangerously oversimplified. Experts are dramatically better at *correcting* a concrete model than at free-recalling their own — the draft converts an extraction problem into an editing problem, which is the single biggest methodological gift AI brings to this group.
- **Map misconceptions at scale.** Read student work — written explanations, shop diagnostics, CNA scenario responses — and flag the rival theory underneath the error; assemble discrepant cases and contrasting examples for the teacher to deploy. Detection scales; confrontation and rebuilding stay mediated.
- **Match tasks to demonstrated readiness.** Sequence practice against what the learner has actually shown, not their cohort label — the legitimate core of the Piagetian row.
- **Lower the asking threshold.** An always-available, judgment-free channel for the questions students will not raise a hand for — anonymous queues, AI first-responders that *route upward*, peer-matching. With the design constraint the trap section sharpens: the channel must coach the question, not vend the answer.

The book's classification hypothesizes the group retains most of its value under AI support — d ≈ 1.00 [HYPOTHESIS] for AI-drafted, expert-validated CTA — the appendix's largest hypothesized figure, and still only a structured conjecture flagged as such.

## The Substitution Trap

**The unvalidated decomposition.** The trap Marcus is staring at: shipping the afternoon draft as the curriculum. In CTE the cost is not abstract — a hallucinated parameter or a missing safety step in a welding progression is a burn, a failed certification, a lawsuit. But the trap's deep version is subtler than error: an LLM decomposition systematically captures the *textbook-consensus* version of a skill and silently omits the local, tacit, exception-handling knowledge that is precisely what CTA exists to extract — the thirty-four decisions in Ray's hands that never made it into any manual. Ship the draft and you have automated the omission problem CTA was invented to solve, with better formatting. The fluency is the camouflage; validation is not a quality step, it is the method.

**The answer machine in help-seeking clothes.** Help seeking splits into two behaviors the research has always distinguished: *adaptive* (seeking hints, explanations, the next step — help that keeps you the solver) and *executive* (seeking the answer — help that replaces you). An unguardrailed AI channel is the most efficient executive-help dispenser ever built, and Bastani's field experiment is the receipt: unrestricted GPT access lifted practice performance 48 percent and left students 17 percent *worse* unaided, while the guardrailed tutor — hints, no answers — avoided the harm. Lowering the threshold is only a gift if what is behind the threshold is adaptive. The same intuition runs the broader tutoring literature: VanLehn's review put human tutoring at 0.79 and step-based intelligent tutoring at a statistically comparable 0.76 — the effect lives in *coached stepping*, not in answer delivery.

**The retirement substitute.** The institutional version: treating the AI-plus-validation product as a reason Ray's replacement needs less expertise, or using the extracted model as cover for not rebuilding human mastery in the program. The decomposition preserves what Ray knows; it does not perform it, demonstrate it under failure, or model the professional identity students apprentice into. Knowledge capture is succession *support*, not succession.

## Case Study

Marcus Webb hired neither the consultant's full package nor the designer's afternoon draft. He bought the validation method and ran the drafting in-house.

**The tool.** A frontier LLM plus a structured elicitation protocol the consultant licensed in a two-day training instead of a four-month engagement. **The influence.** Cognitive task analysis, d = 1.29 — presented to his advisory board with the provenance caveats attached: small meta-analytic base, training-context evidence, and a number that rewards better inputs, not magic.

**The useful moment.** The hybrid workflow outran both pure options. The LLM drafted decompositions for the welding program's twelve core competencies in a week — work the consultant had quoted a month for. Then the drafts met Ray, and the meetings became the best PD the department had ever accidentally run. Confronted with the AI's eleven-step model for vertical-up root passes, Ray produced the response the method counts on — *"No. Nobody sets amperage before checking the fit-up gap; the gap tells you the amperage"* — and four such corrections per competency, the tacit thirty-four surfacing because there was finally a concrete wrong thing to correct. The designer logged every correction as a delta between textual consensus and Ray's mastery: the curriculum's most valuable column. Two genuine AI catches went the other way — novice-error patterns from the wider literature that Ray had stopped noticing he compensates for. The validated progressions then powered readiness-matched practice sequences and misconception-keyed shop checklists.

**The dangerous moment.** Midway through, the program's new AI help channel — meant to lower the asking threshold for students embarrassed to flag down Ray — was discovered doing executive duty: students photographing failed welds and receiving full diagnoses, parameter corrections included. Practice welds improved; the first proctored certification mock collapsed, students unable to diagnose unaided the same failures they had been photographing for weeks — the Bastani curve drawn in filler metal. Separately and worse: one unvalidated draft (HVAC, whose veteran had already retired) had quietly entered use with a refrigerant-handling sequence the certifying examiner flagged as out of order against EPA procedure. No one was hurt; the lesson was priced anyway. Drafts without a validating expert are not drafts; they are liabilities with page numbers.

**The decision.** Adopt the hybrid CTA workflow program-wide, under conditions: (1) no decomposition touches students until a credentialed practitioner has corrected it line by line and signed it — the signature is a professional attestation, archived with the curriculum; (2) safety-critical sequences get *second* validation against the certifying body's standards; (3) the help channel is rebuilt on hint-ladder guardrails — it may name the flaw's category and coach the next diagnostic step, never deliver the diagnosis — and Ray's apprenticeship norm ("bring me your weld and your theory of what went wrong") is taught as the program's help-seeking model; (4) the validation deltas — everything the experts corrected — are maintained as a living document, because they are the program's map of exactly what its AI cannot know; (5) for programs without a resident expert, drafts wait: the method's bottleneck is the validator, and the bottleneck is the point.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** draft task decompositions, prerequisite maps, and novice-error predictions; structure expert-validation interviews; detect misconceptions in student work and assemble discrepant cases; sequence practice to demonstrated readiness; and staff a guardrailed, hint-only help channel that routes hard cases to humans.

**The expert must** validate every decomposition line by line and sign it; **the teacher must** mediate conceptual confrontation and maintain the classroom safety that makes asking cheap; **the learner must** practice the decomposed skill, fight through the discrepant evidence, and formulate their own questions and failure theories — the downstream effort the upstream analysis exists to aim.

**Evidence that learning improved is** unaided performance on the whole task — certifications, proctored demonstrations, transfer problems — improving against the pre-CTA baseline, plus help-seeking shifting measurably from answer-requests toward process-questions.

## The Evidence Check

For any tool or workflow claiming this group:

1. **Whole-task unaided performance.** Certification pass rates, proctored practical exams, novel-fault diagnosis without the channel — against historical baseline. Decomposition is only as good as the recomposed skill.
2. **The validation delta log.** Demand to see what experts corrected in the AI drafts. A vendor or team that cannot produce deltas either had a perfect model (no) or never validated (almost certainly). Delta volume per domain is also your hallucination-risk map.
3. **Help-channel telemetry, classified.** Adaptive versus executive: what fraction of interactions delivered answers versus coached steps? Pair with the Bastani check — assisted practice scores against unaided assessment, divergence watched explicitly.
4. **Readiness-match audit.** Are sequencing decisions driven by demonstrated performance or by cohort defaults with adaptive branding? Sample actual student paths.

What does not count: decomposition page counts, the draft's expert-impressing fluency, help-channel volume or satisfaction, time saved versus consultants (until validated), or practice-task scores with the channel live.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Choose one task-analysis-adjacent practice in your setting — curriculum mapping, skill progressions, a tutoring or help system. One sentence each: the mechanism (instruction built from accurate task and learner models), the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution, and the proof that learning improved.
2. **Run the validation experiment.** Pick one skill you genuinely hold — teaching a lab procedure, coaching an essay, your sport, your craft. Prompt an LLM for a complete novice decomposition. Then correct it line by line and count your deltas: the steps it missed, the order it got wrong, the judgment calls it flattened. Your delta count is the chapter's argument, measured on yourself — and your corrected document is better than either input alone.
3. **Produce the help-channel spec.** Draft a one-page requirements document for an adaptive-only AI help channel in your context: the hint ladder (category → principle → next step → worked parallel — never the answer), the routing rule to humans, the telemetry classification, and the unaided evidence check. Trade with a colleague and red-team each other's spec for the executive-help workaround a struggling student would find first.

## What Would Change My Mind

The chapter's spine is the validation clause, and it rests on an empirical claim: that LLM decompositions of skilled tasks contain expert-detectable errors and omissions at rates that matter. That claim has a shelf life. If systematic studies showed frontier-model decompositions matching validated-CTA quality across domains — expert deltas approaching zero outside the truly tacit crafts — the clause would relax from "validate everything" to "validate the tacit and the dangerous," and the group would drift toward AI-NATIVE. The permissive reading dies a different way: if guardrailed help channels in real deployments drift executive anyway — students engineering answer extraction past any hint ladder, the Bastani gap appearing despite the spec — then help seeking belongs with the danger-zone groups, and this chapter's friendliest row was a misclassification I co-signed.

## Still Puzzling

- The validation bottleneck has economics nobody has worked out: the method's cost collapses except for the expert hours, which means programs *without* resident experts — precisely the under-resourced ones — benefit least from the cost collapse. The tool is cheapest where it was already affordable.
- Piagetian programs at 1.28 remains the group's unresolved row: if it is substantially a measurement correlation, its presence inflates the group average and its "program" framing misleads. It may belong, in spirit, with Chapter 6's context variables — the classification keeps it here, and I keep my doubts on the record.
- When the AI interviews the expert directly — elicitation chatbots are already being piloted — who validates the interview? The method's safeguard assumes a human asking the human; recursive automation of the extraction step has no evidence base at all yet.
