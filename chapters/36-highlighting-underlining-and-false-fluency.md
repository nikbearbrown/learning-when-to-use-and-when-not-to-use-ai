Two documents land on Dale Brogan's desk in the same month and point in opposite directions.

The first is the college's new e-textbook platform announcement. The upgraded reader includes **AI Smart Highlights** — the system pre-marks the key sentences of every chapter, in three colors, keyed to learning objectives, "so students can focus on understanding instead of hunting."

The textbook-reading module has taught the same routine for a decade: preview, read with a highlighter, review your marks before the quiz. Students like it. A marked-up chapter feels like money in the bank.

Hattie's list says d = 0.50. Dunlosky's panel says low utility. The AI says let me do it for you. Sorting out who is right about what — and discovering that the answer routes through the same mechanism in all three cases — is this chapter's work.

It also produces the sharpest statement in this book about what an AI feature can destroy: the one small, valuable thing a weak technique contained.

## The Influence — and the Tension

**Underlining and highlighting (d = 0.50) — rank 82.** The Dunlosky tension belongs on the table immediately, because both citations are real and the apparent contradiction resolves only at the mechanism level.

Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, and Willingham (2013), in *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, evaluated ten learning techniques against rigorous criteria — durability, generalizability, evidence quality — and rated highlighting and underlining **low utility**: in most studies, marking text produced little or no benefit over plain reading, and one study found marking may impair inference-making by chopping attention into isolated fragments. Practice testing and distributed practice took the top ratings. The marker took the floor.

How does the same literature yield Hattie's 0.50? Three honest answers. First, aggregation: Hattie's entry folds underlining and highlighting into a broader band of study-skills meta-analyses, where marking is often confounded with the active reading instruction it travels with — the number may describe the package, not the marker [verify — the precise meta-analytic composition of this entry is worth confirming against the 2018 sources before quoting in a procurement argument]. Second, moderators: the experimental literature contains a consistent bright spot — marking helps when it is *selective and effortful*. Restrict learners to marking one sentence per paragraph and benefits appear; let them paint freely and benefits vanish. Third, the two rulers from Chapter 1: a d assembled from short studies with proximal measures is not a verdict, and neither is a utility rating built on different inclusion criteria. They are answering different questions.

The reconciliation is not a compromise. It is a mechanism claim: **highlighting works exactly to the degree that it forces discrimination, and not at all when it doesn't.** Both literatures agree. The disagreement was only ever about how often real-world highlighting forces discrimination — and the answer, untrained and unguided, is rarely.

## Why It Works: Discrimination Is Comprehension's Tell

When highlighting works, it works because it is a forced-choice act of **selection** — the same operation traced through summarizing (Chapter 28), mapping (Chapter 33), and note-taking (Chapter 35), stripped here to minimum viable form.

To mark the one sentence that matters in a paragraph, you must judge the paragraph's architecture: claim or support, principle or example. The judgment is the learning event. The mark is a byproduct — the cheapest exhaust artifact in this entire book.

Constraint creates the value. An unlimited highlighter requires no choices, and a marked-everything page encodes none. The experimental bright spots all share one design feature: scarcity. One sentence per paragraph. Three marks per page. Choose, and by choosing, process.

And here the second mechanism matters, because highlighting's failure mode is among the most instructive in the study-skills literature. Marked text is *easier to reread*, and ease of processing is what the cognitive system uses as its signal of knowing. Koriat and Bjork (2005) named these **illusions of competence**: the learner's judgment of learning is driven by fluency at study time, which is at best weakly related to retrieval strength later. Rereading one's highlights is a fluency machine — each pass makes the text smoother, the smoothness reads as mastery, and the student walks into the exam having repeatedly experienced the *feeling* of knowing without once testing the *fact* of it.

This is why the marker rates low while feeling indispensable. It manufactures its own evidence of effectiveness, in yellow.

Now the AI Smart Highlights feature can be assessed in one move. Pre-marked text removes the discrimination (the technique's only mechanism) and ships the fluency illusion pre-installed (the technique's only consistent failure mode). It subtracts the engine and keeps the exhaust. It is the only feature in this book that automates only the harm.

## What AI Can Safely Do

Small influence, but a useful support surface — every entry pointed at the discrimination, none at the marks.

**Critique the student's marking.** After a student highlights under constraint, AI compares their selections against the passage's actual argument structure: "You marked three examples and no claims — find the sentence the examples exist to support." Criteria-based feedback on selection quality, at a scale no seminar instructor grading two thousand marked chapters could approach.

**Enforce scarcity.** A reader configured to allow three highlights per page, with a forced one-line "why this sentence" annotation, converts the marker from paint roller to forced choice. This is the rare case where the AI-era version of a tool can be *more* mechanism-faithful than paper.

**Convert marks into retrieval.** Take the student's own highlights and generate closed-book prompts from them the next day: "You marked a sentence about enzyme specificity — state the point from memory." This design move drains the fluency illusion — rereading is replaced by retrieval — while honoring the student's selections as the syllabus of their own gaps.

**Generate contrast markings for diagnosis.** Two pre-marked versions of the same passage — one marking claims, one marking vivid trivia — for students to judge and defend. The student evaluating someone else's discrimination is performing discrimination.

**Teach the upgrade path.** The honest endpoint: highlighting as the entry ramp to stronger techniques. Marks become marginal questions become closed-book self-tests — the Dunlosky-consistent trajectory from low-utility to high-utility behavior.

## The Substitution Trap: Quiet, Cheap, and Already Deployed

Smart Highlights ships enabled. The student opens the chapter and the choosing has been done — accurately, which is the problem. Reading pre-marked text trains exactly one behavior: attending to what an authority flagged. It is reading with the answers shown, forever.

What makes this trap worth its own chapter is its *invisibility multiplied by its install base.* An AI-written essay is a discrete act a student chooses; pre-highlighted text is an ambient condition of the reading environment, chosen by no one, noticed by no one, marketed as a reading aid. No student will ever report having used it, because using it requires nothing.

Its costs are perfectly camouflaged: the marked sentences *are* the important ones, so quiz performance on pre-marked content may hold steady while the capacity being silently un-trained — judging importance in unmarked text — never appears on any dashboard until the student meets a document the AI has not pre-chewed: a primary source, a contract, a ballot measure, a search result.

Discrimination of importance is not a study skill. It is the adult competence the study skill was supposed to be rehearsing.

The fluency amplifier completes the trap. AI-marked text feels maximally fluent — professionally selected, color-coded, objectively authoritative. Every reread smooths it further. The student's judgment of learning inflates on schedule, and the first unassisted encounter with unmarked text supplies the correction at full price, at the worst time.

## One Seminar, One Toggle, One Audit

Dale began by conceding the new instructor's point in public: the seminar had been teaching the low-utility version, and the memo was the best thing to happen to the course in years.

The redesigned module ran on scarcity and retrieval. Students read with a hard three-highlights-per-page limit, each mark requiring a one-line justification; the platform's AI critiqued selections against passage structure overnight — gently, and in the aggregate, giving each instructor a section-level report of selection quality. Twice a week, students faced closed-book prompts generated from their own marks. The contrast exercise became a section favorite: two pre-marked versions of the same anatomy passage, one marking the argument, one marking the memorable trivia, argued over in pairs.

Mid-semester, the platform reports showed the thing Dale had never been able to measure on paper: selection quality rising across ten weeks — students' marks migrating from vivid details toward structural claims [pilot-internal observation; interpret cautiously].

The dangerous moment arrived as a display setting. Three weeks in, an instructor noticed that a third of her section's justification lines read like paraphrases of the AI's own selections — students had found that the pre-marks were visible in the default view and were confirming them rather than making independent choices. Students with pre-marks visible converged on the AI's selections; justification quality flattened. The discrimination practice the whole redesign existed to force was being quietly pre-empted by a toggle.

The fix was one toggle. The lesson was bigger: *the default state of the reading environment is now a pedagogical decision, whether or not anyone makes it.*

**The decision.** Disable Smart Highlights at the institutional level; the pre-marked view available only as a post-hoc comparison layer after students submit their own marks — the answer-key reposition, for the third time in this book. Scarcity limits, justification lines, and retrieval-from-marks stay in the seminar permanently. The seminar's catalog description changed one phrase: "effective highlighting techniques" became "judging what matters in a text." The spring evidence check: students mark and justify selections in a novel, unmarked passage under supervision, scored against the passage's argument structure, fall versus spring.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** critique student selections against text structure, enforce scarcity and justification in the reading environment, generate retrieval prompts from the student's own marks, produce contrast markings for diagnostic argument, and reveal its own marking only after the student's is committed.

**The learner must** perform every act of selection on text that counts — choose the few sentences, defend the choices, and retrieve the marked content from memory later — because the discrimination is the entire mechanism and the marks are worthless without it.

**The instructor must** treat selection quality as the taught and assessed skill, and own the upgrade path from marking toward self-testing.

**Evidence that learning improved is** selection quality on novel unmarked text improving over the term, retrieval performance on self-marked content, and students' judgments of learning converging toward their measured performance — the fluency illusion shrinking on the record. Never the volume of highlighting, the coverage of marks, or performance on text the AI had pre-marked.

---

## The Evidence Check

Five demands for any reading platform or study-skills curriculum touching highlighting.

**Novel-text discrimination.** Supervised marking of an unmarked, unfamiliar passage, scored against its argument structure — the only direct measure of the capacity this technique exists to build, and the one pre-marking silently un-trains.

**The calibration gap.** Judgments of learning collected before quizzes, compared against quiz results. A program that shrinks the gap is defeating the fluency illusion; one that widens it is automating it.

**Retrieval from marks.** Performance on closed-book prompts built from students' own selections — the test of whether marking is feeding study or replacing it.

**The default audit.** Is pre-marking on or off, institution-wide, and who decided? If nobody decided, the vendor did.

**Convergence monitoring.** Where pre-marks are ever visible, measure how fast student selections converge on the AI's. Rapid convergence with flattening justification quality is the discrimination being pre-empted, in real time, in the logs.

What does not count: highlights per chapter, time-in-reader, quiz scores on pre-marked content, or how thoroughly the class's shared annotations cover the textbook.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that reading AI pre-marked text produces equal or better *novel-text* importance-judgment than constrained self-marking would dissolve this chapter's conflict flag — perhaps expert marking functions as a worked example of discrimination, modeling the skill rather than replacing it, with the modeling benefit outrunning the practice cost. The worked-example literature makes this less absurd than it sounds, at least for true novices on unfamiliar genres. If a fading design — pre-marks rich in week one, gone by week six — showed durable discrimination gains over practice-only conditions, this chapter would rewrite its trap as a scaffold mismanaged rather than a mechanism deleted. Nobody has run that study. Until someone does, the default-on, never-fading version that actually ships earns the classification it has.

## Still Puzzling

- The Hattie–Dunlosky tension is resolvable at the mechanism level, but it leaves a meta-question this book keeps meeting: when an influence's d describes a *package* (marking plus the strategy instruction around it), what exactly is a vendor citing when they cite the number? The aggregation problem from Chapter 1, in miniature.
- Highlighting may matter mostly as a gateway — the first active-reading behavior weak readers will actually adopt, valuable as the on-ramp to self-testing. If so, the right metric is not the technique's own effect but its conversion rate into stronger techniques, and nobody measures that.
- The calibration question generalizes beyond this chapter and deserves its own research program: can AI, properly aimed, *reduce* illusions of competence — by forcing retrieval, surfacing prediction-performance gaps, withholding fluency? A technology that mostly manufactures false fluency being turned into the first scaled instrument for measuring it would be a fine irony, and a better product category.
