# Chapter 12 — Cooperation Is the Intervention
*What the Spreadsheet Prices and What It Cannot See.*

Peer tutoring, cooperative versus competitive learning, small group learning, cooperative learning: four influences from 0.40 to 0.53 whose active ingredient is students working on and with each other. In Hattie's synthesis the cooperation is not the delivery format of the intervention; it is the intervention. That makes this AI-resistant territory of a specific kind: a model can organize the groups, prepare the materials, even coach the tutors — but the moment it replaces a peer, the mechanism is gone, because the value ran through a human being explaining to another human being with something at stake.

The budget analyst's note was not hostile. It was arithmetic.

"AI tutor: $11 per student per year. Peer program: stipends, coordinator time, training days. Recommend consolidation."

Aisha Roundtree had built the peer-tutoring center at Westbrook High from a storage room and six volunteers into the school's busiest academic space: forty trained student tutors, two hundred regulars, a waiting list on exam weeks. She had also read enough to know the analyst wasn't wrong about the chatbot — the research on intelligent tutoring is genuinely strong, and the bot would probably help the students who used it well. The number the note couldn't see was what else the center was producing. Half the learning never touches the tutee. It happens to the *tutors* — the B-student who finally understood slope the third time she had to explain it to someone else, the senior whose attendance recovered because Tuesday afternoons, two sophomores were counting on him.

The spreadsheet priced the explanation-delivery service. It could not price the rest of the machine.

Her job for Thursday's budget meeting: explain, in evidence language, what exactly would be consolidated away.

## What the Classification Means

Four influences: above the hinge, AI-RESISTANT, high human irreducibility, moderate substitution risk, cognitive-work conflict present. Effect sizes 0.40 to 0.53 on the 2018 252-influence list, weighted average 0.48. [verify against MetaX near publication]

AI-resistant does not mean AI-useless. It means the active ingredient is a human social mechanism — peer exchange, interdependence, group accountability — that AI cannot perform, only support from outside or imitate from inside. The conflict flag marks the imitation problem: a chatbot that plays the role of a peer is not a worse peer. It is a different intervention entirely, one that happens to share a transcript format with the real thing.

The moderate substitution risk is the spreadsheet's risk. Not students sneaking answers — the silent substitution of earlier chapters — but institutions consolidating line items, replacing a social structure with a service because the two produce similar-looking sessions at very different prices. The substitution is rational, visible, and approved in meetings, which makes it more dangerous than the silent kind, not less.

## The Four Rows

Peer tutoring (d = 0.53) — rank 71 of 252. Structured programs in which students teach students. The finding the budget spreadsheet misses is the oldest in this literature: tutoring benefits flow to *both* roles, and the tutor's gains — from organizing, explaining, fielding confusion, and being responsible for another person's understanding — are frequently comparable to the tutee's. The dataset's hypothesis for AI-replacement is blunt: with the peer exchange removed, almost nothing of the influence remains — d ≈ 0.10 [HYPOTHESIS], logistics-level support only.

Cooperative versus competitive learning (d = 0.53) — rank 72 — and cooperative versus individualistic learning (d = 0.55, from the companion row). These are comparative influences: the d measures cooperation's edge over competition and solo work, grounded in social interdependence theory — Johnson and Johnson's long program on what happens when my success requires yours. Positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face promotive interaction: the named ingredients are all relational. Hypothesis d ≈ 0.10 [HYPOTHESIS] under AI replacement.

Small group learning (d = 0.47) — rank 92. Effects depend on the group being a genuine unit — shared goals, mutual accountability — rather than four students seated together working alone. The dataset flags AI facilitation of group interaction as degradation of the mechanism, not support.

Cooperative learning (d = 0.40) — rank 120. The generic row, sitting exactly on the hinge. A useful reminder that cooperation poorly implemented — no interdependence, no individual accountability — earns mediocre effects. The structure does the work, which cuts both ways: it is exactly the structure that an AI cannot inhabit and a school can fail to build.

One honesty note for the group: these are aggregations across enormously varied implementations, and the comparative rows inherit every critique of meta-meta-analysis this book has flagged. Read them as a consistent directional signal — structured cooperation reliably beats unstructured individualism — not as a promise of 0.53 from any particular Tuesday.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is consequential interdependence: one person's cognitive act mattering to another person who is actually there.

Unpack the tutor's side first, because it is the side the spreadsheet cannot see. To teach slope to a confused sophomore, the tutor must retrieve the concept, reorganize it around someone else's misunderstanding, generate new examples on the spot, and monitor in real time whether the explanation is landing — retrieval, elaboration, and metacognitive monitoring, the highest-value operations in the learning-science inventory, performed under the most motivating condition psychology knows: another person depending on you. The explanation is a by-product. The tutor's reconstruction of their own knowledge is the event.

The tutee's side has a social engine too. Explanations from a near-peer arrive in reachable language, from someone who recently held the same confusion. And asking a peer is cheaper than asking the teacher — but not free. The mild social cost is load-bearing: it keeps help-seeking deliberate, and being helped by someone who chose to show up creates an obligation to try that no software license generates. Cooperative structures generalize the same force — in a well-built group, my preparation matters because my teammates will be examined on what only I was assigned to learn.

Now the necessary complication, because this book does not wave it away. On the tutee's side alone, machine tutoring is genuinely competitive. VanLehn's review put human tutoring at d = 0.79 and step-based intelligent tutoring systems at a statistically comparable 0.76 — the folklore gap between human and machine tutoring has not survived measurement. If peer tutoring were only an explanation-delivery service, the budget analyst would be right, and this chapter would be shorter. The resistance classification rests on what else the structure produces: the tutor's learning, the obligation that gets a sophomore to show up Tuesday, the belonging that a waiting list signals. The chatbot replicates the transcript. It replicates nothing else in the machine.

## What AI Can Safely Do

Train the tutors. Role-play a confused, frustrated, or overconfident tutee for practice; coach hint-giving instead of answer-giving; drill the opening moves of a session. Rehearsal against a simulator before the real human is the legitimate version of AI-as-peer — preparation for the exchange rather than replacement of it.

Coach the tutors live and after. The strongest evidence in this lane is the Tutor CoPilot trial: AI-generated, expert-modeled suggestions delivered to human tutors mid-session moved student mastery by four percentage points overall and nine points for students of the lowest-rated tutors. AI amplifying the human exchange, not replacing it. The gain concentrated exactly where human capacity was thinnest — a design principle worth internalizing.

Run the logistics. Matching, scheduling, reminders, room assignments, waitlist triage — the coordination overhead that kills volunteer programs. This is the cleanest use in the group: pure structure, no mechanism at risk.

Prepare the material. Practice problems, worked examples, and session guides matched to what the tutee's teacher is actually covering, so sessions start with substance instead of archaeology.

Mine the records. Summarize session logs for the coordinator: which topics recur, which pairings work, which tutors need support, which tutees stop coming. Pattern detection across a hundred sessions a week that no human coordinator can hold.

Backstop the off-hours. A guardrailed practice tool at eleven at night when no humans are available — labeled explicitly as practice support, never counted as the program, and watched for substitution.

## The Three Traps

**The consolidation.** The headline trap is Aisha's budget line: replacing the social structure with the service because the sessions look alike. What is deleted is invisible on every metric the service reports — the forty tutors' own learning (the largest single casualty, appearing on no dashboard because the tutors aren't the chatbot's users), the obligation network that produced attendance, the help-seeking habits formed when asking has a face. The chatbot's usage statistics will look excellent, because usage is what it reports. The question the statistics cannot answer is what two hundred and forty students are no longer doing to each other.

**The simulated group.** The subtler trap: AI inside the cooperative structure. A chatbot as fourth group member, an AI that mediates discussion, summarizes disagreements, or smooths conflicts. Each feature removes the friction — and the friction is the curriculum. A group whose disagreements are resolved by a machine is not practicing the resolution of disagreement. Jigsaw-style designs where one expert is an AI dissolve the interdependence, because nobody's effort is actually required anymore. The conflict flag means exactly this: the tool's helpfulness and the mechanism's operation are fighting over the same square of social space.

**Cooperation-flavored individualism.** The oldest failure mode, now AI-accelerated: each student consults the chatbot separately, then the group staples the outputs. The room looks collaborative; the cognition was solitary and machine-assisted; the interdependence never fired. The group's assisted product looks better than anything they could have produced together unaided, and the gap is the learning that didn't happen between them.

## What Happened on Thursday

Aisha Roundtree walked into the budget meeting with one slide and a counter-proposal.

She proposed to keep the chatbot. And she proposed to keep the center. The question was what each one was for.

Her counter-proposal put the chatbot to work in the three places the classification permits. Tutor training first: new tutors now rehearse against the model playing a frustrated freshman — wrong in realistic ways, impatient, occasionally hostile — before facing a real one. Training days that used to consume her stipend budget run themselves. Coordination second: matching, scheduling, and session-log summaries that used to eat her Sundays. And a CoPilot-style experiment third: tutors who hit a wall mid-session can flag the coordinator's tablet, where the model drafts three possible next moves — *ask her to draw it; go back to the table of values; try the ramp example* — for the tutor to choose from. Her veteran tutors mostly ignore it. Her newest tutors call it the difference between drowning and swimming.

The near-miss came from success. Off-hours chatbot usage grew so fast that by November a dozen of the center's regulars had stopped booking sessions entirely — the machine was easier, no scheduling, no social cost, no waiting list. Two of them were students Aisha knew: the kind for whom Tuesday's obligation had been the structural thread holding a week together. Easier had quietly become alone. The chatbot had not malfunctioned. It had substituted, one convenient evening at a time, and the only metric that caught it was a human coordinator noticing absence.

The budget meeting kept both lines, re-scoped. The chatbot is infrastructure: training simulator, coordination engine, off-hours practice support — never described, funded, or measured as tutoring-program replacement. The peer program remains the program. Chatbot-heavy users who drop their human sessions get a personal nudge from a tutor, not an automated one. The CoPilot-style assist stays tutor-facing: suggestions go to the tutor, never directly to the tutee. Evidence is two-sided by design: tutee outcomes on unassisted classroom assessments, and tutor outcomes — grades, attendance, retention in the program — reported to the budget office annually as the return the $11 license cannot generate.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** train and rehearse tutors, coach them with mid-session suggestions, run matching and scheduling, prepare session materials, summarize logs for the coordinator, and provide clearly-labeled off-hours practice support.

**The students must** do the tutoring, the explaining, the asking, and the group work with each other — because the peer exchange, the interdependence, and the mutual accountability are the intervention, on both sides of the table.

**The coordinator must** keep watching for drift from supported-by to replaced-by, which no log flags automatically.

**Evidence that learning improved is** tutee performance on unassisted classroom and district measures, tutor academic trajectories and program persistence, and the health of the structure itself — sessions kept, pairs sustained, help-seeking rates. None of which is a chatbot usage statistic.

## What Evidence to Demand

Both sides of the exchange. Tutee outcomes on work the tool never assisted, and tutor outcomes — the half of the effect that consolidation proposals systematically omit. If nobody is measuring tutor learning, the program's largest asset is invisible at exactly the moment a spreadsheet prices it.

Structure metrics. Session attendance, pair persistence, voluntary return rates, waiting-list behavior. A social intervention's vital signs are social. Declining human-session bookings during rising chatbot usage is the substitution in progress.

Interdependence audit. For cooperative structures: can each member's necessary contribution be named? If the task would complete with one member and a chatbot, the design — not the students — failed first.

Transfer to the unassisted room. Performance in whole-class discussion, on individual assessments, in the next course — the downstream traces of explanations given and received by humans.

What does not count: chatbot session counts, conversation lengths, satisfaction ratings, or the polish of artifacts a group produced by stapling individual AI outputs together.

## What Would Change My Mind

Two findings would force revision. If well-designed studies showed students working with AI partners gaining as much as students in well-structured human cooperative groups — on delayed individual measures and on collaborative-skill transfer to new human groups — then the interdependence story is wrong in part, and the conflict flag is overdrawn. Nothing current shows this; VanLehn covers tutee-side content gains, not the social half. And if longitudinal data showed peer-tutoring programs' tutor-side benefits failing to replicate at scale — the protégé effect as artifact of selection, the kind of student who tutors already rising — then the spreadsheet's arithmetic improves considerably, and this chapter's strongest card weakens. I would still bet on the structure. I would bet less.

## Still Puzzling

The off-hours boundary will not stay drawn. Tonight's convenient chatbot session genuinely helps tonight; a hundred such nights quietly disassemble the program. Where the supplement becomes the substitute is a dose question nobody has data on, and "label it clearly" is a hope, not a mechanism.

Tutor CoPilot's biggest gains went to the weakest tutors — which is exactly what you want from an assist, and raises an unresolved question: does the assist build those tutors' independent skill over time, or create skilled-seeming tutors who cannot operate unassisted? The study measured students. Nobody has measured the tutors' fading curve.

For genuinely isolated learners — rural schools, homebound students, midnight workers — the human structure this chapter defends is sometimes not available at any price. What the second-best arrangement looks like, and how to keep it from becoming the default for the merely under-budgeted, is a question this book's framework can pose but local conscience has to answer.

## Further Reading

**VanLehn, K. (2011). The relative effectiveness of human tutoring, intelligent tutoring systems, and other tutoring systems. *Educational Psychologist*, 46(4), 197–221.** The 0.79/0.76 result — read here as the honest complication: machine tutoring rivals human tutoring on the tutee side, which is precisely why the rest of the mechanism has to be argued and measured.

**Wang, R., et al. (2024). Tutor CoPilot: A human-AI approach for scaling real-time expertise.** The +4/+9 percentage-point trial: AI inside the human tutoring loop, gains concentrated where tutor skill is thinnest — this chapter's design template.

**Johnson, D. W., & Johnson, R. T. (2009). An educational psychology success story: Social interdependence theory and cooperative learning. *Educational Researcher*, 38(5), 365–379.** The theory under the comparative rows: positive interdependence, individual accountability, promotive interaction — the ingredients no software inhabits.

**Bastani, H., et al. (2025). Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning: Evidence from high school mathematics. *PNAS*.** The assisted/unassisted divergence, applied here to groups: collective assisted output as the mask over absent collective cognition.

**Aronson, E., & Patnoe, S. (2011). *Cooperation in the Classroom: The Jigsaw Method* (3rd ed.).** The fullest account of engineering interdependence on purpose — and the bridge to Chapter 15, where jigsaw gets its own decision.
