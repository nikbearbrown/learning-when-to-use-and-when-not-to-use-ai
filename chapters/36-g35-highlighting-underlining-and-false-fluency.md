# Chapter 36 — G35: Highlighting, Underlining, and False Fluency

## The Decision on the Table

Dale Brogan coordinates the student success program at a community college where the most popular course he owns is a one-credit study-skills seminar taken by two thousand students a year, most of them first-generation, many returning to education after years away. The seminar's textbook-reading module has taught the same core routine for a decade: preview the chapter, read with a highlighter, review your highlights before the quiz. Students like the routine. It is concrete, it produces visible evidence of effort, and a marked-up chapter feels like money in the bank.

Two documents have landed on Dale's desk in the same month, and they point in opposite directions. The first is the college's new e-textbook platform announcement: the upgraded reader includes **AI Smart Highlights** — the system pre-marks the key sentences of every chapter, in three colors, keyed to learning objectives, "so students can focus on understanding instead of hunting." The second is a memo from a new instructor, fresh from a cognitive psychology program, citing Dunlosky and colleagues' famous review: highlighting and underlining rated among the *lowest-utility* learning techniques in the literature — and asking, reasonably, why the seminar is teaching it at all.

So Dale faces a two-front decision unusual for this book: a tool that automates a practice, and a critique that says the practice was hollow to begin with. Hattie's 2018 list says d = 0.50. Dunlosky's panel says low utility. The AI says let me do it for you. Sorting out who is right about what — and discovering that the answer routes through the same mechanism in all three cases — is this chapter's work, and it makes this small, humble influence one of the most instructive rows in the dataset.

## What This Group Is

A single influence, classified: Above hinge, AI-AUGMENTED, medium human irreducibility, **low substitution risk**, **cognitive-work conflict present**.

The odd pairing — low substitution risk *and* conflict present — deserves a careful read, because no other row in the book's tail wears it. The conflict is real: the dataset's note is unambiguous that the act of discriminating important from unimportant information is the learning process, and AI doing it removes the benefit. But the stakes are bounded, because highlighting was always a low-leverage technique — a gateway behavior, not a powerhouse. The risk is not that AI hollows out a mighty mechanism; it is that AI removes the *one valuable thing* a weak technique contained, leaving a ritual that is now pure theater. Low substitution risk, total mechanism loss. The smallest trap in the book, and the most perfectly shaped.

## The Influences

**Underlining and highlighting (d = 0.50)** — rank 82 on the 2018 252-influence list. And immediately, the tension the new instructor's memo names must be put on the table, because both citations are real.

Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan, and Willingham (2013), in *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*, evaluated ten learning techniques against rigorous criteria — durability, generalizability, evidence quality — and rated highlighting/underlining **low utility**: in most studies, marking text produced little or no benefit over plain reading, and one study even found marking impaired inference-making, perhaps by chopping attention into isolated fragments. (The same review, for what it is worth, also rated summarization low utility *for untrained students* — the resolution Chapter 28 reached by another road: the technique is the training, not the act's name.) Practice testing and distributed practice took the top ratings; the marker took the floor.

How does the same literature yield Hattie's 0.50? Three honest answers, each instructive. First, aggregation: Hattie's entry folds underlining and highlighting into a broader band of study-skills meta-analyses, where marking is often confounded with the active reading instruction it travels with — the number describes the package, not the marker [verify — the precise meta-analytic composition of this entry is worth confirming against the 2018 sources before quoting in a procurement fight]. Second, moderators: the experimental literature contains a consistent bright spot — marking helps when it is *selective and effortful*. Restrict learners to marking one sentence per paragraph and benefits appear; let them paint freely and benefits vanish. Third, the two rulers from Chapter 1: a d assembled from short studies with proximal measures is not a verdict, and neither is a utility rating built on different inclusion criteria. The two citations are answering different questions.

The reconciliation, then, is not a compromise but a mechanism claim: **highlighting works exactly to the degree that it forces discrimination, and not at all when it doesn't.** Both literatures agree on this. The disagreement was only ever about how often real-world highlighting forces discrimination — and the answer (rarely, untrained) is itself the seminar's curriculum problem.

## Why It Works

When it works, highlighting is a forced-choice act of **selection** — the same operation this book has now traced through summarizing (Chapter 28), mapping (Chapter 33), and note-taking (Chapter 35), here stripped to its minimum viable form.

**Discrimination is comprehension's tell.** To mark the one sentence that matters in a paragraph, you must judge the paragraph's architecture: claim or support, principle or example. The judgment is the learning event. The mark is a byproduct — the cheapest exhaust artifact in this entire book.

**Constraint creates the value.** An unlimited highlighter requires no choices, and a marked-everything page encodes none. The experimental bright spots all share one design feature: scarcity. One sentence per paragraph. Three marks per page. Choose, and by choosing, process.

**The trap inside the technique: fluency.** Here this chapter pays its second debt, because highlighting's failure mode is the purest specimen of an illusion that runs through the whole study-skills tail. Marked text is *easier to reread*, and ease of processing is what the cognitive system uses as its signal of knowing. Koriat and Bjork called these **illusions of competence**: the learner's judgment of learning is driven by fluency at study time, which is at best weakly related to retrieval strength later. Rereading one's highlights is a fluency machine — each pass makes the text smoother, the smoothness reads as mastery, and the student walks into the exam having repeatedly experienced the *feeling* of knowing without once testing the fact of it. This is why the marker rates low while feeling indispensable: it manufactures its own evidence of effectiveness, in yellow.

Now the AI Smart Highlights feature can be assessed in one move. Pre-marked text removes the discrimination (the technique's only mechanism) and ships the fluency illusion pre-installed (the technique's only consistent failure mode). It subtracts the engine and keeps the exhaust. It is — and this chapter says it with some admiration for the purity — the only feature in this book that automates *only* the harm.

## What AI Can Safely Do

Small influence, but a genuinely useful support surface — every entry pointed at the discrimination, none at the marks:

- **Critique the student's marking.** After a student highlights under constraint, AI compares their selections against the passage's actual argument structure: "You marked three examples and no claims — find the sentence the examples exist to support." Criteria-based feedback on selection quality, at a scale no seminar instructor grading two thousand marked chapters could approach.
- **Enforce scarcity.** A reader configured to allow three highlights per page, with a forced one-line "why this sentence" annotation, converts the marker from paint roller to forced choice. This is the rare case where the AI-era version of a tool can be *more* mechanism-faithful than paper.
- **Convert marks into retrieval.** Take the student's own highlights and generate closed-book prompts from them the next day: "You marked a sentence about enzyme specificity — state the point from memory." This single design move drains the fluency illusion (rereading is replaced by retrieval) while honoring the student's selections as the syllabus of their own gaps.
- **Generate contrast markings for diagnosis.** Two pre-marked versions of the same passage — one marking claims, one marking vivid trivia — for students to judge and defend. The student evaluating someone else's discrimination is performing discrimination.
- **Teach the upgrade path.** The honest endpoint, which AI can scaffold: highlighting as the *entry ramp* to stronger techniques — marks become marginal questions become closed-book self-tests — the Dunlosky-consistent trajectory from low-utility to high-utility behavior.

The dataset's hypothesis column reads: the student must select what to highlight, AI highlighting bypasses the discrimination process, hypothesized at d ≈ 0.25 [HYPOTHESIS] for gated use — a conjecture sized, appropriately, to the modesty of the technique itself.

## The Substitution Trap

The trap here is quiet, cheap, and already deployed. Smart Highlights ships enabled. The student opens the chapter and the choosing has been done — accurately, which is the problem. Reading pre-marked text trains exactly one behavior: attending to what an authority flagged. It is reading with the answers shown, forever.

What makes this trap worth its own chapter rather than a footnote to Chapter 28 is its *invisibility multiplied by its install base.* An AI essay is a discrete act a student chooses; pre-highlighted text is an ambient condition of the reading environment, chosen by no one, noticed by no one, marketed as a reading aid. No student will ever report having used it, because using it requires nothing. And its costs are perfectly camouflaged: the marked sentences *are* the important ones, so quiz performance on marked content may hold steady while the capacity being silently un-trained — judging importance in unmarked text — never appears on any dashboard until the student meets a document the AI has not pre-chewed: a primary source, a contract, a ballot measure, a search result. Discrimination of importance is not a study skill. It is the adult competence the study skill was supposed to be rehearsing.

The fluency amplifier completes the trap. AI-marked text feels *maximally* fluent — professionally selected, color-coded, objectively authoritative. Every reread smooths it further. The student's judgment of learning inflates on schedule, and the first unassisted encounter with unmarked text supplies the correction, at full price, at the worst time.

## Case Study

Dale Brogan answered both documents with one redesign, piloted across eight sections of the seminar — and he began, shrewdly, by conceding the new instructor's point in public: the seminar had been teaching the low-utility version, and the memo was the best thing to happen to the course in years.

**The tool.** The e-textbook platform, Smart Highlights included. **The influence.** Underlining and highlighting, d = 0.50 — presented to the seminar instructors with the Dunlosky tension unhidden, and resolved as the curriculum's new through-line: *we teach the discrimination, not the marker.*

**The useful moment.** The redesigned module ran on scarcity and retrieval. Students read with a hard three-highlights-per-page limit, each mark requiring a one-line justification; the platform's AI critiqued the selections against the passage structure overnight — gently, and in the aggregate, giving each instructor a section-level report of selection quality. Twice a week, students faced closed-book prompts generated from *their own* marks. The contrast exercise became a section favorite: two pre-marked versions of the same anatomy passage, one marking the argument, one marking the memorable trivia, argued over in pairs. Mid-semester, the platform reports showed the thing Dale had never been able to measure with paper: selection quality rising measurably across ten weeks — students' marks migrating from vivid details toward structural claims [pilot-internal observation].

**The dangerous moment.** Smart Highlights, enabled by default, had to be discovered before it could be fought. Three weeks in, an instructor noticed that a third of her section's "justification" lines read like paraphrases of the AI's own selections — students had found that the pre-marks were visible in the default view, and were simply confirming them. The behavioral data made it stark: students with pre-marks visible converged on the AI's selections and their justification quality flattened; the discrimination practice the whole redesign existed to force was being quietly pre-empted by a display setting. The fix was one toggle — pre-marks off — but the lesson Dale wrote into the final report was bigger than the toggle: *the default state of the reading environment is now a pedagogical decision, whether or not anyone makes it.*

**The decision.** Keep the platform; disable Smart Highlights at the institutional level, with the pre-marked view available only as a post-hoc comparison layer after students submit their own marks — the answer-key reposition, third time in this book. Scarcity limits, justification lines, and retrieval-from-marks stay in the seminar permanently. The seminar's catalog description changed one phrase: "effective highlighting techniques" became "judging what matters in a text." The spring evidence check: students mark and justify selections in a *novel, unmarked* passage under supervision, scored against the passage's argument structure, fall versus spring.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** critique student selections against text structure, enforce scarcity and justification in the reading environment, generate retrieval prompts from the student's own marks, produce contrast markings for diagnostic argument, and reveal its own marking only after the student's is committed.

**The learner must** perform every act of selection on text that counts — choose the few sentences, defend the choices, and retrieve the marked content from memory later — because the discrimination is the entire mechanism and the marks are worthless without it. **The instructor must** treat selection quality as the taught and assessed skill, and own the upgrade path from marking toward self-testing.

**Evidence that learning improved is** selection quality on novel unmarked text improving over the term, retrieval performance on self-marked content, and students' judgments of learning converging toward their measured performance — the fluency illusion shrinking on the record. Never the volume of highlighting, the coverage of marks, or performance on text the AI had pre-marked.

## The Evidence Check

For any reading platform or study-skills curriculum touching highlighting, demand:

1. **Novel-text discrimination.** Supervised marking of an unmarked, unfamiliar passage, scored against its argument structure — the only direct measure of the capacity this technique exists to build, and the one pre-marking silently un-trains.
2. **The calibration gap.** Judgments of learning collected before quizzes, compared against quiz results. A program that shrinks the gap between confidence and performance is defeating the fluency illusion; one that widens it is automating it.
3. **Retrieval from marks.** Performance on closed-book prompts built from students' own selections — the test of whether marking is feeding study or replacing it.
4. **The default audit.** Is pre-marking on or off, institution-wide, and who decided? If nobody decided, the vendor did.
5. **Convergence monitoring.** Where pre-marks are ever visible, measure how fast student selections converge on the AI's. Rapid convergence with flattening justification quality is the discrimination being pre-empted, in real time, in the logs.

What does not count: highlights per chapter, time-in-reader, quiz scores on pre-marked content, or how thoroughly the class's shared annotations cover the textbook.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Choose one marking-adjacent tool or routine in your setting — an e-reader's highlight layer, a PDF annotator, a "key points" feature. Write one sentence each: the mechanism (forced discrimination of importance), the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution, and the proof that learning happened.
2. **Run the scarcity test on yourself.** Take a dense article you need to learn. Mark page one freely; on page two, allow yourself exactly two marks, each with a one-line justification. Tomorrow, closed-book, write what you remember from each page. The asymmetry is the entire chapter, self-administered in twenty minutes.
3. **Audit a default.** Open whatever reading platform your institution licenses and determine: does any AI pre-marking, key-point extraction, or auto-summary display before the reader has done anything? Write down who at your institution made that choice. If the answer is "no one," draft the one-paragraph memo that makes it a decision.

## What Would Change My Mind

Evidence that reading AI pre-marked text produces equal or better *novel-text* comprehension and importance-judgment than constrained self-marking would dissolve this chapter's conflict flag — perhaps expert marking functions as a worked example of discrimination, modeling the skill rather than replacing it, with the modeling benefit outrunning the practice cost. The worked-example literature makes this less absurd than it sounds, at least for true novices on unfamiliar genres. If a fading design — pre-marks rich in week one, gone by week six — showed durable discrimination gains over practice-only conditions, this chapter would rewrite its trap as a scaffold mismanaged rather than a mechanism deleted. Nobody has run that study. Until someone does, the default-on, never-fading version that actually ships earns the classification it has.

## Still Puzzling

- The Hattie–Dunlosky tension is resolvable at the mechanism level, but it leaves a meta-question this book keeps meeting: when an influence's d describes a *package* (marking plus the strategy instruction around it), what exactly is a vendor citing when they cite the number? The aggregation problem from Chapter 1, in miniature.
- Highlighting may matter mostly as a *gateway* — the first active-reading behavior weak readers will actually adopt, valuable as the on-ramp to self-testing. If so, the right metric is not the technique's own effect but its conversion rate into stronger techniques, and nobody measures that.
- The calibration question generalizes beyond this chapter and deserves its own research program: can AI, properly aimed, *reduce* illusions of competence — by forcing retrieval, surfacing prediction-performance gaps, withholding fluency? A technology that mostly manufactures false fluency being turned into the first scaled instrument for measuring it would be a fine irony, and a better product category.

- **Bjork, R. A., Dunlosky, J., & Kornell, N. (2013). Self-regulated learning: Beliefs, techniques, and illusions. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 64, 417–444.** The wider map: learners systematically prefer fluent, low-utility techniques, which is the demand-side explanation for why Smart Highlights will always sell.
- **Melumad, S., & Yun, J. H. (2025).
