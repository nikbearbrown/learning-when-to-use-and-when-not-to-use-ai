# Chapter 22 — Cohesion and Adventure Beyond the Screen
*What a ropes course and a chatbot have in common: both target student connection. What they share stops there.*

Strong classroom cohesion (0.44) and outdoor/adventure programs (0.43): two above-hinge influences whose mechanism is shared experience among physically co-present people — belonging built through actual weather, actual rope, actual rooms. In Hattie's synthesis their effects are modest but real. The AI relationship is close to null: this is resistant territory not because the tools threaten it directly but because, at budget time, a chatbot line item and a ropes-course line item compete as if they were the same kind of thing. The chapter is about why they are not, and what AI can do at the edges — logistics, planning, follow-up — without pretending to be the experience.

The finance committee's packet frames it as a simple comparison.

Program A costs $310 per student per year. It takes seventh graders into wilderness for four days in September and cycles eighth-grade advisories through a ropes course all year. It produces no dashboard. Three decades of alumni describe it, with suspicious consistency, as the thing they remember from middle school.

Program B costs $19 per student per year. It delivers daily SEL check-ins by chatbot, sentiment analytics, belonging scores by classroom and building, algorithmically suggested connection activities, and a virtual team-building module whose screenshots show avatars solving a rope-bridge puzzle together. It produces nothing but dashboard.

The framing says: both programs target school climate and student connection. One is sixteen times cheaper and generates continuous measurable data. The obvious question is why the district is still paying for the expensive one.

Nora Whelan, who has directed outdoor education for the Calder Valley district for nine years, knows exactly what she has to defeat on Thursday — not the vendor's product, but the framing. Her actual task is to show the committee that the platform measures a thing it cannot make, and her program makes a thing it has never needed to measure. The substitution the packet proposes does not happen at the mechanism level, where it would be obvious. It happens at the spreadsheet level, where the conflict flag is invisible and the per-student cost is not.

This chapter is her briefing document.

---

## What the Group Is

Two influences: above the hinge, **AI-RESISTANT**, **high human irreducibility**, low substitution risk, **conflict present**. Effect sizes 0.43 to 0.44 on the 2018 252-influence list, weighted average 0.43.

Two flags that look contradictory — low substitution risk *and* conflict present — and their coexistence is the group's whole story. The conflict flag says the way AI works is structurally opposed to the way these influences work. Cohesion and adventure run on embodied co-presence: shared physical challenge, real risk, bodies in weather, a group that cannot log off mid-conflict. A screen-mediated tool does not merely fail to deliver this; it is made of the opposite material.

The low substitution-risk rating reflects the dataset's judgment that, mostly, nobody would believe the gross substitution — a chatbot is not obviously a campfire. That was before procurement season. The substitution this group faces arrives not as a tool pretending to be a campfire but as a *budget line* pretending to be comparable — at the spreadsheet level, where the conflict flag is invisible.

The dataset's hypothesis states the committee's real choice: d ≈ 0.05 [HYPOTHESIS] for both rows if the influence is delivered through AI, because classroom community is built through shared physical experience and outdoor challenge is entirely embodied. Not a discount. A deletion.

## The Two Rows

**Strong classroom cohesion (d = 0.44) — rank 101.** The influence is a class-level property: the sense, shared by teacher and students, that the group is working *together* toward learning that matters — mutual trust, low social risk for participation, a felt "we." The evidence base is largely correlational at the classroom level, with the direction of travel plausibly two-way: cohesive classes learn more, and classes that are learning well cohere [verify — cohesion measures vary across the underlying studies; check construct composition near publication]. What the row tells a leader is not "buy cohesion" but "cohesion is above the hinge — the soft thing is a real thing," which matters in committee rooms where *soft* is treated as a synonym for *optional*.

**Outdoor/adventure programs (d = 0.43) — rank 105.** The strongest version of the evidence long predates the current debate: Hattie himself co-authored the touchstone meta-analysis (Hattie, Marsh, Neill, and Richards, 1997) of adventure education and Outward Bound-type programs, and its most cited finding is not the in-program effect but the follow-up: gains — in self-concept, locus of control, teamwork — that *persisted and in some analyses grew* after the program ended [verify — canonical figures near 0.34 overall with additional follow-up gains; reconcile the 1997 values with the 2018 list's 0.43, as the aggregations differ]. Growth that continues after the intervention stops is the signature of something that changed the *person* rather than the performance. Almost nothing else in the education literature shows this pattern.

Note what both rows share with Chapter 7's relationships and Chapter 23's collective efficacy: the influence *is* the human event. There is no version of either row where the mechanism survives delivery through a different medium, because the medium is the mechanism.

## Why It Works: What the Engine Requires

**Cohesion changes the economics of participation.** In a low-trust classroom, every public attempt is a gamble with social capital: a wrong answer costs standing, so rational students minimize attempts, and the classroom's total cognitive output drops to the floor that safety allows. Cohesion collapses the gamble. When the group's norm is "we are doing something hard together," wrong answers become contributions, help-seeking loses its stigma, and the high-value cognitive behaviors — discussion, debate, genuine question-asking — become possible at all. Cohesion is not a warm feeling adjacent to learning. It is the risk-tolerance infrastructure that serious learning runs on.

And it is built the way trust is always built: incrementally, through shared experience where people see each other act. That cannot be batch-processed, accelerated, or delegated, because what is being accumulated is *evidence about each other* — who shows up when things get hard, who is honest when honesty costs something, who holds the rope.

**Adventure runs the same engine at higher voltage.** Take thirty twelve-year-olds, remove the architecture their social hierarchy depends on — hallways, phones, audiences — and give them problems with real consequences: a tarp that must be rigged before the rain, a route that must be navigated by the group that will walk it. Three things happen that no classroom can cheaply produce.

Competence gets revealed rather than asserted: the kid who reads maps becomes valuable in a way no rubric confers. Interdependence gets enforced by physics rather than by group-work instructions: the canoe genuinely does not move with one paddler. And challenge gets calibrated by reality, which is a teacher students do not suspect of grading on a curve. When the tarp holds, the mastery experience is theirs — unmediated, undismissable.

The 1997 follow-up finding makes sense in exactly these terms. What students take home is not content but an updated model of themselves and of groups, and updated self-models keep paying after the bus returns.

**The conflict, stated precisely.** Screen-mediated tools cannot run this engine — not because the software is bad, but because the engine's parts are what mediation removes. Real risk: a simulation's consequences are revocable, and students know it. Embodied co-presence: the trust evidence accumulated on a ropes course includes voice, weather, fear, and held weight — bandwidth no check-in form carries. Inescapability: the expedition's conflicts must be repaired, because nobody can leave; the platform's conflicts are one log-off from over. An avatar rope-bridge is to a ropes course what a flight simulator is to wind: a fine rehearsal for procedures, and not the thing itself. The d ≈ 0.05 [HYPOTHESIS] is the dataset saying the engine does not start.

## What AI Can Safely Do

The conflict flag forbids AI from the event. The perimeter is wide open, and the perimeter is where Nora's program bleeds hours.

**Carry the logistics.** Permission workflows, medical forms, dietary matrices, gear checklists, transport scheduling, weather-contingency replanning — the administrative mass that occasionally kills marginal programs outright, audited and cut.

**Draft the risk paperwork.** Risk-assessment templates, incident-report drafting, compliance documentation — reviewed and owned by humans, because accountability for safety is not delegable.

**Inform the grouping.** Synthesize teacher observations about who is isolated and which combinations have history into draft crew assignments that staff adjust — pattern support for the human judgment that composes a group, never an autonomous sorter.

**Widen the door.** Accessibility planning for students with disabilities, translated family communications about what the expedition is and why it is safe — AI lowering the participation threshold for exactly the families the program most needs to reach.

**Prompt the harvest.** Post-experience reflection prompts, discussion guides for advisories, prompts that help students name what happened on the mountain — supporting the meaning-making conversation without conducting it.

**Assemble the defense.** The unexpected one, and Thursday's: AI compiling attendance, discipline, counselor-referral, and persistence data around program participation across years; processing decades of alumni survey text; turning a beloved-but-undocumented program into an evidenced one. The program never needed a dashboard to work. It turns out to need one to survive.

## The Substitution Traps

**The comparable-line-item trap.** Already on the committee table: treating the platform and the program as substitutable suppliers of "climate and belonging." The trap works by abstraction — climb high enough ("both target student connection") and everything is comparable; the conflict flag lives down at the mechanism level, where one product is shared weather and the other is a sentiment API. The defense is to drag the conversation back down: what does each one *cause*, by what mechanism — and produce the two numbers: 0.43–0.44 for the embodied programs, d ≈ 0.05 [HYPOTHESIS] for their automated understudies.

**The measurement that becomes the mission.** Belonging scores are Goodhart bait of the purest kind. The daily check-in trains students to perform okayness at a chatbot; classrooms learn which answers keep the dashboard green; the district begins managing the score, which was always a proxy and now is not even that. Worse, the score displaces the human sensing it imitates: an advisory teacher who once noticed a quiet kid now has a dashboard asserting the kid is fine — surveillance pre-poisoned by the watched, dressed as care.

**The simulation that satisfies the requirement.** The quietest version: virtual team-building counted as the advisory program, a "digital expedition" counted as outdoor ed, the budget noting that students "completed collaborative challenge modules." Completion is real. The engine never started. This is the d ≈ 0.05 [HYPOTHESIS] purchased at scale and reported as implementation.

**Kraft's ruler on the vendor claim.** The platform deck reports it "improves belonging by up to 40%." Against the group's honest numbers — real programs earning 0.43 on achievement-linked measures, which by field-experiment benchmarks is a *large* effect — a percentage improvement on a proprietary survey index is not a finding. It is a unit error wearing a sales badge. The 40% is a percentage improvement in a score; the 0.43 is a standard-deviation shift in measurable outcomes. These are not the same unit, and presenting them side by side is the abstraction trap in numerical form.

## Thursday

Nora arrived with numbers she had never had before.

Her preparation was the group's safe-AI list, executed in three weeks. The district's data office, with an AI analysis assist, linked nine cohorts of expedition participation against attendance, discipline referrals, and eighth-to-ninth-grade persistence — finding the program's participation gaps (two ZIP codes, special-education students under-enrolled) *and* the suggestive associations her testimony had always gestured at. The associations were labeled as associations, not causation, in her own slides — because the committee deserved the strongest honest version of the claim, not the strongest version. AI-processed alumni survey text put thirty years of "the thing I remember" into the committee packet as themes with counts instead of anecdotes. And the logistics audit was the sleeper hit: AI workflow drafts showed the program could cut roughly a third of its administrative hours, which Nora converted, in the proposal itself, into two additional expedition sessions serving the under-enrolled groups the data had just exposed. The program walked in cheaper per student than it had been in five years.

The dangerous moment arrived as a compromise. A committee member proposed funding both at half strength: "the platform gives us continuous data; the expedition gives us the experience; surely they're complementary." Half strength is how this program specifically dies — a two-day expedition is a field trip, below the challenge threshold the mechanism requires. And the platform at any strength still measures what it cannot make. The committee's instinct to split the difference was the comparable-line-item trap returning in moderate clothing — the hardest version to refuse, because it sounds like prudence.

The program kept full funding, with the AI-recovered hours funding the equity expansion. The platform was declined. The committee, having seen what evidence assembly looked like, directed that the district's existing climate survey be analyzed annually with the same rigor, and that the outdoor program report participation equity and an agreed indicator set each year. Nora left with her budget and a new annual obligation she had, in effect, invented for herself. She reports mixed feelings and no regrets.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** run logistics, draft risk and compliance documentation for human ownership, support grouping decisions with synthesized observations, widen access through translation and accessibility planning, generate reflection and debrief materials, and assemble the evidence base that documents the program's reach and associations.

**The humans must** be the program: staff and students together in the weather, the challenge real, the interdependence enforced by the task, the conflicts repaired in person, the meaning-making conversation led by adults who were there. No screen-mediated module may be counted as delivering either influence.

**Evidence that learning improved is** human-behavioral: participation rates and their equity, classroom risk-taking in the months after shared experiences, persistence and attendance trends among participants — and the long-horizon signal this group uniquely owns: what alumni still carry, sampled honestly, years later.

---

## The Evidence Check

For any tool or program claiming this group's territory, four demands.

**Mechanism disclosure.** What is the causal story, stated at the level of bodies and groups? "Improves belonging" is a claim about a survey. A program should be able to say what students *do together* and why that builds trust. A vendor that cannot answer this question in mechanism terms is selling a proxy.

**The equity of the experience.** Who participates, who is missing, and what would widen the door. AI's best work in this group is making this table, then making programs answer it.

**Behavior, not sentiment.** Classroom participation patterns, conflict-repair instances, attendance, persistence — observed human behavior over self-reported feelings harvested by the instrument under evaluation. Especially: results that came from someone other than the vendor's own survey.

**The follow-up horizon.** This group's evidence signature is persistence after the program ends. Any honest evaluation samples at six months and beyond. Any vendor dashboard that only reports this week's scores is measuring weather and selling climate.

What does not count: belonging-score dashboards, check-in completion rates, sentiment trendlines, avatar-module completions, or the continuous-data argument itself — continuous measurement of a proxy is not evidence about the construct, and in this group it usually degrades it.

## What Would Change My Mind

The conflict flag rests on embodiment being load-bearing rather than incidental. Strong evidence that screen-mediated shared challenge — well-designed multiplayer problem-solving with persistent groups and real social stakes — produces cohesion gains that *transfer to the physical classroom and persist*, at magnitudes approaching the in-person programs, would force a rewrite from AI-resistant toward medium-flexible. The remote-collaboration literature gives the hypothesis nonzero hope; the follow-up persistence bar — this group's signature — is where I expect digital substitutes to keep failing.

Separately: if the 1997 follow-up findings fail verification or replication, the adventure row's strongest card leaves the deck, and the budget defense reverts to the cohesion mechanism alone [verify].

## Still Puzzling

- The grouping assist is the perimeter's slipperiest stone: AI-suggested crew composition is "support for human judgment" right up until staff stop adjusting the drafts. At what point does the sorter become the composer of the social experience this group says must stay human?
- Cohesion's d = 0.44 is classroom-level and substantially correlational. The honest causal question — how much cohesion does a given shared experience buy, for whom — is nearly unstudied, which means programs like Nora's are defended and attacked with roughly equal evidential force. The data assembly this chapter recommends is a patch, not a cure.
- The belonging-analytics genre keeps growing because districts genuinely lack early sight of isolated kids. Is there a configuration of passive signal — attendance, nurse visits, cafeteria seating patterns — that gives adults early sight without training children to perform wellness for a sentiment model? Nobody has found the line yet, and the vendors are not looking for it.

## Further Reading

- **Hattie, J., Marsh, H. W., Neill, J. T., & Richards, G. E. (1997). Adventure education and Outward Bound: Out-of-class experiences that make a lasting difference. *Review of Educational Research*, 67, 43–87.** The touchstone: effects that persisted and grew at follow-up — the evidence signature this group's defense stands on, co-authored by the dataset's own architect.
- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49, 241–253.** The ruler that makes 0.43 from real programs read as large — and vendor percentage claims read as the unit errors they are.
- **Deutsch, M. (1949). A theory of cooperation and competition. *Human Relations*, 2, 129–152.** The interdependence theory underneath both rows: why outcomes linked across persons change behavior in ways no individual incentive reproduces.
- **Boss, S., & Krauss, J. (2022). *Reinventing Project-Based Learning* (3rd ed.).** The classroom-scale translation: structured interdependence and real audiences as cohesion machinery for schools whose budget will never include a mountain.
