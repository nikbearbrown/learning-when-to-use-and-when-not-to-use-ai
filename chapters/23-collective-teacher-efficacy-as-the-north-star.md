# Chapter 23 — Collective Teacher Efficacy as the North Star
*The largest number in the dataset is on the table, attached to the proposal least capable of producing it.*

One influence, the largest number on Hattie's entire list: collective teacher efficacy, d = 1.57 — a faculty's shared, evidence-fed belief that their teaching causes their students' learning. It is also one of the list's most misread numbers, a correlational measure of a school's collective state rather than a switch anyone can flip. The AI relationship is almost entirely indirect: no tool can hold a belief for a faculty, but tools can feed or starve the evidence loops the belief grows from. High substitution risk here means something specific — platforms marketed as the thing itself, purchased in place of the slow institutional work the number actually describes.

The vendor's opening slide resolves on the boardroom screen, and it is the slide Dr. Lenore Adeyemi knew was coming: a bar chart of Hattie's top influences with the tallest bar glowing. *Collective teacher efficacy: d = 1.57. The number one influence on student achievement. Now measurable, trackable, and improvable — with EfficacyIQ.*

The product is a collective efficacy dashboard. Quarterly staff surveys feed building-level "efficacy scores." AI analyzes open-response items for "belief signals." Principals get heat maps and benchmark comparisons against districts the vendor declines to name. An AI assistant generates "efficacy-building communications" — celebration messages for staff — and recommends "high-impact efficacy interventions" from a template library.

The superintendent is interested. Two board members arrived already persuaded that 1.57 is the district's most underexploited resource. The pitch deck's logic is a clean syllogism: the research says collective efficacy is the most powerful influence; you cannot improve what you do not measure; therefore, measure it with us.

Lenore half-agrees with the premise and entirely distrusts the conclusion, and she has one week to articulate the difference.

## What the Number Is

Collective teacher efficacy at d = 1.57 is rank 1 of 252 on the 2018 list. The chapter does not get to quote that number and move on. The number's provenance is the chapter.

The construct: a faculty's shared belief that *we — this staff, together — can produce learning in our students, whatever they arrive with*. Not individual confidence aggregated but a group-level property, in Bandura's social-cognitive lineage. Such beliefs shape what a group attempts, how long it persists, how it reads setbacks. The school-level research began with Goddard, Hoy, and Woolfolk Hoy around 2000, finding collective efficacy strongly associated with achievement differences between schools, even controlling for demographics — in correlational designs. The headline d traces to Eells's 2011 dissertation, a meta-analysis of *correlational* studies relating measured collective efficacy to achievement.

Now the honesty ledger, in full, because the case turns on it.

**The 1.57 is a converted correlation, not an intervention effect** [contested]. No one assigned schools to high-efficacy and low-efficacy conditions and measured the achievement difference. The figure summarizes how strongly two measured variables travel together, translated into d-units that invite — but do not license — comparison with intervention rows. The conversion and the construct's ranking have drawn specific, published methodological criticism [contested].

**The causal arrow runs both ways at minimum.** Faculties whose students achieve have excellent grounds for believing in their collective capability — mastery experience, Bandura's strongest efficacy source, *is* prior success. Some unknown fraction of the 1.57 is achievement causing belief.

**Confounding is live.** Stable leadership, adequate resources, low staff turnover, and functional culture plausibly raise both efficacy beliefs and achievement.

What survives the ledger: a strong, replicated association plus a credible mechanism with experimental support at the underlying Bandura level. The defensible reading — collective efficacy is a *marker and plausible multiplier* of organizational health, well worth cultivating through its known sources, and worth approximately nothing as a number to chase. The district that hears "build the conditions Bandura described" has read the row correctly. The district that hears "acquire 1.57" has bought the bar chart.

The dataset's own hypothesis about what survives automation is the bluntest in the classification: d ≈ 0.15 [HYPOTHESIS] — AI dashboard only; collective trust cannot be automated. From 1.57 to 0.15: the largest hypothesized collapse in the book.

## How the Mechanism Actually Works

Grant the mechanism its best case and trace it, because the tracing is what exposes the dashboard.

A faculty's shared belief in its collective capability changes behavior through channels any teacher will recognize. Teachers in high-efficacy schools attempt more — demanding instruction, stubborn re-teaching — because effort spent feels causally connected to outcomes. They persist longer through implementation dips. They attribute student struggle to instruction, which is actionable, rather than to students, which is final. And they cooperate at higher bandwidth: collective belief makes deprivatized practice feel like shared problem-solving instead of exposure.

Every channel terminates in *teacher behavior toward students*. Belief is the fuel, not the engine. If the behavior never changes, the belief was decoration.

Where does the fuel come from? Bandura's four sources, and the order matters:

**Mastery experience** — the strongest source, and the only one that compounds. The faculty *does something together that works and knows it worked*. A collaboratively planned intervention, run, measured, and seen to move students. This is where the construct is grown, not surveyed.

**Vicarious experience** — watching credible colleagues succeed with *our* students. Peer observation, within-school exemplars.

**Social persuasion** — leadership narrative and feedback: real, but weak alone, and parasitic on the first two. Persuasion without mastery experience is morale poster manufacturing.

**Affective state** — the stress and emotional climate the work happens in. Chronic overload reads, group-wide, as evidence of incapability.

Every source is experiential and social — had, together, over time, in teams that plan, act, examine evidence, and attribute success to themselves. There is no API to mastery experience. This is why the influence is AI-RESISTANT, high irreducibility.

The dashboard's category error is now exact. It instruments source 3 — and thinly, through survey language — while doing nothing for sources 1 and 2, and, via survey fatigue and surveillance feel, plausibly degrading source 4. It measures the fuel gauge and calls itself an engine.

## What AI Can Safely Do

The safe zone is strictly infrastructural — everything that lowers the cost of the experiences the mechanism actually runs on.

**Make mastery experiences visible.** Assemble before/after evidence of team-driven instructional changes: the assessment data, work samples, and trend lines that let a faculty *see* that what it did worked. Bandura's strongest source has a clerical bottleneck. AI removes it.

**Prepare the inquiry cycle.** Summarize student work patterns before team meetings, draft the data displays, transcribe and action-list the discussion — so collaborative inquiry runs on teacher judgment instead of teacher clerical stamina.

**Protect the time.** Scheduling support that finds and defends common planning time — the binding constraint on every source-1 activity in every real school.

**Lower the cost of vicarious experience.** Logistics for peer observation — coverage, scheduling — and clip summaries from lesson video that make colleague success shareable, with Chapter 14's consent and privacy rules applied.

**Equip the leadership narrative.** Draft celebrations of *specific, evidenced* team accomplishments — social persuasion anchored to mastery experience, which is the only kind that compounds. The anchor is the condition; unanchored AI-generated affirmation is the trap one paragraph below.

## The Substitution Trap

**The dashboard as the work.** The flagship trap is the purchase itself, reasoned as the vendor's syllogism. Walk the mechanism and watch it miss: the survey instruments beliefs without touching their sources; the heat map gives principals a number to manage; and Goodhart arrives on schedule — staff learn the survey's stakes, and the scores drift up while nothing under them moves. Leadership attention — the scarcest resource the real mechanism needed — is now spent on score movement instead of on time, teams, and evidence. The district reports rising collective efficacy while d ≈ 0.15 [HYPOTHESIS] accrues underneath.

**Synthetic persuasion.** The AI "efficacy-building communications" feature deserves its own autopsy. Social persuasion works through *credibility* — Bandura is explicit — and a faculty that discovers its celebration messages are machine-generated has learned the precise opposite of the intended lesson: leadership cares enough about our beliefs to automate the caring. This counterfeits the *weakest* efficacy source while spending the credibility the strongest sources depend on.

**The score as a label.** Building-level efficacy scores, benchmarked and circulated, are the labeling machine scaled to faculties. The "low-efficacy school" tag changes how the district treats the building, how the building reads itself, and what its best teachers do next — they leave. The construct's own logic predicts the harm: tell a group it collectively cannot, and you have administered anti-efficacy through the instrument that claimed to build it.

**Chasing the number itself.** The meta-trap: 1.57 is not an available purchase. By Kraft's benchmarks, honest field interventions clear 0.20 rarely and 0.50 almost never. Any proposal promising motion on a 1.57-scale construct, by survey subscription, is quoting a correlational artifact as a product roadmap. The grander the number on the slide, the more certain it is describing something a vendor cannot sell — because things that large are properties of organizations, grown, not features, shipped.

## The Case in Full

The leadership team met without the vendor first, which was Lenore's one procedural demand. The meeting opened with her two-line memo: *The number is real as a correlation, unavailable as a purchase. Proposal: spend the same money on the mechanism and use AI as its clerk.*

The redirected pilot ran in Brookfield's two middle schools. The district bought no efficacy product. It bought time and clerical removal — common planning periods protected by scheduling, and an AI workflow that prepared each grade-team inquiry cycle: student work summarized before meetings, data displays drafted, decisions transcribed into action lists, and — the load-bearing piece — the before/after evidence assembled at cycle's end so the team could see whether its instructional bet had paid.

The first compounding event arrived in March. The seventh-grade team's reteach design for proportional reasoning moved their common assessment visibly, the AI-assembled evidence brief said so in two pages, and the team presented it to the faculty themselves. Mastery experience, made visible, narrated by its owners — source 1 feeding source 2, with the machine carrying chairs.

Success regrew the appetite. By May, a board member — pleased and reading the spring survey's modest climate uptick — proposed scaling what was working by adopting the dashboard after all, *now* with something to measure. The instinct was sincere and exactly wrong, and Lenore's refusal memo became the district's phase gate: the moment building-level belief scores become managed numbers, the teams' candor — the thing the inquiry cycles run on — becomes the cost of someone's benchmark. The district would track the behaviors and the student outcomes. The beliefs would be surveyed annually, anonymously, unbenchmarked, as a thermometer no one is paid to move.

**The decision: four conditions.** No efficacy dashboard; the construct is monitored, never managed. The mechanism funded directly: protected collaborative time, AI clerical support for inquiry cycles, peer-observation coverage. Leadership communications celebrate specific evidenced team wins, written by the humans claiming the credit. Evidence check fixed: inquiry-cycle completion and quality — did teams act, measure, and attribute? — instructional changes documented per team, student outcomes on the cycles' own measures, and turnover and climate as slow indicators. The 1.57 appears nowhere in the plan. The sources do.

## What Would Change My Mind

Two findings, in opposite directions. A rigorous intervention study — schools randomized to a multi-year program feeding Bandura's sources (protected collaboration, structured inquiry, leadership development) — showing achievement effects approaching even a fifth of the headline figure would upgrade this chapter's "marker and plausible multiplier" to "confirmed lever." Conversely, careful causal work showing the efficacy-achievement association is mostly achievement-driven or confound-driven — belief as exhaust, not fuel — would demote this row from north star to weathervane, and the right spend would become the underlying practices with the construct ignored entirely. The honest current position: the dataset's number one influence is the book's least causally secured high-band claim — exactly why it got the [HYPOTHESIS] discipline at full strength.

## Still Puzzling

Even an anonymous, unbenchmarked annual survey teaches a faculty that its beliefs are of administrative interest, and beliefs observed are beliefs performed, a little. Is there any measurement of collective efficacy that does not begin corrupting it?

AI clerical support makes inquiry cycles cheaper — but part of source 1's power may be the *effort* of the shared work, the same way this book treats learner effort as mechanism elsewhere. If the machine carries too many chairs, does the experience still read as *ours*? Nobody knows where that line is.

The construct is grouped here as a school-level property, but AI is changing what "the faculty's collective work" even is. Whether collective efficacy can form around AI-saturated practice, or is diluted by it, is a question the literature has not yet thought to ask.

## Further Reading

**Bandura, A. (1997). *Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control*.** The theoretical bedrock: the four sources, the group-level extension, and the credibility conditions on persuasion that the synthetic-celebration feature violates by design.

**Goddard, R. D., Hoy, W. K., & Woolfolk Hoy, A. (2000). Collective teacher efficacy: Its meaning, measure, and impact on student achievement. *American Educational Research Journal*, 37, 479–507.** The construct's school-level founding study — an honest model of correlational claims carefully made.

**Eells, R. J. (2011). *Meta-analysis of the relationship between collective teacher efficacy and student achievement* (doctoral dissertation, Loyola University Chicago).** The source of the 1.57: read it to see exactly what was aggregated — correlations — and therefore what the famous number is and is not.

**Donohoo, J. (2017). *Collective Efficacy: How Educators' Beliefs Impact Student Learning*.** The practitioner translation that carried the construct into district planning — best read alongside the published critiques of the effect-size conversion [contested].

**Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49, 241–253.** The ruler of last resort: what real interventions deliver, and why any slide promising motion on a 1.57 construct has confused a correlation for a catalog item.
