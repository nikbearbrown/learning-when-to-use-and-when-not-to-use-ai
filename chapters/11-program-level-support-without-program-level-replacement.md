# Chapter 11 — Program-Level Support Without Program-Level Replacement
*The autopilot that does not announce itself as the thing that has replaced you.*

Mathematics programs, science programs, formative evaluation, exposure to reading — four program-level influences from 0.43 to 0.59 in Hattie's synthesis. These are curricula and routines, not single classroom moves, and AI can support them at scale: generating problems, tracking formative signals, recommending texts. The substitution risk is high at a different altitude than usual — not the student outsourcing homework but the institution outsourcing the program, mistaking a platform license for a mathematics program. The chapter is about keeping AI inside the program without letting it quietly become the program.

The AI mathematics suite comes in a pitch deck, and the deck's best feature is the one the assistant superintendent underlined twice: *autopilot mode*. The system detects a student's gaps and assigns remediation automatically. No teacher action required.

Elena Vasquez chairs the mathematics department. Her two newest teachers are drowning in prep and want the suite. Her most experienced teacher — the one who has run the department's common-formative-assessment cycle for a decade — slid the deck back across the conference table with one sentence: "This thing wants my Tuesday meeting."

That is the decision, stated exactly. The suite can either feed the department's instructional program — the planning, the common assessments, the Tuesday data meetings, the reteach decisions — or it can quietly become the program, with the department reduced to proctoring it. Both futures run on identical licenses. Elena has to write the adoption memo that forces the first future and forbids the second.

## The Mechanism the Effect Sizes Were Measuring

This group collects four influences: mathematics programs (d = 0.59), science programs (d = 0.48), providing formative evaluation (d = 0.48), and exposure to reading (d = 0.43). Weighted average 0.49, above the hinge, augment-classified, high substitution risk, no cognitive-work conflict on the student side.

The substitution risk has moved house from previous chapters. In Chapter 8 it lived at the teacher's elbow — a script that replaced seeing. Here it operates at the organizational level. These are program influences, things schools implement rather than things students do, and what gets substituted is the program itself. An AI suite does not need to write a single sentence for a single student to hollow this group out. It only needs to replace the implementation — the teacher sense-making, the instructional coherence, the adult decisions — that the effect sizes were measuring in the first place.

The unifying mechanism is **coherence sustained by human implementation**. Programs outperform ad-hoc teaching not because their materials are magic but because they align four things that drift apart by default: what gets taught, in what sequence, how learning is checked, and what happens when the check comes back bad. The fourth link carries the most weight and is the easiest to lose. A mathematics program without a functioning respond-to-evidence loop is a pacing guide. Formative evaluation without changed instruction is data collection as ritual.

Every link in that chain is an adult cognitive act: sequencing judgment, assessment interpretation, the diagnosis of *why* thirty students missed the proportionality item — procedure slip, rate misconception, reading load — and the redesign of Wednesday because of it. This is why the group sits at medium irreducibility. The learner's work is not the bottleneck. The organization's thinking is.

The same chain explains the substitution risk. Each link can be individually automated with a plausible-sounding justification: the suite sequences (adaptive pathing), checks (auto-generated items), diagnoses (misconception dashboard), and responds (autopilot remediation). Automate all four and you have not augmented the program — you have replaced it with a different program, one whose implementation quality is whatever the vendor shipped, and whose track record is not what the d = 0.59 measured.

## The Influences

**Mathematics programs (d = 0.59)** — rank 49 of 252. The number belongs to coherent, implemented programs: sequenced curricula with aligned materials, teacher support, and sustained use — not to any binder or platform on its own. The same program produces different results in different buildings, which is the quiet warning inside every program-level effect size. Implementation is the active ingredient, and implementation is made of teacher behavior. The dataset's hypothesis: adaptive practice is legitimate augmentation; conceptual instruction and misconception remediation stay human.

**Science programs (d = 0.48)** — rank 84. Same logic, plus a sharper division. The dataset marks simulations and adaptive content as useful, and inquiry facilitation — the orchestration of prediction, experiment, argument from evidence — as the human core. A simulation can present the discrepant event. Making the discrepancy *matter* in a room of twelve-year-olds is teaching.

**Providing formative evaluation (d = 0.48)** — rank 87, and the most misread row in the group. In Hattie's framing this influence is teacher-facing: the practice of teachers gathering evidence of their own effect and adjusting instruction — teachers *seeing their impact*, not students receiving more quizzes. The d belongs to the loop closing through a teacher's changed next move. The hypothesis splits accordingly: AI may collect and visualize; interpretation and instructional adjustment are the mechanism and stay human.

**Exposure to reading (d = 0.43)** — rank 104. Volume of reading matters. AI's legitimate role is increasing exposure — adaptive text selection, matching, abundance — while the dataset notes that a teacher's visible enthusiasm for reading shapes whether students *want* the exposure. A recommendation engine can put the right book in a hand. It cannot make the hand reach.

All four from the 2018 list. In honest field trials on standardized measures, Kraft's benchmarks apply: effects of this size are large, not modest. A vendor deck implying the suite will deliver 0.59 because the *influence* carries 0.59 has confused the category with the product.

## What AI Can Safely Do

The safe zone is the loop's logistics and its evidence supply — never the interpretation and decision that constitute the loop's mechanism.

**Draft the program's raw material.** Worked-example variants, problem sets at controlled difficulty, lab and simulation setups, item banks aligned to this week's objective — at a volume that makes the department's common-assessment cycle cheap to run instead of heroic.

**Collect and visualize formative evidence.** Score what is scorable, aggregate by standard and misconception, and put the pattern in front of Tuesday's meeting in minutes instead of someone's Sunday.

**Flag, with reasons.** Surface which students missed *which way* — the wrong-answer taxonomy a teacher can interrogate — rather than a red/yellow/green that hides the diagnosis.

**Multiply exposure.** Adaptive text and problem selection that keeps every student supplied with material at reachable difficulty. Abundance is the one thing the machine does better than any department.

**Support the new teacher.** Draft lesson sequences against the program's scope for the teacher to edit — the suite as a senior colleague's filing cabinet, not the senior colleague.

The boundary in imperative form: use AI to make the program's loop faster and better-fed. Never let it close the loop — interpret, decide, reteach — on the department's behalf.

## The Substitution Trap

**Autopilot remediation.** The flagship feature is the flagship trap. The moment gap-detection flows straight into auto-assigned remediation, the teacher's diagnostic act — the load-bearing link — is bypassed by design. What autopilot can diagnose is the pattern of wrong answers. What it cannot diagnose is the cause behind the pattern, the conceptual model in the student's head, or whether the right response is a video, a small group, a manipulative, or a conversation. The loop keeps closing, smoothly, wrongly often enough — and because it closes silently, nobody is present to notice which.

**The Tuesday meeting dies politely.** No one cancels the data meeting. It just thins. The dashboard is so legible that interpretation feels redundant. The suite has already assigned the remediation, so the reteach decision feels pre-made. Attendance becomes review, then becomes optional. Within two terms, the school still *has* a formative evaluation practice on paper and no longer has the influence — because the influence was never the data. It was teachers confronting evidence of their own effect and changing course.

**Program drift.** The suite's sequence quietly outranks the department's. Teachers teach toward what the platform assesses. The platform assesses what it can auto-generate. What it can auto-generate skews procedural. Eighteen months in, the enacted curriculum is the vendor's, adopted by erosion rather than decision — and the inquiry lessons, the rich tasks, the things that made the science program a program, are the residue that didn't fit the item types.

Liljedahl's classroom research names the student-side risk in one word: *mimicking*. Students reproducing demonstrated procedures without thinking is precisely the mode an unsupervised video-then-practice-then-remediate loop rewards. The loop keeps producing green bars. The thinking it was supposed to build never starts.

## The Case in Full

Elena Vasquez wrote the memo, and its first line set its teeth: *We are buying a faster loop, not a second program.*

The pilot's wins were exactly where the classification predicts. Common formative assessments that had taken the department a planning period to write and a weekend to score were drafted by the suite in minutes, edited by the team in twenty, and scored overnight — the Tuesday meeting got its evidence while the teaching was still warm. The misconception dashboard caught something real in week five: a wrong-answer cluster on proportional reasoning that split seventh grade cleanly into two different errors, an additive-thinking group and a units-confusion group. The two-group reteach that followed was the best instructional decision of the term, and it was a human decision the machine had made affordable.

Week eight, and the dangerous moment arrived wearing the suite's best feature. A struggling student's parent called to thank the school for "all the extra help" — autopilot had been assigning her daughter remediation videos and practice sets nightly, fifty minutes a night, for three weeks. Her in-suite mastery bars were climbing. Her performance in class discussion and on the department's paper transfer task had not moved, and her enthusiasm for mathematics was visibly sinking under the nightly grind. The girl had become very good at the suite — pausing videos at the worked step, pattern-matching the practice items. Mimicking, industrialized. No teacher had assigned, reviewed, or even seen the three weeks of instruction. It had simply flowed, gap to video to practice to green bar, through a loop with no adult in it.

**The decision.** Adopt, with the loop rewired. Autopilot off, contractually — gap detection routes to the teacher's queue as a *recommendation with reasons*, and remediation is assigned by humans, in class structures the teacher chooses. The Tuesday meeting is the only body that closes the loop: the suite feeds it, never replaces it, and the meeting's reteach decisions are logged next to the dashboard's recommendations — a running record of where the humans and the machine disagreed, which became the department's best professional development artifact. The department's sequence governs: the suite is re-mapped each unit to the program's scope, not vice versa. Evidence lives off-platform: the district interim assessment and a quarterly transfer task, novel problems, no suite, paper, scored blind against last year's baseline. In-suite mastery was named in the memo as a non-metric.

The vendor accepted the configuration. The implementation contract made autopilot opt-in per teacher, not opt-out per district — a clause Elena's experienced teacher wrote, and the one the vendor's sales engineer was most reluctant to initial.

## What Would Change My Mind

The chapter's spine is that the diagnostic-and-respond link must stay human — so a credible randomized comparison in which full-autopilot remediation matched teacher-mediated response on delayed, off-platform, transfer-bearing outcomes, across a school year and across achievement levels, would force a rewrite. Adaptive-practice research has produced respectable wins in narrower lanes, so the outcome is not unthinkable. What the honest reading of current evidence says is that it has not been shown at program scale, and the mimicking risk runs the other way. Separately: if departments using recommendation-queue designs in practice rubber-stamp the queue — overrides near zero everywhere — then the human in the loop was always ceremonial, and the real choice is starker than this chapter pretends: autopilot, or no suite.

## Still Puzzling

Formative evaluation's d was earned when evidence was scarce and teacher attention was the constraint. When evidence becomes abundant and *interpretation* becomes the constraint, does the influence's value rise, or does abundance itself degrade the confronting-your-own-effect mechanism into scrolling?

Exposure to reading sits oddly in a suite-dominated chapter: recommendation engines demonstrably increase matched exposure, yet the teacher-enthusiasm mediator suggests a ceiling nobody has measured. Whether algorithmic abundance plus human book-talk beats either alone is an open and very buyable question.

The new-teacher dilemma has no clean answer: the suite helps novices most and erodes exactly the program-implementation muscles novices most need to build. A department that protects its veterans' judgment while letting its novices lean may be growing a generation that can run the platform and not the program.

## Further Reading

**Hattie, J., & Timperley, H. (2007). The power of feedback. *Review of Educational Research*, 77(1), 81–112.** The feed-up/feed-back/feed-forward frame that makes formative evaluation a teacher-action influence rather than a quizzing influence.

**Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The benchmark realism this chapter leans on: why 0.10–0.20 from a real intervention is large, and why decks quoting influence-level d values as product promises should be read twice.

**Liljedahl, P. (2021). *Building Thinking Classrooms in Mathematics*.** The mimicking diagnosis, and the strongest available account of classroom structures that force thinking — the human program autopilot quietly replaces.

**Bastani, H., et al. (2025). Generative AI without guardrails can harm learning: Evidence from high school mathematics. *PNAS*.** The assisted/unassisted divergence, here read at organizational scale: in-suite performance is assisted performance.

**U.S. Department of Education, Office of Educational Technology (2023). *Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning*.** The teacher-in-the-loop doctrine applied to exactly this product category, including its warnings on automated assignment of instruction.
