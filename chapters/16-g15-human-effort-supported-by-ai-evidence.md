# Chapter 16 — G15: Human Effort Supported by AI Evidence

## The Decision on the Table

Renee Castillo directs Crossroads Academy, an alternative high school and adult-education center where the enrollment paperwork tells the same story in two hundred handwritings: students for whom the regular system's pace, schedule, or patience ran out. Her staff's core skill is not content delivery. It is noticing — catching the week a student's attendance wobbles, the assignment half-attempted after months of full effort, the silence that precedes a disappearance. At Crossroads, persistence is the curriculum. Everything else is what persistence is spent on.

The state's adult-education grant this year comes bundled with an engagement-analytics platform. It watches what Renee's staff cannot: login patterns, assignment-start latency, time-on-task across the online coursework half her students rely on, week-over-week effort trends. It flags slippage days before a human would see it. It also arrives with a feature suite the vendor calls "automated persistence support" — streaks, badges, motivational nudge messages timed by an engagement model, and escalating reminder sequences for students going quiet.

Her intake counselor, who has talked more dropouts back through the door than anyone in the building, read the brochure and handed it back with one question: "So when a student gets the 3 a.m. *We miss you!* text and realizes no one actually sent it — what does that cost us?" Renee has until the grant acceptance deadline to decide which half of this gift to take.

## What This Group Is

Two influences: **High band**, AI-AUGMENTED, **high human irreducibility**, low substitution risk, no cognitive-work conflict. Effect sizes 0.72 to 0.77 on the 2018 252-influence list, weighted average 0.74.

Read the signature carefully, because it is gentler than the last chapter's and the gentleness is informative. No conflict flag: nothing about how AI works structurally contradicts how effort or teacher development works. Low substitution risk — *as classified*: the dataset judges that AI's natural roles here (monitoring, evidence, logistics) sit comfortably apart from the mechanism. But high human irreducibility anchors the group, and it draws the chapter's actual line: the mechanism in both rows is sustained human commitment — a student's, a teacher's — and commitment is mediated by relationships with people, not by message queues. AI's role in this group is the most cleanly evidentiary in the book: it can *see* effort, surface effort, and protect the time of the humans who respond to effort. What it cannot do is be the reason anyone keeps going.

The trap, accordingly, is not theft of cognition but counterfeit of care — automation creeping from the evidence layer into the response layer, where it does not belong and does not work.

## The Influences

**Effort (d = 0.77)** — rank 19 of 252. The honesty note comes first here, because this row is unusually easy to misread: effort is not an intervention. It is a learner disposition-and-behavior — engagement, persistence, the willingness to spend oneself on hard tasks — and much of its evidence base is correlational: students who exert more effort achieve more, with the arrows running both directions (effort produces achievement; achievement, by making effort feel worthwhile, produces effort). Nobody can buy d = 0.77 off a shelf, because the row names an outcome of good conditions as much as a cause of good results. What the influence tells a leader is where the leverage lives: in whatever makes sustained trying *rational* for a particular student. The dataset's split is exact — AI disengagement detection and monitoring legitimately support the influence, at a hypothesized d ≈ 0.40 [HYPOTHESIS], while sustained academic persistence is mediated by teacher relationships.

**Comprehensive instructional programs for teachers (d = 0.72)** — rank 29. The staff-side twin: sustained, structured professional development — not the one-day workshop, but multi-component programs combining content, modeled practice, classroom application, and follow-through over time. The broader PD literature (Kraft and colleagues' synthesis of coaching programs is the touchstone) consistently finds the active ingredients are the *sustained and relational* components: observation, feedback, coaching cycles — and that effects shrink as programs scale away from them. The dataset's hypothesis mirrors the student row: AI can scaffold content delivery, curation, and logistics at d ≈ 0.55 [HYPOTHESIS]; teacher learning through peer observation and coaching is human-dependent.

The two rows belong together because they are the same mechanism at two altitudes: a person deciding, week after week, that the hard work is worth continuing — and the conditions that make that decision keep coming out yes.

## Why It Works

Strip effort to its engine and you find a continuously re-evaluated bargain. Expectancy-value theory gives the cleanest frame: a learner persists when the task seems doable (expectancy), worth doing (value), and when quitting would cost something that matters. For Renee's students, every term in that equation has been damaged by history — expectancy by years of failure, value by curricula that never connected, and the cost of quitting by institutions that did not visibly notice when they did. What rebuilds the equation, in the persistence research and in every practitioner's experience, is overwhelmingly relational and evidential: tasks calibrated so success is genuinely reachable; progress made visible so the student can see the trend her discouragement hides; and — the load-bearing term at a place like Crossroads — a specific person who notices. Being noticed converts effort from a private gamble into a social fact. It raises the cost of quitting (someone will know, and that someone matters) and raises the value of trying (someone sees it).

Now place the platform against the engine. Noticing has two components that ordinarily travel together: *detection* (something changed in this student's behavior) and *response* (a person acts on it, toward the student). Human staff are expensive at detection — twelve advisees per advisor is watchful; sixty is triage — and irreplaceable at response. The platform is the inverse: superhuman at detection, incapable of response in the mechanism's sense, because a response that builds persistence works through the relationship it expresses. The 3 a.m. text fails not because its words are wrong but because it expresses no relationship; the moment its automation is discovered, it testifies to the *absence* of one — the institutional equivalent of a birthday card from a bank, sent to someone whose trust in institutions is already scar tissue.

The teacher-PD row runs the identical analysis. Teacher development persists, in programs that work, through the same bargain — reachable practice change, visible progress, and a coach or peer who notices. AI can curate the content and run the logistics; the coaching relationship carries the persistence. Goodhart's law patrols both rows: the instant effort *metrics* become targets — streaks, minutes, badges — students and teachers alike optimize the metric, and the metric decouples from the disposition it proxied. Login minutes are easy to manufacture at 1 a.m. with a video running. Trying is not the same as appearing to try, and machines, so far, mostly measure appearing.

## What AI Can Safely Do

- **Detect early, with the evidence shown.** Disengagement flags from login latency, submission patterns, and effort trends — surfaced days before human noticing thresholds, with the underlying pattern attached so the responding adult starts the conversation informed, not scripted.
- **Make progress visible to its owner.** Trend lines a student reviews *with* an advisor — the evidence layer of expectancy repair: look what eight weeks of your effort did. The data serves the conversation; it does not replace it.
- **Triage the noticing.** Rank-order who most needs a human touch this week, so the advisory team's scarce relational hours land where the engine is actually stalling.
- **Protect the relational hours.** Draft the administrative residue — attendance reports, grant paperwork, scheduling — that currently eats the staff time the mechanism runs on. Every clerical hour returned to a counselor is the platform's most legitimate contribution to d = 0.77.
- **Carry the PD program's plumbing.** Curate content to each teacher's context, schedule coaching cycles, summarize classroom video for the coach's preparation (Chapter 14's rules apply), track follow-through — the comprehensive program's logistics, automated so its relationships can be afforded.
- **Calibrate the work itself.** Adaptive difficulty that keeps tasks in the reachable zone — expectancy support delivered through the work rather than through messages about the work.

## The Substitution Trap

**The counterfeit noticer.** The flagship trap is the feature suite Renee's counselor flagged: automated nudges, streaks, badges, "We miss you!" sequences. The failure is twofold. Mechanically, gamified engagement leans on the wrong motivators for this population — external prompts that decay with familiarity and an effort-theater economy that Goodhart predicts: streaks maintained by logged-in idleness, badges earned by the students who least needed them, metrics rising as the disposition flatlines. Relationally, it is worse than inert. Discovery is inevitable — students compare notes, and the messages' uniformity confesses their authorship — and at a school whose students are expert detectors of institutional insincerity, the discovered counterfeit doesn't just fail to build trust; it spends it. The platform's most dangerous property is that this feature *works* on the dashboard for a semester: nudge-response rates, streak statistics, engagement upticks. The metrics measure the theater.

**Surveillance without relationship.** The same detection layer this chapter endorses, minus the human response, is just watching. Students who learn they are monitored but never personally contacted draw the accurate conclusion — the institution tracks me; no one here knows me — and monitoring data starts arriving pre-poisoned, as students game what they know is watched. Detection earns its keep only when it reliably produces a human knock.

**The content library wearing a program's name.** The PD-side trap: the "comprehensive instructional program" procured as an AI-curated content platform — personalized modules, completion tracking, zero observation, zero coaching. It automates the components the research says carry the least weight and deletes the ones that carry the most, then reports completion rates as teacher growth. The d = 0.72 belonged to programs with humans in the loop *as the loop*; the library version is a different product renting the row's evidence.

## Case Study

Renee Castillo accepted the grant, took half the gift, and wrote the other half out of the configuration before the first login.

**The tool.** The engagement-analytics platform. **The influences.** Effort, d = 0.77 — presented to staff with the correlational caveat aloud: this number describes what persistence is worth, not what a dashboard delivers — and the teacher-program row, d = 0.72, because Crossroads' new-staff induction was being rebuilt the same year.

**The useful moment.** Detection earned its license in week six. The platform flagged Marcus — twenty-four, two jobs, eight months of grinding consistency toward a diploma — on assignment-start latency: still logging in, but hovering, not starting. No human had seen it; his advisor's caseload is fifty-one. The flag produced a Tuesday conversation, the conversation produced the fact (his second job had moved to nights; he was reading assignments at 2 a.m., too exhausted to begin), and the fact produced a schedule rebuild that the platform could never have prescribed and the advisor could never have known to offer. Marcus is still enrolled. The advisory team's Monday huddle now opens with the platform's triage list — ten minutes of machine evidence allocating five days of human noticing — and the staff's flat verdict by December was that the flags were finding the quiet fades that used to surface only as withdrawal paperwork.

**The dangerous moment.** It arrived through the grant's reporting portal. The state dashboard displays "engagement scores" by site — heavily weighted toward logins, minutes, and streaks — and Crossroads ranked low against sites running the full nudge suite. The pressure was structural and polite: a program officer's email noting the gap and asking about her "persistence-feature adoption plan." For two weeks the temptation had a budget line attached. What stopped it was her counselor running one comparison by hand: at the top-ranked site's published numbers, nudge-driven re-engagement looked impressive for a month and evaporated by the quarter — while Crossroads' human-response model showed smaller spikes and a flatly better completion trend. Renee sent the comparison with her reply, declined the nudge suite again, and asked the program officer which number the grant was actually purchasing.

**The decision.** The configuration memo, four lines long, became local legend. (1) The platform detects; humans respond: every flag routes to a named adult, every contact is personal, and no automated message reaches a student — the auto-nudge module stays off, in writing. (2) Forty-eight-hour rule: a flag unanswered by a human within two days escalates to Renee, because detection without response is surveillance. (3) Students see their own data, with a person: progress reviews use the trend lines in advisory conversations, never as posted leaderboards. (4) Evidence is persistence itself, measured where it matters: term-to-term retention, completion rates, re-enrollment after stop-outs, and unassisted work quality — with engagement-score rankings explicitly excluded from staff evaluation. The new-teacher induction got the same architecture: AI curates each teacher's content and schedules the cycles; a human mentor observes and coaches; completion dashboards report to no one's evaluation.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** monitor engagement signals and flag disengagement early with evidence attached, triage the response queue, render progress visible for human-led conversations, calibrate task difficulty, draft administrative work to free relational hours, and run the logistics and curation of teacher-development programs.

**The humans must** make every response to a flagged student personally — a named adult, a real conversation — and carry the coaching, observation, and follow-through of teacher development; **the learner must** still do the trying, on work that stays genuinely theirs — because persistence is built in the relationship between a person and someone who notices, and the noticing cannot be delegated past the detection step.

**Evidence that learning improved is** retention, completion, and re-enrollment trends; the quality of unassisted student work over time; and — for the teacher program — observed practice change in classrooms, not module completion.

## The Evidence Check

For any tool or program claiming this group:

1. **Persistence outcomes, long horizon.** Term-to-term retention, completion, stop-out returns — against multi-year baseline, with at least one full year of follow-through. Engagement features reliably produce one good semester of metrics; the mechanism is only visible past the novelty decay.
2. **Flag-to-human latency.** What fraction of disengagement flags produced a personal human contact, and how fast? This is the single number that distinguishes the evidence layer from the surveillance layer — and most platforms do not report it because most deployments would fail it.
3. **Goodhart audit.** Sample the high-engagement-score students: does logged effort correspond to actual work produced, unassisted? Streaks and minutes that outrun work quality are theater being purchased at dashboard prices.
4. **Practice change, not module completion.** For PD programs: classroom observation evidence that taught practices appear and persist, plus coaching-cycle records — the components the d = 0.72 actually rewards.

What does not count: login totals, time-on-platform, streaks, badges, nudge open rates, module completion percentages, or engagement-score rankings against sites running theater at full volume.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Pick one effort-adjacent system in your setting — an early-warning dashboard, an engagement platform, a PD program. One sentence each: the mechanism (the bargain that makes trying rational, and the person who notices), the safe AI support, the forbidden substitution, and the proof that learning happened.
2. **Trace your last ten flags.** If you run any early-warning system, pull the last ten alerts and audit each: did a human personally respond, how fast, and what changed? Your flag-to-human ratio is this chapter's diagnosis of your deployment — detection layer, or surveillance layer.
3. **Find the counterfeit care in your stack.** List every automated message your institution currently sends students in a motivational register — reminders, congratulations, miss-you notes. For each, ask the counselor's question: what does it cost when the student realizes no one sent it? Rewrite one of them as a flag-to-human workflow instead, and price the difference honestly.

## What Would Change My Mind

The chapter's line — automate detection, never response — rests on the claim that automated motivational contact decays and, when discovered, corrodes trust in exactly the populations that depend most on its sincerity. A strong longitudinal trial showing automated nudge systems sustaining persistence gains past novelty, *in high-risk populations*, with trust measures intact after disclosure, would move the line: the response layer would open to hybrid designs. The nudge literature shows real short-term effects, so the possibility is live; what's missing is the duration and the disclosure test. On the PD side: if scaled AI-coached teacher development — model-in-the-loop observation feedback, no human coach — produced classroom practice change matching human coaching cycles, the most expensive component of the d = 0.72 would be optional, and the implications for under-resourced systems would be too large to dismiss out of loyalty to the human version. I would want the observation evidence, not the satisfaction surveys.

## Still Puzzling

- Effort's correlational core never fully resolves: when a Crossroads student persists, how much was the noticing, the calibrated work, the visible progress — and how much selection among those who walked in the door? Practitioners cannot run the counterfactual, and the platform's arrival makes the attribution *harder*, since it improves several terms of the bargain at once.
- Detection has a dignity cost even when response is human: some students experience the advisor's well-timed Tuesday check-in, sourced from a latency flag, as care — others, on learning its origin, as being managed. Whether transparency about the flagging strengthens or weakens the noticing's relational value is an empirical question nobody at any vendor is asking.
- The grant dashboard problem generalizes and has no local solution: as long as funders consume engagement scores, institutions that refuse effort theater will rank below institutions that run it. The fix is upstream, in what funders count.

- **Kraft, M. A., Blazar, D., & Hogan, D. (2018). The effect of teacher coaching on instruction and achievement: A meta-analysis. *Review of Educational Research*, 88(4), 547–588.** The evidence that the relational components of teacher development carry the weight — and the sobering scale-up findings that explain why content libraries keep failing to replicate them.
- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49(4), 241–253.** The benchmark discipline for both rows: what real persistence and PD interventions deliver, and why dashboard-decorated d values deserve a second read.
- **Wigfield, A., & Eccles, J. S. (2000). Expectancy-value theory of achievement motivation. *Contemporary Educational Psychology*, 25(1), 68–81.** The engine under the effort row: doable, worth it, and costly to abandon — the three terms every intervention here is actually adjusting.
- **Bastani, H., et al. (2025). Generative AI can harm learning. *PNAS*.** Read here for its quietest implication: effort is the variable unguardrailed assistance removes — which makes the disposition this chapter protects the upstream defense for every other chapter's gate.
- **U.S. Department of Education, Office of Educational Technology (2023). *Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Teaching and Learning*.** The policy treatment of early-warning systems and automated communication, including the human-response commitments this chapter writes into configuration memos.
