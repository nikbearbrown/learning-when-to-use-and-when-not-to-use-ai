# Chapter 1 — Hattie's Visible Learning and the AI Question

## The Decision on the Table

Dana Whitfield, director of curriculum and instruction for a 22,000-student district, has forty minutes before the board's technology committee convenes, and a vendor deck open on her laptop. Slide 7 is the one the superintendent flagged. It shows a single number in a font size usually reserved for stadium scoreboards: **d = 0.70**. Underneath: *"Feedback is one of the most powerful influences on student achievement — John Hattie, Visible Learning. Our platform delivers personalized AI feedback to every student, on every assignment, instantly."*

The board likes the slide. The board has heard of Hattie. Two members have the bar-graph poster of his effect sizes hanging in their offices. The vendor's logic seems airtight: feedback has one of the largest effect sizes ever synthesized in education research; this product automates feedback; therefore the district should buy the product. The superintendent's note to Dana reads, in full: *"This checks out, right?"*

It does not check out. But the reasons it doesn't are not the reasons most people reach for first — not "AI bad," not "Hattie debunked," not "we can't afford it." The deck fails at a precise point: it treats an effect size as a property of a *product category* when it was a measurement of a *learning mechanism*. The 0.70 was synthesized from studies of feedback that changed what a teacher did next and what a learner understood about their own work — almost all of it predating large language models. Whether this platform touches that mechanism, replaces it, or quietly deletes it is exactly the question the slide is designed to keep anyone from asking.

This chapter gives you the tools to ask it. By the end you will be able to read an effect size without being misled by it, carry the serious critiques of Hattie's project honestly without surrendering its usefulness, understand the seven-dimension classification this book layers on top of his list — including the one column you must never mistake for evidence — and write the device the whole book turns on: a phase gate that states, before adoption, what AI may do, what humans must do, and what would count as proof that learning happened.

## What Visible Learning Is — and Which List This Book Uses

In 2009 John Hattie published *Visible Learning*, a synthesis of more than 800 meta-analyses covering roughly 50,000 individual studies of what influences student achievement. A meta-analysis combines many studies of one intervention into a single summary effect; Hattie's project was a meta-*meta*-analysis — combining the combinations — and it produced something education had never had: one ranked list, from the most powerful influences on achievement down to the actively harmful ones, all expressed in a common unit.

The list has grown by accretion ever since: 138 influences in 2009, 150 in *Visible Learning for Teachers* (2012), 195 by 2015, 252 in the 2018 list, and 357 across eleven domains in *Visible Learning: The Sequel* (2023), which synthesizes more than 2,100 meta-analyses. The numbers attached to individual influences drift between editions as new meta-analyses are averaged in — a fact that will matter a great deal in the next chapter.

**This book freezes on the 2018 list of 252 influences.** Every effect size quoted in these pages, every rank, every group chapter is anchored to that snapshot. We say this now, once, plainly, so that when a colleague objects "but the Sequel says 357" or "MetaX shows a different decimal," you can answer: yes, the franchise has moved; this book deliberately holds one edition still so that its second layer of analysis — the AI classification — has a stable object to classify. The argument of the book does not depend on any particular decimal surviving the next edition. It depends on mechanisms, which are far more durable than meta-analytic averages.

One more thing about what the list *is*, because the misreading starts here. The 252 entries are not 252 things you can buy, or even 252 things you can do. The list mixes **interventions** (worked examples, phonics instruction), **practices** (feedback, questioning), **psychological conditions** (self-efficacy, boredom), **background factors** (socio-economic status, relative age within a class), **structural variables** (school size, tracking), and **harms** (corporal punishment, retention). "Deafness" appears on the list at d = −0.61. Nobody is proposing an intervention called deafness; the entry describes an association between a condition and measured achievement. A reader who treats the list as a menu has already made the category error that this entire book exists to prevent — and that AI vendors, as we will see, are commercially motivated to encourage.

## How to Read an Effect Size Without Being Misled

An effect size — Cohen's *d* — expresses a difference between groups, or growth over time, in standard-deviation units. If a class of students scores half a standard deviation higher after an intervention than a comparison group, that's d = 0.50. The unit's virtue is that it lets you put a reading intervention and a math intervention on the same scale. Its vice is that it tempts you to believe the scale means the same thing everywhere. It does not.

### The two rulers

Jacob Cohen, who invented the convention, proposed rough benchmarks in the 1980s: 0.2 is small, 0.5 medium, 0.8 large. Hattie's hinge sits inside that frame: since the average effect across his 2009 corpus was about 0.40, he argued, almost everything "works" relative to doing nothing — so the interesting question is what works *best*, and 0.40 marks the boundary between above-average and below-average impact. He loosely equated 0.40 with about a year's typical growth.

Then in 2020, Matthew Kraft published a paper that practitioners should staple to every vendor deck they receive. Examining effect sizes from well-designed field experiments — randomized trials, real schools, standardized achievement outcomes — Kraft found the realistic distribution sits far lower: on that evidence, **less than 0.05 is small, 0.05 to 0.20 is medium, and 0.20 or above is large**. A d of 0.20 from a year-long randomized trial on a state assessment is a genuinely big deal. The same 0.20 inside Hattie's synthesis looks like a failure.

These are two different rulers, built from two different bodies of evidence. Hattie's corpus is dominated by the studies researchers actually publish: often small, brief, with researcher-designed tests administered close to the intervention — all conditions known to inflate effects. Kraft's benchmarks come from the harder world of large samples, long durations, and distal measures. Neither ruler is wrong; what's wrong is laying one ruler against the other's measurements. A vendor who quotes "Hattie says 0.40 is the minimum meaningful effect" against a rigorous trial showing 0.17 is committing a category error — and so is a skeptic who dismisses Hattie's whole list because field trials rarely hit its averages.

A concrete version of the trap: two interventions both report d = 0.40 — one from ten one-week lab studies with researcher-built tests, the other from three year-long district randomized trials on state assessments. The numbers are identical; the second is worth incomparably more. An effect size does not carry its own pedigree. You have to ask.

### What the hinge is for — and the two abuses

Used properly, d = 0.40 is a **triage signal**. With 252 influences and finite institutional attention, you need a way to decide where to look first, and "above or below the average of everything studied" is a defensible first cut. That is the entire job this book asks the hinge to do: prioritize the reading order.

The abuses come in a matched pair.

**Abuse one: the hinge as verdict.** "Below 0.40 means it doesn't work." Think about what the hinge *is* — roughly the average of all influences studied. By construction, something close to half of everything education does falls below it. If below-the-hinge meant *meaningless*, then half of schooling — including, as we'll see in Chapter 2, worked examples, classroom management, and most educational technology as actually measured — would be meaningless by definition. That is not an empirical finding; it is an artifact of where the average happens to sit. Some popular books on educational technology run precisely this argument, wielding the 0.40 cutoff as a guillotine. This book declines the guillotine in both directions: we will not let the hinge condemn the below, and we will not let it canonize the above.

**Abuse two: the hinge as shopping list.** "Above 0.40 means buy a tool for it." But effect sizes attach to influences as studied — usually human-delivered, in particular conditions, on particular learners. A product claiming an influence's effect size is claiming credit for studies it was not part of. The hinge also says nothing about cost, scalability, equity, or implementation — the four things adoption decisions actually turn on. And the equation "0.40 ≈ one year's growth" is itself contested: effects run systematically larger for younger students (Hattie himself has suggested expecting d above 0.60 in early grades versus 0.30 for older students), partly because older students' scores are more spread out, which mechanically shrinks d for identical learning. The hinge moves under your feet depending on who was studied.

Hold this rule for the rest of the book: **an effect size is a prioritization signal, never a verdict.** It tells you where to direct your scarce attention. It cannot tell you what to buy, what to cut, or what will happen in your building.

## The Critiques, Carried Honestly

If this book is going to use Hattie's list for thirty-eight more chapters, it owes you the strongest case against doing so — stated before the using, not footnoted after. The critical literature is real, technical, and in important respects correct.

**Bergeron and Rivard (2017)** published, in the *McGill Journal of Education*, a paper whose title does not hedge: "How to Engage in Pseudoscience with Real Data." Their argument, made by statisticians: Cohen's d cannot serve as a universal impact ruler across studies with different populations, different measures, and different designs, and averaging such d's into rankings borrows the *form* of science without satisfying its assumptions. It became the journal's most-viewed article. The charge stings because the statistical core of it is right — which is exactly why this book quarantines every effect size behind the two-rulers lesson above.

**The CLE errors.** Alongside Cohen's d, the 2009 book reported a second statistic, the Common Language Effect size — and many of those values were mathematically impossible: negative probabilities, probabilities above 100 percent. Hattie had used z-values directly where probabilities belonged. Readers flagged it around 2011; Hattie acknowledged the error, and reprints were corrected. Two things are simultaneously true and must be held together. First: **the CLE errors do not contaminate the d-based rankings** — the rankings never rested on that statistic. The speedometer was broken; the odometer was fine. Second: a synthesis offered as the foundation for evidence-based practice shipped with basic statistical errors that no reviewer caught, and that fact is permanently relevant to how much deference the enterprise deserves. Not zero deference. Not full deference. Adult deference.

**Simpson (2017)**, in the *Journal of Education Policy*, supplied the mechanism behind Bergeron and Rivard's charge: an effect size is a ratio, and researchers can enlarge it without making an intervention any better — shrink the denominator by studying restricted samples, sharpen the numerator with proximal researcher-built measures. League tables of effect sizes, Simpson concluded, may really be hierarchies of *openness to research-design manipulation*. **Slavin (2018)** made the practitioner version of the same point bluntly: the meta-meta-analysis accepts its underlying meta-analyses uncritically, and those are dominated by small, brief, artificial studies that inflate effects. **Wecker and colleagues (2016/2017)**, in a German-language methodological review, concluded that the comprehensive-integration ambition is "visionary" but not currently achievable in a scientifically sound manner, leaving a large share of the findings open to reasonable doubt. And a sociological literature (McKnight and Whitburn, 2020, among others) warns about Visible Learning as *brand* — about what institutions do when a ranked list acquires the authority of scripture and the marketing budget of a franchise.

There is also a critique internal to the averaging itself, and it is the one this book leans on hardest: **a single number per influence averages over moderators that can reverse the effect**. Hattie's own feedback synthesis acknowledges that roughly one-third of feedback effects in the underlying literature are *negative*. The d = 0.70 on the vendor's slide is the mean of a distribution that includes a fat tail of feedback that made things worse. Practitioners do not live in the mean; they live in the distribution. Chapter 2 will show this dynamic moving a famous influence's public number by twenty points without any underlying study changing.

### So why use the list at all?

Here is the book's stance, stated once and relied on everywhere: **the list is unusable as a measurement instrument and usable as a map.** It is the most comprehensive record in existence of which mechanisms educators have cared enough about to study, with a rough — design-confounded, moderator-averaged, but not random — signal of where the strong mechanisms cluster. Used as a prioritization map, it tells you where to dig. Used as a shopping list, it tells you lies.

Run the stress test on the list's own #1: collective teacher efficacy, d = 1.57. Critics note the figure is correlational, definitionally fuzzy, and almost certainly inflated — fair on all counts. Now halve it. Halve it again. The decision-relevant content — *shared professional trust among teachers is strongly associated with achievement, and it cannot be purchased as software* — survives untouched, because that content lives in the mechanism, not the decimal. Every chapter in this book is built to pass that test; if a chapter's guidance would change because an effect size fell from 0.79 to 0.40, the chapter is built wrong. When the colleague who has read Bergeron and Rivard moves to dismiss the whole framework — and that meeting is practically a rite of committee life — the honest reply is: concede the statistics, all of them, then ask whether the *mechanism claims* still stand at half the published magnitude. They do. Proceed on those.

## The Man on the Slide Co-Wrote the Caution

There is a fact Dana Whitfield can bring to the board meeting that reframes the entire vendor deck, and it fits in one sentence: **the researcher being quoted at the board co-authored a paper about minimizing AI's damage to education.**

In 2023, Arran Hamilton, Dylan Wiliam, and John Hattie released a working paper titled "The Future of AI in Education: 13 Things We Can Do to Minimize the Damage." The title is not ironic. The paper argues that without deliberate intervention, AI could diminish human agency and stifle the development of the very capabilities schooling exists to build; its recommendations include risk-based access restrictions for children, auditability and guardrails, and AI self-disclosure. Whatever one's view of every recommendation, the rhetorical situation is unambiguous: vendors quote Hattie's effect sizes as a license for AI products, while Hattie's own published position on AI is co-authored caution.

His longer record points the same direction. Across decades of syntheses, technology's measured effect on achievement has hovered stubbornly around 0.3 — below his own hinge — through film strips, computers, laptops, and the internet. The 2018 list's ICT entry (d = 0.47) carries, in this book's dataset, the sternest note in all 252 rows: a pre-LLM aggregate that must not be cited as evidence for modern AI products. And the technology findings he does endorse concentrate where this book will keep landing: technology helps when it amplifies feedback, supports focus, and consolidates prior learning — not when it delivers first-exposure instruction or replaces the teacher's judgment.

None of this makes Hattie an authority on large language models, and this book will not quote him as one. The point is narrower: the gap between the research and the sales deck is visible *within the work of the single most-quoted researcher in the genre*. If the man on the slide doesn't endorse the syllogism on the slide, the syllogism needs a better defense than his name.

## The Seven Dimensions: This Book's Own Layer — Read the Label

Hattie's list answers one question: what is associated with achievement? It cannot answer the question Dana actually faces: *what should AI be allowed to do here?* To get from the first question to the second, this book adds a classification layer — and before describing it, an act of labeling that the entire book's credibility depends on.

**The seven-dimension framework is the author's analytical synthesis. It is not published research. It is not Hattie. It is a structured set of judgments, applied row by row to all 252 influences, and every one of those judgments is open to challenge.** When this book says "summarization is classified AI-NATIVE with conflict present," the verb is *classified* — by the author, on stated reasoning — not *found*, *shown*, or *proven*. The framework's value is that it makes the judgments explicit, consistent, and arguable, which is more than can be said for the implicit judgments embedded in every procurement decision that never names its assumptions.

The seven dimensions, for each influence:

1. **Effect-size band** — High (d ≥ 0.70), Above hinge (0.40–0.69), Below hinge (0.15–0.39), Negligible (0.01–0.14), Harmful (< 0). Hattie's number, banded; the only dimension inherited from the source.
2. **AI feasibility** — the load-bearing taxonomy:
   - **AI-NATIVE**: current AI can perform the core task itself (generate worked examples, summarize a text, produce practice items).
   - **AI-AUGMENTED**: AI can strengthen the influence — planning, drafting, diagnosis, feedback at scale — while humans retain the decisive cognitive or relational work.
   - **AI-ADJACENT**: the influence is mostly context, condition, or structure (SES, school size, prior achievement); AI can read the signal but cannot pull the lever.
   - **AI-RESISTANT**: the active ingredient is relationship, presence, trust, or collective agency; AI can support the perimeter but must not touch the mechanism.
3. **Human irreducibility** — how much of the mechanism consists of things this series argues machines cannot reliably supply: relationship, judgment, social presence, embodiment, metacognition, accountability.
4. **Substitution risk** — the probability that AI use *replaces* the learning-producing act while improving the visible artifact.
5. **Mechanism conflict** — whether the way AI tools naturally operate contradicts the reason the influence works (AI summarizes → the student never compresses; AI answers → the student never retrieves).
6. **Vendor activity** — how aggressively the influence is currently being productized, which is a risk signal, not a quality signal.
7. **Hypothetical AI effect** — and here, stop.

### The [HYPOTHESIS] column

The seventh column attaches a number to a guess: *if* AI were deployed on this influence under the stated conditions, what effect might plausibly result? Every populated cell in that column carries the tag **[HYPOTHESIS]** — for the feedback row it reads "d ≈ 0.50 [HYPOTHESIS]: AI fast task-level feedback is legitimate; relational and self-regulation feedback must be human."

Learn to read that label now, because the failure mode is predictable and ugly. Those numbers look exactly like the real effect sizes sitting six columns to their left. They are not the same kind of object. The d = 0.70 is a (flawed, averaged, but real) summary of measured studies. The d ≈ 0.50 [HYPOTHESIS] is the author's calibrated conjecture — a way of stating, with falsifiable precision, what the mechanism analysis predicts. It exists to be tested by your pilots, not quoted in your slide decks. If you ever catch this book's hypothetical numbers circulating as findings — in a grant application, a vendor pitch, a strategic plan — you will be watching the exact pathology this chapter teaches you to detect, performed with this book as the ammunition. The evidence-language discipline holds throughout: measured results are *associated with*; classifications are *classified as*; the seventh column is *hypothesized*. Nothing in that column is ever a finding.

### What the classification reveals

Run the layer across all 252 influences and a structure appears that no tool-by-tool debate ever surfaces. AI-ADJACENT is the largest class (97 influences, 38.5%) — a plurality of Hattie's list consists of context and conditions no tool can buy you out of. AI-AUGMENTED covers 88 (34.9%), AI-RESISTANT 38 (15.1%), and AI-NATIVE — the zone where AI can actually perform the task — just 29 influences, 11.5%.

Now restrict to the 122 influences at or above the hinge: 81 are AI-AUGMENTED, 18 AI-RESISTANT, 14 AI-NATIVE, 9 AI-ADJACENT. In the High band (d ≥ 0.70, 32 influences), 24 are AI-AUGMENTED and only 4 AI-NATIVE.

Read that distribution slowly, because it is the structural finding of the whole book: **the high-impact end of Hattie's list is augmentation territory, not replacement territory.** The things most strongly associated with achievement are overwhelmingly things AI can *support* — and only rarely things AI can *do*. The AI-native zone is small, mostly procedural, and not concentrated at the top. Whatever the right AI strategy for a school is, the data's shape says it cannot mainly be a substitution strategy.

### One row, end to end

Walk the feedback row — the vendor's slide — through all seven dimensions. Feedback: d = 0.70, High band. AI-AUGMENTED: AI can genuinely accelerate task-level feedback. Medium human irreducibility: Hattie and Timperley's (2007) model locates feedback's power across task, process, and self-regulation levels, and the upper levels are relational and metacognitive. **High substitution risk. Partial mechanism conflict**: instant AI feedback enables hint-clicking — students gaming the system for answers instead of using feedback to adjust their own process. **High vendor activity** — the most-cited claim in EdTech marketing. And the dataset's note records what the marketing strips out: about one-third of feedback effects in the research are negative.

Seven columns convert "feedback works, buy the platform" into something a professional can act on: *task-level AI feedback is legitimate and valuable; process and self-regulation feedback must remain human; the failure mode is system-gaming; the risk is highest precisely because the sales pressure is highest.* That sentence — not the 0.70 — is what Dana takes to the board.

And the same seven columns force *different* answers for influences a generic AI conversation would lump together. Summarization (d = 0.79): AI-NATIVE, conflict present — AI performs the task superbly, which is exactly the danger, because the student who receives the summary skips the compression that was the learning. Teacher clarity (d = 0.75): AI-AUGMENTED, no conflict — drafting clearer goals and sequences with AI takes nothing from the learner. Teacher-student relationships (d = 0.52): AI-RESISTANT, conflict present — the relationship *is* the mechanism; AI's legitimate role is freeing the teacher's time to have it. Three influences, one excited vendor category, three opposite adoption rules. That discrimination — not enthusiasm, not refusal — is the skill this book teaches.

## Substitution Risk, Mechanism Conflict, and the Phase Gate

Two of the seven dimensions do most of the protective work, and they need precise definitions because everything else in the book builds on them.

**Substitution risk** is the risk that AI improves the visible artifact while deleting the invisible learning event. The essay gets better; the writer doesn't. The summary is accurate; nobody compressed anything. The homework is correct; nothing consolidated. Substitution is dangerous precisely because every observable signal improves while the one thing that matters — change in the learner — quietly stops happening.

**Mechanism conflict** is the structural version: the way the tool works contradicts the reason the influence works. Summarization works *because* the learner selects and compresses; a tool that selects and compresses on the learner's behalf is not supporting the influence, it is impersonating it. When this book flags "conflict present," it means the tool's default behavior and the influence's active ingredient are fighting over the same cognitive act.

The empirical anchor for both concepts — the study this book will return to more than any other — is Bastani and colleagues' randomized field experiment, published in *PNAS* in 2025. Nearly a thousand high-school students practiced math under three conditions. Students with unrestricted GPT-4 access performed **48 percent better during practice** — and then **17 percentage points worse on the subsequent unassisted exam** than students who had practiced with no AI at all. The help helped until the help left. But the third arm is the one that matters for policy: students using a *guardrailed* tutor — built on the same model, but designed to give hints and feedback without surrendering answers — practiced 127 percent better with **approximately zero harm** on the exam. Same technology. Opposite outcomes. The difference was nothing but design: a specification of what the AI would and would not do.

That specification has a name in this book: the **phase gate**. Three blanks, filled in before adoption, in writing:

> **AI may do X. The teacher and learner must do Y. Evidence that learning improved is Z.**

X enumerates the safe support roles. Y names the cognitive and relational work that constitutes the mechanism — the work that may never cross to the machine. And Z is the discipline most adoptions skip: evidence of *learning*, defined in advance, and defined correctly. In-tool analytics — usage minutes, completion rates, scores on assisted work — are measures of the artifact and the activity. The Bastani students' practice metrics were spectacular. Z must be **delayed, unassisted, ideally transfer-bearing performance**: what the learner can do later, alone, on something new. If a tool's success cannot be stated in those terms, the tool is asking to grade its own homework.

Surrounding evidence triangulates the design point. VanLehn's (2011) review found step-based intelligent tutoring systems reaching effects around 0.76 — comparable to human tutors, and a standing rebuttal to the folklore that human tutoring sits two sigmas above everything (Bloom's famous benchmark has not survived replication). Wang and colleagues' Tutor CoPilot trial (2024) put AI assistance *inside* the human loop — coaching 900 live tutors in real time — and moved student mastery by 4 percentage points overall and 9 for novice tutors: AI amplifying the human mechanism rather than replacing it. And a working paper by Poulidis, Bastani, and Bastani studying chess academies suggests that self-regulation alone does not prevent over-reliance on AI assistance — structure works, willpower doesn't (working paper; unreviewed — treat accordingly). The pattern across all of it: **outcomes track the division of labor, not the sophistication of the model.** Which is why this book's central artifact is a division-of-labor document.

One cautionary tale to close the loop. A school piloting an AI writing-feedback tool declared the pilot a success on four measures: usage minutes, drafts produced, rubric scores of final essays, teacher satisfaction. Every one is an artifact or activity metric. When week ten brought an unassisted, on-demand writing task, performance was flat against baseline. Nobody had defined Z in advance, so the tool had spent a semester being evaluated on evidence it generated about itself. The pilot didn't fail — the *audit* failed. It audited the artifact. The phase gate exists so that the question "did anyone learn anything?" is locked in writing before the first license is purchased, while everyone is still sober.

## How to Use the Thirty-Eight Group Chapters

The 252 influences cluster into 38 groups sharing a classification signature, and Chapters 2 through 39 walk them — Chapter 2 takes the full below-hinge bucket; then the recurring patterns (safe augmentation, metacognitive gates, AI-native practice with warnings, AI-resistant social mechanisms); then the high-stakes single influences: collective teacher efficacy, feedback, summarization, note taking, discussion, writing.

Do not read this book cover to cover unless you want to. It is built as a decision manual: when a tool, pitch, or pilot lands on your desk, find the influence it claims to serve, go to that group's chapter, and run the protocol. Every group chapter has the same anatomy — a decision moment, the mechanism analysis, the safe AI roles, the substitution trap, a case study, the phase gate, the evidence check, exercises — so the book rewards consultation, not just reading.

The protocol itself is four questions. They are the whole method, compressed:

1. **What is the influence, and what makes it work?** Name the mechanism — the specific cognitive, relational, or organizational act that produces the learning. If you cannot name it, stop; nothing downstream can be evaluated.
2. **What may AI do here?** Which feasibility class, which support roles strengthen the mechanism rather than impersonating it?
3. **What must humans keep?** Where is the substitution risk; which acts, if delegated, delete the learning event?
4. **What is the evidence Z?** What delayed, unassisted, transfer-bearing performance would show learning improved — and is anyone committed to collecting it?

The chapter deliverable operationalizes the four questions as a one-page **AI adoption audit**: influence → effect size (with band and ruler) → mechanism → AI role → human role → learner role → substitution risk → evidence of learning. Dana Whitfield's audit of the vendor deck fits on one page. Influence: feedback. Effect size: d = 0.70, Hattie 2018 list — synthesized from largely human-delivered, pre-LLM studies; not a property of any product. Mechanism: feedback that changes the learner's next action, at task, process, and self-regulation levels. AI role: instant task-level feedback, drafted comments for teacher review, error-pattern surfacing. Human role: process and self-regulation feedback; deciding what the feedback means and what happens next. Learner role: acting on feedback — revising, not regenerating. Substitution risk: high — hint-clicking; feedback consumed as answers. Evidence of learning: performance on delayed, unassisted tasks, agreed before purchase; in-tool analytics explicitly excluded. Recommendation: no purchase from the deck; a one-semester gated pilot with Z defined in the contract.

Forty minutes was enough. It usually is, once you know the four questions.

## What Would Change My Mind

This chapter rests on claims that evidence could overturn, and honesty requires naming them. If well-designed classroom trials accumulated showing that *unguardrailed* AI assistance produces durable gains on delayed, unassisted, transfer-bearing measures across contexts — the direct negation of the Bastani pattern — this book's substitution-risk architecture would be overbuilt, and its central caution wrong in the most consequential way. If the early generative-AI outcome literature matures and its effects hold up large and general under skeptical reads (current meta-analytic estimates run from strikingly large to modest once study quality is weighted — the literature is young, heterogeneous, and vendor-adjacent), the augmentation-only reading of the high band would need loosening. And if the author's classifications fail in the field — if influences tagged AI-RESISTANT keep yielding to well-designed tools without mechanism loss — then the seven dimensions are miscalibrated and should be revised row by row, in public. The framework is a hypothesis about where the boundaries sit. Hypotheses are for testing.

## Still Puzzling

- **Where exactly does augmentation tip into substitution?** The book draws the line at "who performs the cognitive act," but real tools sit on gradients — a hint is a partial answer; a draft comment is partial composition. The Bastani guardrail result shows the line exists; no one has mapped its position across task types.
- **Can the hypothetical column be tested at scale?** The [HYPOTHESIS] estimates are falsifiable in principle. A consortium of districts running gated pilots with pre-registered Z measures could start confirming or demolishing them within a few years. Nothing like that infrastructure currently exists.
- **What does the hinge mean after AI?** Every effect size in the 252 list was synthesized in a pre-LLM world. If AI changes the cost structure of entire influence categories, the rankings' practical meaning shifts even where the numbers stand still. The next edition of the franchise will not settle this; the field's measurement conventions will have to.

This book uses the list anyway — but only in ways that survive this paper.

Working paper.** Co-authored caution from the most-quoted name in the genre. Unreviewed working paper — but indispensable context for any meeting where Hattie's numbers are used to sell AI.

- **Hattie, J., & Timperley, H. (2007). The power of feedback. *Review of Educational Research*, 77(1), 81–112.** What the 0.70 on the vendor slide was actually a measurement of: feedback as a multi-level mechanism that changes the learner's next action, not a product category.
