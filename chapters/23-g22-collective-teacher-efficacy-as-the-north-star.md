# Chapter 23 — G22: Collective Teacher Efficacy as the North Star

## The Decision on the Table

Dr. Lenore Adeyemi, assistant superintendent for instruction in the Brookfield district, watches the vendor's opening slide resolve on the boardroom screen, and it is the slide she knew was coming: a bar chart of Hattie's top influences with the tallest bar glowing. *Collective teacher efficacy: d = 1.57. The number one influence on student achievement. Now measurable, trackable, and improvable — with EfficacyIQ.*

The product is a collective efficacy dashboard. Quarterly staff surveys feed building-level "efficacy scores"; AI analyzes open-response items for "belief signals"; principals get heat maps and benchmark comparisons against districts the vendor declines to name; an AI assistant generates "efficacy-building communications" — celebration messages for staff — and recommends "high-impact efficacy interventions" from a template library. The superintendent is interested. Two board members have read the Hattie bar chart somewhere and arrived already persuaded that 1.57 is the district's most underexploited resource. The pitch deck's logic is a clean syllogism: the research says collective efficacy is the most powerful influence; you cannot improve what you do not measure; therefore, measure it with us.

Lenore's problem is that she half-agrees with the premise and entirely distrusts the conclusion, and she has one week to articulate the difference. The largest number in the dataset is on the table, attached to the proposal least capable of producing it. This is the book's sharpest exhibit on substitution risk, and the substitution on offer is the subtlest kind: not a machine doing students' thinking, but a dashboard doing a faculty's believing.

## What This Group Is

One influence, classified: High band, **AI-RESISTANT**, **high human irreducibility**, **high substitution risk**, no cognitive-work conflict. Effect size 1.57 — the top of the 2018 252-influence list, rank 1.

The signature deserves a slow read, because it is unique in the book. AI-RESISTANT plus high irreducibility says what Chapters 7, 12, and 22 said about relational mechanisms: the active ingredient is human. But the high substitution risk is unusual *for* an AI-resistant row — Chapter 22's embodied programs carried low substitution risk because nobody believes a chatbot is a campfire. Here the dataset flags the opposite condition: institutions desperately *want* this one to be automatable, because the influence is expensive in the currency districts have least of — sustained collaborative time, leadership credibility, organizational trust — and the number attached to it is large enough to justify almost any purchase that claims it. The dataset's own hypothesis about what survives automation is the bluntest in the classification: d ≈ 0.15 [HYPOTHESIS] — AI dashboard only; collective trust cannot be automated. From 1.57 to 0.15: the largest hypothesized collapse in the book — the dashboard keeps the measurement and deletes the mechanism.

And no cognitive-work conflict — correctly: the work at risk here is not cognitive but social and organizational, the slow construction of a faculty's shared, evidence-grounded belief in its own capability. AI does not contradict that work. It simply cannot perform it, and the danger is purchasing the performance anyway.

## The Influences

**Collective teacher efficacy (d = 1.57)** — rank 1 of 252. This chapter does not get to quote that number and move on; the number's provenance is the chapter.

The construct: a faculty's shared belief that *we — this staff, together — can produce learning in our students, whatever they arrive with*. Not individual confidence aggregated, but a group-level property, in Bandura's social-cognitive lineage: such beliefs shape what a group attempts, how long it persists, how it reads setbacks. The school-level research program began with Goddard, Hoy, and Woolfolk Hoy around 2000, finding collective efficacy strongly associated with achievement differences between schools, even controlling for demographics — in correlational designs. The headline d traces to Eells's 2011 dissertation, a meta-analysis of *correlational* studies relating measured collective efficacy to achievement; Donohoo's books and Hattie's platform carried the converted figure into every leadership workshop.

Now the honesty ledger, in full, because the case turns on it:

- **The 1.57 is a converted correlation, not an intervention effect** [contested]. No one assigned schools to high-efficacy and low-efficacy conditions and measured the achievement difference. The figure summarizes how strongly two measured variables travel together, translated into d-units that invite — but do not license — comparison with intervention rows. The conversion and the construct's ranking have drawn specific, published methodological criticism [contested].
- **The causal arrow runs both ways at minimum.** Faculties whose students achieve have excellent grounds for believing in their collective capability — mastery experience, Bandura's strongest efficacy source, *is* prior success. Some unknown fraction of the 1.57 is achievement causing belief.
- **Confounding is live.** Stable leadership, adequate resources, low staff turnover, and functional culture plausibly raise both efficacy beliefs and achievement.

What survives the ledger is a strong, replicated association plus a credible mechanism with experimental support at the underlying (Bandura) level. The defensible reading: collective efficacy is a *marker and plausible multiplier* of organizational health, well worth cultivating through its known sources — and worth approximately nothing as a number to chase. The district that hears "build the conditions Bandura described" has read the row correctly. The district that hears "acquire 1.57" has bought the bar chart.

## Why It Works

Grant the mechanism its best case and trace it, because the tracing is what exposes the dashboard.

A faculty's shared belief in its collective capability changes behavior through channels any teacher will recognize. Teachers in high-efficacy schools attempt more — demanding instruction, stubborn re-teaching — because effort spent feels causally connected to outcomes. They persist longer through implementation dips — setbacks read as solvable rather than terminal. They attribute student struggle to instruction (actionable) rather than to students (final) — arguably the construct's behavioral core. And they cooperate at higher bandwidth: collective belief makes deprivatized practice feel like shared problem-solving instead of exposure. Note that every channel terminates in *teacher behavior toward students*. Belief is the fuel, not the engine; if the behavior never changes, the belief was decoration.

Where does the fuel come from? Bandura's four sources, and the order matters:

1. **Mastery experience** — the strongest source, and the only one that compounds: the faculty *does something together that works and knows it worked*. A collaboratively planned intervention, run, measured, and seen to move students.
2. **Vicarious experience** — watching credible colleagues succeed with *our* students: peer observation, within-school exemplars.
3. **Social persuasion** — leadership narrative and feedback, real but weak alone, and parasitic on the first two: persuasion without mastery experience is morale poster manufacturing.
4. **Affective state** — the stress and emotional climate the work happens in; chronic overload reads, group-wide, as evidence of incapability.

Every source is experiential and social — had, together, over time, in teams that plan, act, examine evidence, and attribute success to themselves. This is why the influence is AI-resistant: there is no API to mastery experience. And it is why the genuine AI opportunity here is real but strictly *infrastructural*: machines can make the experiences easier to have and the evidence of success easier to see. They cannot have the experience, hold the belief, or be the colleague whose success persuades.

The dashboard's category error is now exact: it instruments source 3 (and thinly — survey language), while doing nothing for sources 1 and 2 and, via survey fatigue and surveillance feel, plausibly degrading source 4. It measures the fuel gauge and calls itself an engine.

## What AI Can Safely Do

The infrastructural list — everything that lowers the cost of the experiences the mechanism actually runs on:

- **Make mastery experiences visible.** Assemble before/after evidence of team-driven instructional changes: the assessment data, work samples, and trend lines that let a faculty *see* that what it did worked. Bandura's strongest source has a clerical bottleneck; AI removes it.
- **Prepare the inquiry cycle.** Summarize student work patterns before team meetings, draft the data displays, transcribe and action-list the discussion — so collaborative inquiry runs on teacher judgment instead of teacher clerical stamina.
- **Protect the time.** Scheduling support that finds and defends common planning time — the binding constraint on every source-1 activity in every real school.
- **Lower the cost of vicarious experience.** Logistics for peer observation (coverage, scheduling), and — with consent — clip summaries from lesson video that make colleague success shareable (Chapter 14's rules apply).
- **Equip the leadership narrative.** Draft the celebration of *specific, evidenced* team accomplishments — social persuasion anchored to mastery experience, which is the only kind that compounds. The anchor is the condition; unanchored AI-generated affirmation is the trap one bullet below.

## The Substitution Trap

**The dashboard as the work.** The flagship trap is the purchase itself, reasoned as the vendor's syllogism: the research says efficacy matters → we must measure efficacy → measurement plus encouragement equals improvement. Walk the mechanism and watch it miss: the survey instruments beliefs without touching their sources; the heat map gives principals a number to manage; and Goodhart arrives on schedule — staff learn the survey's stakes and the scores drift up while nothing under them moves. The district now *reports* rising collective efficacy while the d ≈ 0.15 [HYPOTHESIS] reality accrues underneath. The substitution is complete when leadership attention — the scarcest resource the real mechanism needed — is spent on score movement instead of on time, teams, and evidence.

**Synthetic persuasion.** The AI "efficacy-building communications" feature deserves its own autopsy. Social persuasion works through *credibility* — Bandura is explicit — and a faculty that discovers its celebration messages are machine-generated has learned the precise opposite of the intended lesson: leadership cares enough about our beliefs to automate the caring. Chapter 16 called this counterfeit care; here it counterfeits the *weakest* efficacy source while spending the credibility the strongest sources depend on.

**The score as a label.** Building-level efficacy scores, benchmarked and circulated, are Chapter 18's labeling machine scaled to faculties: the "low-efficacy school" tag changes how the district treats the building, how the building reads itself, and what its best teachers do next (they leave). The construct's own logic predicts the harm — tell a group it collectively cannot, and you have administered anti-efficacy through the instrument that claimed to build it.

**Chasing the number itself.** The meta-trap that makes this chapter the book's starkest argument for its evidence-language rule: 1.57 is not an available purchase. By Kraft's benchmarks, honest field interventions clear 0.20 rarely and 0.50 almost never; any proposal promising motion on a 1.57-scale construct, by survey subscription, is quoting a correlational artifact as a product roadmap. The grander the number on the slide, the more certain it is describing something a vendor cannot sell — because the things that large are properties of organizations, grown, not features, shipped.

## Case Study

The leadership team met without the vendor first, which was Lenore's one procedural demand, and the meeting opened with her two-line memo: *The number is real as a correlation, unavailable as a purchase. Proposal: spend the same money on the mechanism and use AI as its clerk.*

**The tool.** EfficacyIQ, declined — and a far less glamorous configuration of AI assistance, adopted. **The influence.** Collective teacher efficacy, d = 1.57, presented to the board with the provenance ledger intact: converted correlation [contested], bidirectional causality, Bandura's sources — and the dataset's d ≈ 0.15 [HYPOTHESIS] automation discount displayed beside the vendor's bar chart, which had a clarifying effect on the room.

**The useful moment.** The redirected pilot ran in Brookfield's two middle schools. The district bought no efficacy product; it bought *time and clerical removal* — common planning periods protected by scheduling, and an AI workflow that prepared each grade-team inquiry cycle: student work summarized before meetings, data displays drafted, decisions transcribed into action lists, and — the load-bearing piece — the before/after evidence assembled at cycle's end so the team could see whether its instructional bet had paid. The first compounding event arrived in March: the seventh-grade team's reteach design for proportional reasoning moved their common assessment visibly, the AI-assembled evidence brief said so in two pages, and the team presented it to the faculty themselves. Mastery experience, made visible, narrated by its owners — source 1 feeding source 2, with the machine carrying chairs.

**The dangerous moment.** Success regrew the appetite. By May, a board member — pleased, and reading the spring survey's modest climate uptick — proposed "scaling what's working" by adopting the dashboard after all, *now* with something to measure. The instinct was sincere and exactly wrong, and Lenore's refusal memo became the district's phase gate: the moment building-level belief scores become managed numbers, the teams' candor — the thing the inquiry cycles run on — becomes the cost of someone's benchmark. The district would track the behaviors and the student outcomes; the beliefs would be *surveyed* annually, anonymously, unbenchmarked, as a thermometer no one is paid to move.

**The decision.** (1) No efficacy dashboard; the construct is monitored, never managed. (2) The mechanism funded directly: protected collaborative time, AI clerical support for inquiry cycles, peer-observation coverage. (3) Leadership communications celebrate specific evidenced team wins, written by the humans claiming the credit. (4) Evidence check fixed: inquiry-cycle completion and quality (did teams act, measure, and attribute?), instructional changes documented per team, student outcomes on the cycles' own measures — and turnover and climate as the slow indicators. The 1.57 appears nowhere in the plan. The sources do.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** assemble the evidence of team impact that mastery experience requires; prepare, transcribe, and action-list collaborative inquiry; defend common time through scheduling; carry the logistics of peer observation; and draft leadership communications that humans anchor to specific, real team accomplishments and send under their own credibility.

**The humans must** do the work the belief is *about* — plan together, teach, examine results together, attribute success to their own collective action — and leadership must protect the time, model the attribution, and refuse to convert belief into a managed metric.

**Evidence that learning improved is** student outcomes on the measures each team's inquiry cycle chose, accumulating across cycles and faculties — with completed act-measure-attribute cycles and documented instructional change as the leading indicators, and anonymous, unbenchmarked belief surveys as a thermometer read once a year and never chased.

## The Evidence Check

For any proposal invoking collective efficacy — and the 1.57 will be on its first slide — demand:

1. **Source mapping.** Which of Bandura's four sources does the proposal actually feed, concretely? A proposal that names none, or only persuasion, is selling fuel-gauge polish.
2. **The behavior chain.** What teacher behaviors toward students will change, and how would anyone know? Belief that never reaches instruction is climate decoration.
3. **Goodhart exposure.** Will any belief measure acquire stakes — benchmarks, dashboards, principal goals? If yes, the measure is already corrupted; the only safe efficacy survey is a low-stakes one.
4. **Provenance honesty.** Does the proposal present 1.57 as an intervention payoff? That is the disqualifying tell — the vendor either does not understand the evidence or is hoping you do not.

What does not count: efficacy-score trendlines, survey participation rates, sentiment-analyzed "belief signals," AI-counted celebration messages, or benchmark position against unnamed districts — every one a measurement of the proxy, several of them a tax on the construct.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Take one culture- or belief-targeted initiative in your setting (efficacy, morale, "climate"). Write one sentence each: the mechanism (Bandura's sources, fed by real shared work), the safe AI support (clerk to the inquiry cycle; evidence assembly), the forbidden substitution (managed scores; synthetic persuasion), and the proof it worked (behavior chains and student outcomes, not survey drift).
2. **Run the source map on your own school.** List the last three genuine mastery experiences your faculty had *as a faculty* — collective bets that ran, got measured, and were seen to work. If the list is hard to make, the problem is not belief measurement, and you have just located where the AI clerk should report on Monday.
3. **Rewrite the vendor slide.** Take the sentence "Collective teacher efficacy is the number one influence (d = 1.57)" and rewrite it in evidence-honest language — construct, correlational basis, conversion caveat, mechanism. Under 60 words. You have just written the disclosure the deck omitted, and the skill transfers to every bar chart you will ever be shown.

## What Would Change My Mind

Two findings, in opposite directions. A rigorous intervention study — schools randomized to a multi-year program feeding Bandura's sources (protected collaboration, structured inquiry, leadership development) — showing achievement effects approaching even a fifth of the headline figure would upgrade this chapter's "marker and plausible multiplier" to "confirmed lever." Conversely, careful causal work showing the efficacy-achievement association is mostly achievement-driven or confound-driven — belief as exhaust, not fuel — would demote this row from north star to weathervane, and the right spend would become the underlying practices with the construct ignored entirely. The honest current position: the dataset's number one influence is the book's least causally secured high-band claim — exactly why it got the [HYPOTHESIS] discipline at full strength.

## Still Puzzling

- Even an anonymous, unbenchmarked annual survey teaches a faculty that its beliefs are of administrative interest, and beliefs observed are beliefs performed, a little. Is there any measurement of collective efficacy that does not begin corrupting it?
- AI clerical support makes inquiry cycles cheaper — but part of source 1's power may be the *effort* of the shared work, the same way this book treats learner effort as mechanism elsewhere. If the machine carries too many chairs, does the experience still read as *ours*? Nobody knows where that line is.
- The construct is grouped here as a school-level property, but AI is changing what "the faculty's collective work" even is. Whether collective efficacy can form *around* AI-saturated practice, or is diluted by it, is a question the literature has not yet thought to ask.

## Further Reading

- **Bandura, A. (1997). *Self-Efficacy: The Exercise of Control*.** The theoretical bedrock: the four sources, the group-level extension, and the credibility conditions on persuasion that the synthetic-celebration feature violates by design.
- **Goddard, R. D., Hoy, W. K., & Woolfolk Hoy, A. (2000). Collective teacher efficacy: Its meaning, measure, and impact on student achievement. *American Educational Research Journal*, 37, 479–507.** The construct's school-level founding study — and an honest model of correlational claims carefully made.
- **Eells, R. J. (2011). *Meta-analysis of the relationship between collective teacher efficacy and student achievement* (doctoral dissertation, Loyola University Chicago).** The source of the 1.57: read it to see exactly what was aggregated — correlations — and therefore what the famous number is and is not.
- **Donohoo, J. (2017). *Collective Efficacy: How Educators' Beliefs Impact Student Learning*.** The practitioner translation that carried the construct into district planning — best read alongside the published critiques of the effect-size conversion [contested].
- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49, 241–253.** The ruler of last resort: what real interventions deliver, and why any slide promising motion on a 1.57 construct has confused a correlation for a catalog item.
