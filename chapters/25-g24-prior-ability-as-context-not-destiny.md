# Chapter 25 — G24: Prior Ability as Context, Not Destiny

## The Decision on the Table

Marisol Vega chairs the master-schedule and placement committee at a comprehensive high school of 1,900 students. Every spring the committee makes hundreds of consequential calls: who goes into Algebra II versus the support section, who gets recommended for AP, which incoming ninth graders land in the "foundations" English block. This year the district's new scheduling platform offers to help. It ingests middle-school records, screener results, and attendance, and produces a "readiness index" for each student, with placement recommendations attached. The sales engineer is careful to say the index "doesn't decide anything — it just informs."

The counselors are cautiously enthusiastic; placement season currently runs on overworked judgment and incomplete folders. But an English teacher on the committee raises her hand: "When the index says a kid isn't ready, who un-says it? And when do we ever check whether the kids it routed into foundations would have been fine in the regular section?" Marisol has until the April board meeting to recommend how the readiness index may and may not be used.

The committee is staring at the strangest entry in the High band of Hattie's list — an influence with one of the largest numbers and no lever attached.

## What This Group Is

This group is a single influence classified: High band, AI-ADJACENT, low human irreducibility, low substitution risk, no mechanism conflict.

That combination is unique among the high-stakes chapters, and translating it is the chapter's first job. AI-ADJACENT means AI works *next to* this influence, not *on* it: there is nothing here for a tool to deliver, because the influence is not a practice or a program — it is a description of what students bring through the door. Low irreducibility and low substitution risk follow from the same fact: there is no learning mechanism inside this influence for AI to hollow out, because there is no mechanism at all. Only information.

That sounds like the safest possible classification. It is not. The risk in this group is not that AI will replace a learning process; it is that an institution will mistake a description for a verdict and let software turn yesterday's record into next year's ceiling. This is an ethics chapter wearing a statistics costume.

## The Influences

**Prior ability (d = 0.94)** — rank 9 on the 2018 252-influence list. The number quantifies the association between what students arrive with and what they later achieve. It is the reason every serious education study *controls for* prior ability or achievement rather than randomizing it: nobody can assign a student a different past. In a well-run trial, prior achievement is the baseline covariate researchers subtract so the treatment effect becomes visible. In Hattie's list, it appears alongside genuine interventions as if it were one of them — a mixing of categories the reader has met before and will meet again.

Two neighboring rows sharpen the picture. The same 2018 list carries **prior achievement at rank 61 (d = 0.55)** — overlapping construct, different number, a useful reminder that effect sizes are sensitive to how a construct is defined and which studies are pooled. And one rank below prior ability sits **"strategy to integrate with prior knowledge" (rank 10, d = 0.93)** — nearly the same number, opposite classification (AI-AUGMENTED), because it describes something teaching *does*. That adjacent pair is the sharpest contrast in the dataset: *who the student is* (read it, plan around it) versus *what teaching does with what the student knows* (work it). The closer a construct sits to what was taught, the more it is a lever; the closer it sits to who the student is, the more it is context.

The misconception to kill on sight: "d = 0.94 means ability determines outcomes, so teaching matters little." The number is an average association, not a ceiling. The entire above-hinge list documents instruction-side variation that operates within every level of prior ability.

## Why It Works

Strictly, the question for this group is not "why does it work?" but "why does it predict?" Prior measures bundle together everything that has already happened to a learner — accumulated knowledge, practiced skills, home opportunity, prior schooling quality, test-taking familiarity — and that bundle carries forward. New learning builds on old learning; that is the most secure finding in cognitive science, and it is why the prediction is strong.

But notice what the bundle means for interpretation. "Prior ability" is partly accumulated *opportunity* wearing the costume of capacity. A student measured after six years in an under-resourced school carries that history in her score. Treat the score as a fixed trait and you convert a history of opportunity into a forecast of limitation. Treat it as a starting-point description and it becomes the most useful planning information a teacher can have. Same number, opposite institutions.

This is also where the influence earns its place in an AI book. Prior-performance data is the fuel of every adaptive platform, placement algorithm, and readiness index on the market. The question is never whether AI will use prior ability — it cannot do anything else — but whether the system treats the measurement as provisional context or as destiny.

## What AI Can Safely Do

Because the classification finds no mechanism to protect, the safe-use list is genuinely substantial:

- **Build the starting-point map.** Synthesize patchy records into a per-class prerequisite picture: "most of this section lacks fraction–decimal equivalence; eight students show algebra readiness." This is context-reading for human planning — the legitimate AI-ADJACENT role in its purest form.
- **Flag prerequisite gaps before a unit**, so diagnosis happens by design rather than by mid-unit surprise.
- **Suggest differentiated entry points** — provisional groupings, alternative on-ramps — that a teacher reviews and that expire on a schedule.
- **Serve as the honest moderator in evaluations.** When the school pilots any AI tool, disaggregate outcomes by prior-achievement tercile instead of reporting one average. The pattern matters: structured tutoring-style support has shown its largest documented gains where baseline capability was weakest (in the Tutor CoPilot study, +9 percentage points for students of lower-rated tutors versus +4 overall), while unstructured AI access has shown the opposite — in Bastani and colleagues' field experiment, unguardrailed GPT-4 inflated practice performance and then *harmed* unassisted exam performance. The same tool is a different intervention at different points of the prior-ability distribution. That is what "context" means operationally.

## The Substitution Trap

The trap here is not substitution of cognitive work. It is the conversion of context into destiny, and it runs through two documented loops.

**The placement loop.** An adaptive platform initializes on prior performance, serves easier content to the low scorer, generates less growth, and then reads the smaller growth as confirmation of the initial estimate. This is a Matthew effect — to those who have, more is given — running at machine speed, the algorithmic descendant of everything the human tracking literature warned about. A path computed from yesterday's deficits can be a slow lane with better graphics.

**The expectation loop.** Dashboard labels shape teacher expectations, and expectations shape behavior toward students — the dynamic education research has documented for decades under "teacher expectations" and "labeling." A readiness index that quietly tags a fourteen-year-old as "not ready" is manufacturing the conditions of its own accuracy.

Call the combined pattern the algorithmic Matthew effect — noting honestly that this framing is an argument assembled from documented components (placement-loop dynamics, expectation effects, predictive analytics inheriting historical bias; see Holstein & Doroudi 2021), not a single cited finding. The audit questions that catch it are concrete: after a student's two bad weeks (illness, a family move), how long until the system re-estimates upward? Is there a human override, and is it ever used? Who reviews placement drift by subgroup each term? If nobody in the building can answer, the platform is manufacturing destiny, whatever the sales engineer says.

A final distinction with ethical teeth: *diagnosing prior knowledge* ("what does this student already know about fractions?") and *measuring prior ability* ("what is this student's ability level?") can run on identical data. The first produces an instructional plan with a short shelf life. The second produces a label with a long institutional one.

## Case Study

Marisol Vega's placement committee piloted the readiness index for one placement season under committee-written rules.

**The tool.** The scheduling platform's readiness index and placement recommendations. **The influence.** Prior ability — the committee named the issue precisely: "this number describes where students start; our decisions decide where they're allowed to go."

**The useful moment.** For incoming ninth graders, the index did something the counselors could not: it synthesized three middle schools' inconsistent records into comparable starting-point profiles, and it surfaced thirty-one students whose strong recent trajectories were buried under weak sixth-grade scores — students a folder-skim would likely have routed into foundations sections. The committee placed most of them in regular sections with a monitoring plan. That is context-reading at its best: better information, human decision.

**The dangerous moment.** In the second week, a counselor processing 400 placements began accepting the index's recommendation wherever it was "obvious" — which in practice meant wherever it matched the prior record, which is to say wherever it mattered most. One case forced the issue: a student with a low index (new to English, two school changes, a bad seventh-grade year) whose math teacher insisted she belonged in honors geometry. The index had no field for "her teacher has watched her reason for a year." The committee realized the recommendation column had quietly become the decision column — the gate crossed not by malice but by workload. They also noticed what the platform never offered: any report on what happened to last year's "not ready" students, or any breakdown of recommendations by subgroup.

**The decision.** Adopt for one more year, with conditions written into the contract renewal: (1) the index informs, humans decide — operationally enforced by removing the one-click "accept recommendation" button and requiring a named staff member on every placement; (2) any placement *below* a student's requested or teacher-recommended level requires a second human reviewer; (3) all support-section placements expire at the semester with a mandatory re-look, so no September estimate can silently become a four-year track; (4) the data office produces a twice-yearly drift report — placement recommendations and overrides broken down by race, language status, and IEP status — reviewed by the committee; (5) the school tracks the "boundary students" (placed against the index's recommendation, in either direction) as the standing test of the index's actual accuracy.

## The Phase Gate

**AI may** aggregate and describe what students bring — synthesizing prior records into starting-point maps, flagging prerequisite gaps, surfacing students whose trajectories contradict their averages, and disaggregating outcome data by prior-achievement band so the school can see who any program is actually serving.

**The teacher and the institution must** decide what the description means and act on it as provisional: choose placements with a named human responsible for each, build in scheduled re-estimation so labels expire, override the index when contextual knowledge contradicts it, and audit the system's recommendations for subgroup drift. The teacher must treat every starting-point map as a plan for the first three weeks, not a forecast of June.

**Evidence that learning improved is** growth measured *relative to the starting point* on assessments outside the platform — plus a second, less obvious record: documented upward revisions of the starting estimates themselves. A school using prior-ability data well will regularly discover students it underestimated. A school whose initial estimates are never revised upward is not measuring ability; it is enforcing it.

## The Evidence Check

Proof, for this group, is mostly proof about the institution rather than the tool:

1. **Growth against baseline, externally measured.** Students placed with the system's help show learning growth on assessments the platform does not control, compared from their own starting points.
2. **Boundary-student outcomes.** Students placed against the index's recommendation succeed at rates that justify the override policy — or fail in ways that teach the school something specific. Either way, the school is checking.
3. **No subgroup drift.** Placement recommendations, overrides, and time-in-support-sections show no widening gaps by race, language status, or disability status across terms.
4. **Estimate revision rates.** A healthy fraction of initial placements are revised — in both directions — at scheduled checkpoints. Zero revisions means the September snapshot has become the curriculum.

What does not count: the index's own accuracy statistics (predicting outcomes the index itself helped produce), "growth within level" reports, or green dashboard checkmarks for students who never saw grade-level work.

## Reader Exercises

1. **The reader audit.** Pick one tool or routine in your setting that reads prior performance — an adaptive platform's initial placement, a screener-driven grouping, a readiness report. Write one sentence each for: mechanism (what the data actually describes), safe AI support, forbidden substitution (where the description could become a verdict), and proof that learning happened.
2. **The lever / context / correlation sort.** Classify each statement: "scaffolding raises achievement"; "prior ability predicts achievement at d = 0.94"; "high-school achievement predicts career performance." One is a lever, one is context, one is a correlation between outcomes. Then find one claim in a vendor brochure or district document and classify it the same way. (This is the same sort the below-hinge chapter taught — now applied at the top of the list, where the stakes are higher.)
3. **Run the destiny audit.** For one adaptive platform or placement process you can actually inspect, answer in writing: How long after a bad stretch does the system re-estimate upward? Who can override it, and how often was the override used last term? Who saw a subgroup drift report, and when? Bring the unanswerable questions to the people who bought the tool.

## What Would Change My Mind

A longitudinal study showing that adaptive-platform initial placements do *not* persist — that students starting in lower-level content streams are re-estimated upward at rates matching their actual capability, across subgroups, without the human-override machinery this chapter prescribes — would substantially soften the placement-loop warning. If well-designed platforms turn out to self-correct faster and less prejudicially than human placement committees (which have their own ugly track record), the right conclusion would be to automate *more* of placement, not less, and this chapter's gate conditions would need inverting.

## Still Puzzling

- The honest unknown of the decade: does AI personalization narrow or widen prior-ability gaps? The early evidence runs both directions depending on design (the Bastani versus Tutor CoPilot pattern), and no settled meta-analytic answer exists yet.
- Where exactly is the boundary between "ability" and "accumulated opportunity"? The dataset keeps prior ability (0.94) and prior achievement (0.55) as separate rows, which is itself an argument that the construct is unstable — but placement decisions cannot wait for the construct debate to resolve.
- No one has located a longitudinal study of LLM-era placement loops on long-run student trajectories. Until one exists, every district running a readiness index is the study.

## Further Reading

- **Holstein, K., & Doroudi, S. (2021). Equity and Artificial Intelligence in Education. arXiv:2104.12920.** The clearest map of how AI systems in education can amplify or reduce existing inequities — including the placement and prediction dynamics this chapter calls the algorithmic Matthew effect.
- **O'Neil, C. (2016). *Weapons of Math Destruction*.** The general theory of the feedback loop: a model trained on the past, used to allocate the future, grading itself on outcomes it shaped. Chapter-length cases that read as warnings for every readiness index.
- **Steele, C. M. (2010). *Whistling Vivaldi*.** What labels do to performance from the inside — the psychological loop that pairs with the algorithmic one. (Note that parts of the stereotype-threat literature have a contested replication record; read for mechanism, not magnitude.)
- **Kraft, M. A. (2020). Interpreting effect sizes of education interventions. *Educational Researcher*, 49, 241–253.** Essential here for the distinction this chapter leans on: what a 0.94 means when it describes a background association rather than an intervention's payoff.
- **Bastani, H., et al. (2025). Generative AI can harm learning. *PNAS*.** The field experiment behind this chapter's equity warning: the same AI access produced gains with guardrails and harm without — design, not technology, decided whether prior differences shrank or compounded.
