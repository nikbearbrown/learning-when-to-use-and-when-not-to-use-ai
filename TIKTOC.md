# PART 1 — BOOK CONCEPT AND THESIS

## Book Concept Summary

> This book teaches **when to use AI and when not to use AI in learning** to **educators, instructional designers, school leaders, AI product evaluators, and serious practitioners** by translating Hattie's Visible Learning influences into reader-facing AI judgment groups. It uses effect size as the prioritization signal, then asks a second question Hattie did not have to answer: **does AI strengthen the mechanism of learning, or does it replace the human cognitive, relational, or pedagogical act that makes the mechanism work?** It succeeds if the reader can look at any proposed AI learning tool, name the educational mechanism it claims to support, identify the substitution risk, and write a practical phase gate before adoption.

**One-sentence logline:**
Use AI where it strengthens the learning mechanism; stop AI where it substitutes for the human work that creates learning.

## Central Thesis

This book argues that AI adoption in education should be governed by mechanism, not novelty. Hattie's Visible Learning gives the reader a map of influences associated with achievement; the AI classification layer tells the reader whether those influences are AI-native, AI-augmented, AI-adjacent, or AI-resistant, and whether AI use creates substitution risk or mechanism conflict. The practical conclusion is neither pro-AI nor anti-AI: AI belongs where it preserves or improves the learning-producing mechanism, and it should be constrained where it removes retrieval, metacognition, relationship, discussion, judgment, effort, authorship, or collective agency.

## Thesis Test

The TOC reflects the thesis at every level:

- **Chapter 1:** Establishes the evidence frame: effect sizes, the d = 0.40 hinge, and why educational mechanisms matter more than tool categories. Pass.
- **G01:** Collapses d < 0.40 influences into an exploration bucket, preventing the book from over-investing in low-impact novelty. Pass.
- **G02–G21:** Shows the major recurring patterns: safe augmentation, metacognitive gates, AI-native practice, AI-resistant social mechanisms, and partial-conflict cases. Pass.
- **G22–G38:** Treats high-stakes single-influence groups as precise decision chapters: collective teacher efficacy, feedback, summarization, writing, ICT, note taking, discussion, and parent involvement. Pass.
- **Every group chapter:** Ends with an explicit phase gate: AI may do X; teacher/learner must do Y; evidence of learning is Z. Pass.

**Thesis test: PASS**

# PART 2 — LEARNER PROFILE

## Primary Reader

The primary reader is an education professional who is being asked to make AI decisions before the field has stabilized: a teacher leader, instructional designer, principal, district curriculum director, faculty developer, learning engineer, or AI committee member. They have seen both sides of AI: better drafts, faster feedback, adaptive practice, and useful planning support; also student shortcutting, shallow fluency, hallucinated outputs, vendor overclaiming, and institutional pressure to adopt tools before anyone can explain the learning mechanism.

**Specific person:** A district instructional leader in 2026 who has been asked to evaluate AI writing tutors, AI feedback systems, AI lesson-planning tools, and AI tutoring platforms. They know Hattie or have heard of Visible Learning, but they need a usable decision framework, not another abstract debate about whether AI is good or bad.

## Prior Knowledge Assumed

- Basic familiarity with teaching, curriculum, assessment, or learning design
- General awareness that Hattie's Visible Learning uses effect sizes
- Basic experience with generative AI tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or education-specific AI products
- Comfort reading short tables and classification labels

## Prior Knowledge NOT Assumed

- Statistical training beyond a plain-language understanding of effect size
- Deep familiarity with every Hattie influence
- Machine learning or AI engineering expertise
- Prior knowledge of phase gates, substitution risk, or mechanism conflict
- Agreement with every Hattie ranking or meta-analytic choice

## Prior Misconceptions

1. **"High effect size means buy a tool for it."** A high effect size points to a powerful influence, not a product category.
2. **"If AI can do the task, AI should do the task."** Some tasks are the learning mechanism; outsourcing them destroys the benefit.
3. **"AI-resistant means no AI."** AI may still support observation, coordination, documentation, or preparation.
4. **"AI-native means safe."** AI-native practices can carry high substitution risk, especially summarization, writing, tutoring, and testing.
5. **"Low effect means useless."** Below-hinge influences may still matter locally, but they should not dominate strategic AI adoption.
6. **"Human irreducibility is sentimental."** In the dataset it names mechanisms like teacher credibility, classroom cohesion, collective efficacy, peer influence, and relationship.

## Motivation Type

**Primary:** Practical and institutional. The reader has to make adoption, design, or policy decisions.

**Secondary:** Intellectual. The reader wants a principled answer to the AI-in-learning debate.

**Tertiary:** Ethical. The reader is worried that AI may make learning look better while making learners less capable.

# PART 3 — BOOK TYPE AND DEPLOYMENT SPECIFICATION

## Book Type

**Primary type:** Practitioner handbook with evidence-framework bones.

This is not a statistics textbook, an AI tools catalog, or a Hattie commentary. It is a decision manual. Readers should be able to read the opening chapter, then consult the group chapters when evaluating a tool, intervention, or classroom routine.

## Deployment Specification

**Primary adoption context:**
Professional learning programs for K-12 districts, higher education teaching centers, instructional design teams, AI adoption committees, and learning engineering groups.

**Secondary adoption context:**
Graduate courses in learning design, educational technology, AI in education, teacher leadership, and learning analytics.

**How the book can be used:**

- As a cover-to-cover short course in AI adoption judgment
- As a chapter-by-chapter companion to an AI pilot process
- As a reference for product review meetings
- As a faculty development text for teaching-with-AI workshops
- As a source of case studies for graduate education courses

**What the book is not designed for:**

- A comprehensive defense or critique of Hattie's methodology
- A technical manual for building AI systems
- A product recommendation guide
- A universal policy template
- A replacement for local teacher judgment

# PART 4 — FIELD POSITIONING

## Positioning Statement

The gap this book fills is the space between evidence-informed teaching and AI adoption. Hattie helps educators ask, "What tends to matter for achievement?" AI literacy frameworks help educators ask, "What can this technology do?" This book connects the two: "Given what makes this influence work, should AI support it, stay adjacent to it, or be kept away from the active mechanism?"

## Positioning by Neighboring Field

**vs. Visible Learning summaries:**
Visible Learning summaries explain effect sizes and interventions. This book adds the AI adoption layer: feasibility, irreducibility, substitution risk, mechanism conflict, and phase gates.

**vs. AI tool guides for teachers:**
Tool guides show prompts and workflows. This book asks whether the workflow preserves the learning-producing mechanism.

**vs. AI literacy frameworks:**
AI literacy frameworks teach concepts such as bias, hallucination, privacy, and prompt design. This book adds educational mechanism judgment: when a tool should not do the thing it is technically able to do.

**vs. edtech procurement rubrics:**
Procurement rubrics evaluate privacy, cost, accessibility, and implementation. This book evaluates instructional legitimacy: what work belongs to AI, teacher, learner, peer group, or institution.

**vs. anti-AI arguments:**
The book does not reject AI. It distinguishes augmentation from substitution and gives readers precise language for the boundary.

# PART 5 — THREE-ACT LEARNING ARC

## Act One — Establish the Lens

**Chapters:** 1–5

The reader learns the core framework: effect sizes, the hinge point, group signatures, safe augmentation, and early metacognitive gates. By the end of Act One, the reader can explain why a tool's capability is not the same thing as educational legitimacy.

**Act One milestone:** Analyze one AI tool using the phase-gate worksheet.

## Act Two — Learn the Major Patterns

**Chapters:** 6–22

The reader moves through the recurring categories: AI-adjacent context signals, human-irreducible relationships, instructional clarity, task analysis, AI-native practice, self-regulated learning, cooperation, diagnosis, memory, motivation, problem solving, testing, and cohesion. By the end of Act Two, the reader can classify an influence and anticipate the likely adoption risks.

**Act Two milestone:** Compare three tools that claim to support the same influence and decide which one preserves the mechanism best.

## Act Three — Handle High-Stakes Single Cases

**Chapters:** 23–39

The reader studies single-influence chapters where the boundary is sharp: collective teacher efficacy, teacher judgment, classroom discussion, summarization, feedback, note taking, concept mapping, parent involvement, ICT, and technology in writing. By the end of Act Three, the reader can make and defend a use / do-not-use decision in front of a school team.

**Act Three milestone:** Produce an AI adoption memo with mechanism, evidence, phase gate, risks, and implementation conditions.

# PART 8 — CHAPTER ANATOMY TEMPLATE

Every group chapter should use this repeatable structure:

1. **Opening classroom problem:** A realistic decision moment, not an abstract definition.
2. **Group translation:** Plain-language explanation of the group signature.
3. **The Hattie examples:** Highest-ranked influences and why they are grouped together.
4. **Mechanism analysis:** What makes the influence work.
5. **AI support roles:** What AI can safely do.
6. **Substitution trap:** What AI must not do.
7. **Case study:** A human-readable scenario with a teacher, learner, department, district, or product team.
8. **Phase gate:** The explicit division of labor.
9. **Evidence check:** What would count as proof that learning improved.
10. **Reader exercise:** Apply the group logic to one local tool or practice.

# PART 9 — CASE STUDY STRATEGY

Case studies should feel local and operational. They should not be generic stories about technology adoption. Each case should include:

- A named decision maker, such as a teacher, instructional coach, department chair, principal, parent liaison, or AI committee member
- A concrete tool or AI workflow under consideration
- A specific Hattie influence from the group
- A moment where the AI tool is useful
- A moment where the AI tool becomes dangerous because it crosses the phase gate
- A final adoption decision with conditions

Case studies should vary across contexts: elementary literacy, secondary mathematics, college writing, teacher professional learning, intervention teams, parent communication, project-based learning, tutoring, and district procurement.

# PART 11 — HARD TOPICS, CONTESTED CLAIMS, AGING RISK

## Hard Topics

- Hattie methodology critiques and the limits of meta-meta-analysis
- The difference between intervention, condition, trait, context, and harm in the influence list
- AI tools that improve artifacts but weaken learning
- High-effect human mechanisms that institutions may wish were automatable
- Vendor claims around feedback, tutoring, writing, and personalization
- Equity risks when AI reads context but cannot repair structural causes

## Contested Claims

1. The d = 0.40 hinge is useful but not sacred.
2. AI-native does not mean educationally safe.
3. Some AI-resistant influences can still use AI around the edges.
4. Productive struggle is not inefficiency; sometimes it is the mechanism.
5. Human relationship is not a soft add-on; in several groups it is the intervention.

## Aging Risk

The highest aging risk is vendor activity. Tool capabilities and product categories will change faster than the Hattie influence list. To manage this, chapters should focus on mechanisms and phase gates rather than naming specific products. Product examples can be framed as replaceable illustrations.

## Chapter 1 — Hattie's Visible Learning and the AI Question

**Purpose:** Give readers the conceptual tools they need before the group chapters begin.

**Core argument:** Hattie's Visible Learning is useful here because it shifts the AI conversation away from tool excitement and toward educational mechanisms. The question is not "Can AI do this?" The question is "What makes this influence work, and would AI strengthen or replace that mechanism?"

**Must cover:**

- What an effect size is, why d = 0.40 is treated as a practical hinge, and why it is not a magic cutoff.
- Why the list contains interventions, conditions, practices, background factors, and harmful influences, not a simple menu of things to buy.
- The four AI feasibility categories: AI-NATIVE, AI-AUGMENTED, AI-ADJACENT, AI-RESISTANT.
- Human irreducibility: relationship, judgment, social presence, embodiment, metacognition, and accountability.
- Substitution risk: when AI makes the artifact look better while reducing the learning event.
- Mechanism conflict: when the way an AI tool works conflicts with the reason an influence works.
- The phase gate: specify what AI can do, what humans must do, and what evidence proves learning happened.

**Opening case:** A school board asks whether to buy an AI tutoring platform because "feedback has a high effect size." The chapter shows why this is the wrong level of analysis: feedback matters only when it changes teacher action, learner understanding, and next-step practice.

**Chapter deliverable:** Readers complete a one-page AI adoption audit: influence, effect size, mechanism, AI role, human role, learner role, substitution risk, evidence of learning.
