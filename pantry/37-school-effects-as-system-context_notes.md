## A. Conceptual foundations

### Context is signal, not treatment
The examples (85. Generalized school effects (d=0.48)) are mostly conditions, predictors, or system context. AI can read, summarize, and visualize these signals, but it does not thereby repair the condition. Prior achievement, socioeconomic status, school effects, ICT infrastructure, working memory, and age-within-cohort are not buttons an AI tool can press.

The chapter should teach the reader to separate prediction from intervention. A model can identify risk; only a human system can allocate support, redesign instruction, or change conditions.

**Common misconception:** Readers may think a better prediction model is the same as a better intervention.

**Worked example:** AI flags that students with weak prior knowledge are struggling in algebra. The intervention is not the flag; it is the teacher's regrouping, prerequisite lesson, practice design, and follow-up evidence.

**Source(s):** OECD, AI and the Future of Skills, Volume 2 (2023): https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/ai-and-the-future-of-skills-volume-2_a9fe53cb-en.html; U.S. Department of Education, Artificial Intelligence and Future of Teaching and Learning (2023): https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf

### Avoiding deficit labels
Context signals become harmful when they harden into destiny. Prior ability, socioeconomic status, working memory, or school-level patterns should guide support, not lower expectations. This is especially important with AI because predictive systems can scale labels quickly and make them feel objective.

The author should include equity language: use context to remove barriers and target support, not to sort learners into thinner opportunities.

**Common misconception:** Data feels neutral, so readers may overlook how labels change teacher expectations or learner identity.

**Worked example:** A dashboard shows low prior achievement; the phase gate requires a teacher to inspect work samples and choose a reversible support plan rather than assigning a permanent track.

**Source(s):** U.S. Department of Education, Artificial Intelligence and Future of Teaching and Learning (2023): https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf; UNESCO, Guidance for generative AI in education and research (2023): https://www.unesco.org/en/articles/guidance-generative-ai-education-and-research

### Phase gate for analytics
AI may aggregate records, summarize patterns, and propose hypotheses. Humans must decide meaning, intervention, communication, and ethical use. Evidence of learning must be outcome improvement after support, not dashboard accuracy alone.

This chapter type should connect strongly to later teacher-judgment and school-effect chapters.

**Common misconception:** A high AUC model can still produce no learning if the institution does nothing useful with the signal.

**Worked example:** AI identifies attendance-pattern risk; the school team contacts family, adjusts support, and tracks whether engagement and learning recover.

**Source(s):** OECD, AI and the Future of Skills, Volume 2 (2023): https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/ai-and-the-future-of-skills-volume-2_a9fe53cb-en.html; Generative AI and agency in education scoping review (2024): https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00631

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Effect sizes are useful prioritization signals, not local guarantees.
- AI adoption should be judged by the learning mechanism it preserves or disrupts.
- Delayed, unaided performance is stronger evidence of learning than in-tool fluency or artifact polish.
- Predictive context signals do not by themselves improve learning.

**Contested or emerging:**
- Hattie-style rankings are methodologically debated and should not be treated as exact prescriptions.
- Generative AI evidence in education is fast-moving; many claims are still vendor, observational, or early-stage rather than causal.
- Learning analytics can support equity or amplify tracking harms depending on governance and action.
