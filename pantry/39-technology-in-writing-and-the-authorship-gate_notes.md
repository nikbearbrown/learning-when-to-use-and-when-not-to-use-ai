## A. Conceptual foundations

### AI-native does not mean safe
The examples (114. Technology in writing (d=0.42)) are technically easy for AI to support: practice items, tutoring moves, vocabulary variants, spelling checks, writing suggestions, summaries, or adaptive drills. That technical fit creates the adoption temptation. The central chapter warning is that fluency, speed, and personalization can mask substitution of learner effort.

AI-native work is safest when the learner still retrieves, attempts, explains, revises, or transfers. It becomes dangerous when the model supplies the answer, summary, draft, or reasoning path before the learner has generated one.

**Common misconception:** Readers may equate adaptive or personalized with educationally legitimate. Personalization is only useful if it preserves the learning operation.

**Worked example:** AI generates ten vocabulary practice contexts; the learner must choose, recall, explain, and use the word in a new sentence without seeing the answer first.

**Source(s):** Roediger & Karpicke, Test-enhanced learning / retrieval practice (2006): https://www.psychologicalscience.org/journals/psychological-science/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x/; U.S. Department of Education, Artificial Intelligence and Future of Teaching and Learning (2023): https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf

### Retrieval, generation, and authorship
Practice testing, repeated reading, memorization, writing, and summarization work because learners perform effortful operations. Retrieval strengthens access; generation forces organization; revision reveals gaps; authorship requires choosing meaning. AI can multiply opportunities for these operations, but it can also erase them.

The author should frame AI as a practice generator and feedback assistant, not as the performer of the target cognition. Delayed, unassisted evidence is the key measurement rule.

**Common misconception:** Students mistake answer recognition for retrieval and polished model text for their own understanding.

**Worked example:** Before AI gives a hint, the learner must submit an attempt and a confidence rating. After the hint, the learner explains what changed.

**Source(s):** Roediger & Karpicke, Test-enhanced learning / retrieval practice (2006): https://www.psychologicalscience.org/journals/psychological-science/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x/; Hattie & Timperley, The Power of Feedback (2007): https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.3102/003465430298487

### Guardrails for AI-native tools
Guardrails include attempt-first gates, hint ladders, delayed answer reveal, self-explanation prompts, fading support, and teacher-visible audit trails. These should be chapter-specific: writing needs authorship gates; testing needs retrieval gates; tutoring needs answer-withholding; repeated reading needs human fluency performance.

A strong notes-to-chapter conversion should include a use / do-not-use boundary in imperative language.

**Common misconception:** The mistake is to rely on a product's general safety claims instead of specifying the learning operation it must preserve.

**Worked example:** AI may generate decodable texts for phonics practice; the teacher still listens for sound-symbol mapping and the learner still reads aloud.

**Source(s):** AI-based intelligent tutoring systems review (2025 preprint): https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.18882; Generative AI and agency in education scoping review (2024): https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00631

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case 1: Attempt-first AI practice
For 114. Technology in writing (d=0.42), an AI tool generates practice variations but withholds answers until the learner attempts retrieval or composition. The tool is valuable because it multiplies opportunities, not because it completes them.

### Case 2: Teacher-visible guardrail audit
The teacher reviews where learners requested answers, skipped hints, or overused suggestions. The data becomes a self-regulation conversation rather than a hidden shortcut.

### Failure case: Beautiful artifacts, weak transfer
Learners submit AI-polished summaries, notes, or writing. Immediate quality rises, but delayed unaided explanation or transfer falls because the learner did not perform the target operation.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Effect sizes are useful prioritization signals, not local guarantees.
- AI adoption should be judged by the learning mechanism it preserves or disrupts.
- Delayed, unaided performance is stronger evidence of learning than in-tool fluency or artifact polish.
- Retrieval and generation are learning operations that should not be bypassed.

**Contested or emerging:**
- Hattie-style rankings are methodologically debated and should not be treated as exact prescriptions.
- Generative AI evidence in education is fast-moving; many claims are still vendor, observational, or early-stage rather than causal.
- How much guardrailing is enough for learner-facing AI tutors remains an active research and product-design question.
