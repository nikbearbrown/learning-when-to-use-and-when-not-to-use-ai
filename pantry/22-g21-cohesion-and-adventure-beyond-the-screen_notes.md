## A. Conceptual foundations

### Human irreducibility
This chapter belongs to the human-irreducible side of the map. The examples (101. Strong classroom cohesion (d=0.44), 105. Outdoor/adventure programs (d=0.43)) work through relationship, trust, peer interdependence, credibility, embodied presence, family connection, or collective agency. AI may support logistics and sensemaking, but it cannot be treated as the intervention itself.

The author should make the distinction between supporting the conditions for a human mechanism and replacing that mechanism. AI can schedule peer groups, summarize observation notes, translate family communications, or surface participation patterns. It cannot be the trusted adult, the peer audience, the family relationship, or the collective professional will.

**Common misconception:** Readers often think AI-resistant means no AI anywhere. The better distinction is no AI in the active ingredient.

**Worked example:** AI helps a teacher identify isolated students before a service-learning project, but the relationship work happens in the project, conversation, feedback, and shared accountability.

**Source(s):** Visible Learning MetaX teacher-student relationships details: https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/influences/view/teacher-student_relationships; U.S. Department of Education, Artificial Intelligence and Future of Teaching and Learning (2023): https://www.ed.gov/sites/ed/files/documents/ai-report/ai-report.pdf

### Social mechanisms need real consequences
Peer tutoring, jigsaw, cooperation, classroom discussion, and parental involvement depend on one person's action mattering to another person. The instructional force comes from interdependence: explaining to a peer, being accountable to a group, trusting the teacher, or being known by a family-school partnership.

AI simulations can be useful rehearsal spaces, but they should not be mistaken for the relational event. A simulated discussion partner can help a learner prepare; it cannot create the same social risk, repair, belonging, or accountability as a real classroom discussion.

**Common misconception:** A chatbot role-play feels like discussion, so readers may assume it substitutes for discussion. It usually rehearses language, not the social mechanism.

**Worked example:** Students prepare claims with AI before a Socratic seminar. The seminar remains human because classmates respond, challenge, misunderstand, and revise each other in real time.

**Source(s):** Effects of the Jigsaw method on student educational outcomes (2023): https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1216437/full; Collective Teacher Efficacy according to Visible Learning: https://visible-learning.org/2018/03/collective-teacher-efficacy-hattie/

### Phase gate for relationship-rich work
The gate should put AI before and after the human event: preparation, grouping, accessibility support, documentation, translation, reflection prompts, and evidence synthesis. During the event, the human relationship or peer exchange must remain load-bearing.

Evidence should include changes in participation, trust, peer explanation quality, attendance, persistence, student voice, or teacher-team action, not just completion of AI-generated artifacts.

**Common misconception:** Teams may overvalue dashboards because they are easier to observe than trust. The chapter should force evidence to include human behavior.

**Worked example:** AI creates heterogeneous jigsaw groups and prints role cards; students still teach their expert segment to peers; evidence comes from peer explanations and individual transfer checks.

**Source(s):** Effects of the Jigsaw method on student educational outcomes (2023): https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1216437/full; Visible Learning MetaX teacher-student relationships details: https://www.visiblelearningmetax.com/influences/view/teacher-student_relationships

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case 1: AI around the relationship, not inside it
A teacher team considers AI support for 101. Strong classroom cohesion (d=0.44). The tool can compile observation notes, help form groups, translate family messages, and prepare prompts. The adoption condition is that the human event remains human: teacher-student conference, peer exchange, family conversation, or group decision. Source frame: relationship and collective-efficacy evidence in the source bank.

### Case 2: Simulated practice before real interaction
Students use AI to rehearse questions or language before a live discussion or family meeting. The simulation lowers entry cost but is not counted as the intervention; the real evidence is participation and learning in the human setting.

### Failure case: Replacing social accountability with chatbot fluency
A school replaces peer tutoring or discussion with AI conversation logs. Students produce more text but lose the peer obligation to explain, listen, repair misunderstanding, and be accountable to a real audience.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Effect sizes are useful prioritization signals, not local guarantees.
- AI adoption should be judged by the learning mechanism it preserves or disrupts.
- Delayed, unaided performance is stronger evidence of learning than in-tool fluency or artifact polish.
- Relationship-rich influences require real human accountability and social presence.

**Contested or emerging:**
- Hattie-style rankings are methodologically debated and should not be treated as exact prescriptions.
- Generative AI evidence in education is fast-moving; many claims are still vendor, observational, or early-stage rather than causal.
- The boundary between useful simulation and harmful substitution is context-dependent.
