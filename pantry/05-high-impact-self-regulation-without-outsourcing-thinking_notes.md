## A. Conceptual foundations

### Metacognition is the learner's work
The examples (2. Self-reported grades (d=1.33), 10. Strategy to integrate with prior knowledge (d=0.93), 11. Self-efficacy (d=0.92)) involve planning, monitoring, feedback use, summarization, self-questioning, note taking, reflection, or goal setting. These practices work because learners inspect and regulate their own thinking. AI can make the work visible, but it should not do the regulating for them.

The author should emphasize that metacognition is not a worksheet label; it is a control loop: set a goal, attempt, monitor, adjust, and evaluate.

**Common misconception:** Readers may ask AI to plan, summarize, and evaluate everything, leaving the learner with only acceptance.

**Worked example:** AI asks a learner to compare their summary with a source and identify what they omitted; the learner writes the revision rationale.

**Source(s):** Education Endowment Foundation, Metacognition and self-regulation evidence review: https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/evidence-reviews/metacognition-and-self-regulation; Judgment of Learning: A Human Ability Beyond Generative AI (2024): https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.13392

### Feedback must change action
Feedback is powerful only when it answers the learner's next question and changes what they do next. Hattie and Timperley describe feedback around task, process, self-regulation, and self levels; the chapter should warn that praise, scores, or fluent comments are weak if they do not guide revision.

AI feedback can scale immediacy, but it can also overwhelm, overcorrect, or remove teacher judgment. The phase gate should require learner uptake evidence.

**Common misconception:** The common error is to count comments delivered rather than revisions made.

**Worked example:** AI marks three unclear claims in a draft; the learner chooses one, revises it, and explains why the revision improves the argument.

**Source(s):** Hattie & Timperley, The Power of Feedback (2007): https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.3102/003465430298487

### False fluency and convenience
Summaries, notes, highlights, and generated goals often feel like learning because they reduce friction. But convenience can replace selection, organization, retrieval, and self-monitoring. The chapter should teach readers to ask whether the learner can reproduce, explain, or transfer the idea without the tool.

This is the heart of many do-not-use boundaries: do not let AI produce the cognitive artifact before the learner has worked on it.

**Common misconception:** Students confuse having a clean artifact with having a usable memory or skill.

**Worked example:** A student receives AI lecture notes. The evidence check is a closed-note retrieval quiz and a self-explanation, not the presence of notes.

**Source(s):** Roediger & Karpicke, Test-enhanced learning / retrieval practice (2006): https://www.psychologicalscience.org/journals/psychological-science/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x/; Education Endowment Foundation, Metacognition and self-regulation evidence review: https://educationendowmentfoundation.org.uk/education-evidence/evidence-reviews/metacognition-and-self-regulation

## B. Domain examples and cases

### Case 1: AI as preparation accelerator
A teacher uses AI to prepare examples, diagnostic prompts, or formative checks for 2. Self-reported grades (d=1.33). The teacher chooses, edits, and sequences; students still attempt and explain.

### Case 2: Professional learning loop
A department uses AI to summarize student work patterns before a meeting. The human team interprets causes, chooses an intervention, and returns with evidence.

### Failure case: Outsourcing the hard part
The tool generates plans, feedback, or summaries so fluently that teachers or learners stop doing the diagnostic, reflective, or retrieval work that made the influence effective.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Effect sizes are useful prioritization signals, not local guarantees.
- AI adoption should be judged by the learning mechanism it preserves or disrupts.
- Delayed, unaided performance is stronger evidence of learning than in-tool fluency or artifact polish.
- Metacognitive and self-regulatory strategies require explicit teaching, practice, and transfer.

**Contested or emerging:**
- Hattie-style rankings are methodologically debated and should not be treated as exact prescriptions.
- Generative AI evidence in education is fast-moving; many claims are still vendor, observational, or early-stage rather than causal.
- Automated feedback quality varies widely; uptake and revision matter more than comment volume.
